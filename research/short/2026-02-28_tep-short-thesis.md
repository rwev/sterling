# Short Thesis: Teleperformance SE (TEP.PA / TLPFY)

## Bear Case Summary

Teleperformance is the world's largest contact center and business process outsourcing company, with EUR 10.2 billion in 2025 revenue and approximately 500,000 employees across 100 countries. The company's core business model -- deploying large numbers of human agents to handle customer interactions -- is facing secular disruption from AI-powered customer service automation. Revenue growth has decelerated from high-single-digits to 0-2% guidance for 2026, organic growth targets have been cut (from 2-4% to 0-2%), the founder CEO has been replaced by a McKinsey transformation executive, and short interest on the Paris listing has risen from 3.8% to 6.4% of shares on loan in a single month. At EUR 53.88 (approximately $30 on the US ADR), the stock is down 50% from its 52-week high but remains expensive relative to a business whose organic growth is approaching zero and whose largest input cost -- labor -- is being systematically displaced by the technology it claims to be adopting. Net debt of EUR 5.7 billion (2.3x EBITDA) adds leverage to a deteriorating earnings trajectory. Management's medium-term target of 4-6% organic growth by 2028 requires a successful AI transformation that, if achieved, would by definition eliminate the need for hundreds of thousands of the company's own employees -- a paradox the market has not fully priced.

## What the Market Is Missing

1. **The growth deceleration is structural, not cyclical.** Teleperformance cut its 2025 organic growth target from 2-4% to a realized 1.3%, and guided 2026 at 0-2% with Q1 expected below the yearly range. This is not a temporary macro headwind. Enterprise clients are deploying AI chatbots, voice agents, and automated resolution systems that directly replace the human agents Teleperformance provides. Block's 40% workforce reduction, cited in our macro outlook as an AI displacement signal, is representative of a broader trend. Every major enterprise is evaluating or deploying AI customer service tools. Teleperformance's addressable market is shrinking, not growing.

2. **The CEO departure signals board-level recognition of structural decline.** Daniel Julien, the founder and CEO who built Teleperformance over four decades, is being replaced effective March 15, 2026 by Jorge Amar, a McKinsey digital transformation partner. When a founder exits and is replaced by a restructuring consultant, the board is telling the market something: the current business model is broken and requires fundamental reinvention. The market is treating this as a positive "transformation catalyst." We treat it as confirmation that the legacy business is in secular decline.

3. **The AI transformation paradox.** Teleperformance's stated strategy is to become an AI-powered customer experience company. The company has launched over 400 AI projects and targets EUR 100 million in annual run-rate savings from AI-driven efficiency. But consider the implication: if AI can generate EUR 100 million in savings by replacing human labor within Teleperformance's own operations, what is preventing Teleperformance's clients from deploying the same AI to bypass Teleperformance entirely? The company's AI strategy is essentially a proof of concept for its own obsolescence. The more successfully Teleperformance demonstrates that AI can replace human agents, the more its clients will conclude they do not need Teleperformance.

4. **Leverage amplifies the downside.** Net debt of EUR 5.7 billion against EUR 1.49 billion in adjusted EBITA (2025) yields a leverage ratio of approximately 2.3x. This is manageable in a stable business but creates meaningful equity risk in a declining one. If EBITA declines 15-20% -- plausible if organic revenue turns negative and restructuring costs persist -- leverage approaches 3x and the BBB credit rating comes under pressure. A downgrade would increase borrowing costs and further compress free cash flow. The EUR 70-90 million in restructuring costs guided for 2026 are just the beginning; the cost of transforming a 500,000-person labor force is measured in billions, not millions.

5. **Short interest is rising for a reason.** Bloomberg reported on February 24 that speculators are ramping up bets against Teleperformance, with shares on loan rising to 6.4% from 3.8% in late January. Short sellers are typically early but directionally correct in identifying secular declines. The rising short interest reflects a growing consensus among sophisticated investors that AI disruption of the contact center industry is not a future risk -- it is a present reality.

## Accounting and Fundamental Concerns

### Revenue Quality -- FY2025 Results (February 26, 2026)

Full-year 2025 revenue of EUR 10.2 billion declined 0.7% on a reported basis. Management highlights 1.3% "like-for-like" growth excluding hyperinflation effects, but this adjustment obscures the underlying reality: in constant currencies and on a reported basis, the business is flat to declining. The 2026 guidance of 0-2% like-for-like growth, with Q1 expected below the range, suggests the trajectory is still deteriorating. At the midpoint, this is a 1% growth business trading at a market capitalization of approximately EUR 9 billion (roughly 0.9x revenue). For context, the stock traded at 2-3x revenue when growth was in the high-single-digits.

### EBITA Margin Compression -- Structural vs. Cyclical

Adjusted EBITA margin was 14.6% in 2025, down from approximately 15.1% the prior year. Management guides to 14.6% for 2026, acknowledging EUR 70-90 million in restructuring charges. The medium-term target is 15.5% by 2028, which requires AI-driven efficiency gains to more than offset revenue deceleration. This is speculative. If revenue growth turns negative -- plausible as AI adoption accelerates -- margin targets become unachievable regardless of cost savings, because Teleperformance's cost structure includes significant fixed overhead (real estate, technology platforms, management layers) that cannot be eliminated proportionally.

### Net Income Trajectory

Net income was EUR 497 million in 2025, down 5% YoY. On a per-share basis, the decline is steeper due to share buybacks not keeping pace with earnings deterioration. If organic revenue growth turns negative in 2027 and restructuring costs persist, net income could fall below EUR 400 million, pushing the stock to 20x+ trailing earnings at current prices -- expensive for a declining business.

### Free Cash Flow and Capital Returns

Teleperformance returned 42% of free cash flow through dividends and buybacks in 2025. If free cash flow compresses due to restructuring costs and declining EBITA, the company faces a choice: maintain shareholder returns (unsustainable) or cut them (negative signal). The EUR 3 billion cumulative net free cash flow target for 2026-2028 (EUR 1 billion/year) depends on stable-to-growing EBITA, which we believe is at risk.

## Valuation

### Bull Case (What Needs to Go Right for Longs)

AI transformation succeeds: Teleperformance becomes an AI-enabled CX platform, margins expand to 15.5%+, revenue re-accelerates to 4-6% by 2028, and the stock re-rates as a technology-services company. In this scenario, EBITA reaches EUR 1.7-1.8 billion and the stock trades to EUR 80-90: approximately 50-70% upside.

### Bear Case (Our Thesis)

AI displacement accelerates, organic revenue turns negative by 2027, restructuring costs escalate, EBITA declines to EUR 1.2-1.3 billion, leverage approaches 3x, and the BBB credit rating is downgraded. The new CEO conducts a strategic review that reveals the core business is in terminal decline. In this scenario, the stock trades to EUR 30-35 (0.5-0.6x revenue): approximately 35-45% downside.

### Base Case

AI disruption is real but gradual. Revenue growth hovers at 0-1% for the next 2-3 years. Margins stabilize at 14-14.5% after restructuring. The stock trades at EUR 45-50: modest downside but not a compelling standalone short. The bear case is more likely than the base case because AI adoption curves are exponential, not linear.

## Differentiation from TASK Short

TaskUs (TASK) is already in the short book as a BPO/AI displacement thesis. Teleperformance differs in three material ways: (1) it is 8-9x larger by revenue (EUR 10.2B vs. $1.2B), making it a bellwether for the entire contact center industry -- if TEP declines, the sector follows; (2) it carries meaningful leverage (2.3x net debt/EBITDA vs. TASK's post-recap leverage), creating downside convexity; and (3) the CEO departure is a unique catalyst that TASK does not share. The two positions are complementary, not redundant: TASK is a micro-cap special situation driven by leveraged recap and client concentration; TEP is a large-cap secular decline driven by AI displacement and CEO transition.

## Key Risk to the Short

The primary risk is that Teleperformance's AI transformation succeeds and the company pivots from a labor-arbitrage model to a technology-enabled platform with higher margins and renewed growth. If the new CEO delivers tangible evidence of this pivot -- measured in AI-driven revenue growth, margin expansion, and client retention -- the stock could rally sharply from depressed levels. The 50% decline from the 52-week high means the bar for a positive surprise is low. The stop loss at EUR 65.00 limits this risk. Additionally, Teleperformance's geographic diversification (100 countries) provides a buffer against AI displacement, which may proceed unevenly across regions and client segments.

## Conviction Level

High. The contact center industry is one of the most clearly identifiable victims of AI automation. Teleperformance's size, leverage, decelerating growth, and CEO departure create a confluence of negative factors that the market has partially but not fully discounted. The 50% decline from highs reflects recognition of the AI threat; the remaining downside reflects the gap between the market's assumption of "gradual disruption" and our expectation of "accelerating disruption." Each quarter that organic growth fails to inflect upward will validate the thesis.
