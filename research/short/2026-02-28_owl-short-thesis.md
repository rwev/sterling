# Short Thesis: Blue Owl Capital Inc. (OWL)

## Bear Case Summary

Blue Owl Capital is the most concentrated private credit manager in the software lending vertical at precisely the moment AI is disrupting the revenue models of its borrower companies. The reflexive loop -- AI displaces software labor, software company revenues compress, private credit loans sour, lenders restrict capital, further starving the sector -- is not theoretical. It is unfolding in real time. Blue Owl halted quarterly redemptions from its retail-focused fund (OBDC II) in mid-February, sold $1.4 billion in loans to institutional buyers, and has watched its stock fall for 11 consecutive sessions to a 2.5-year low. At approximately $10.55, the stock trades at roughly 11x forward earnings and appears optically cheap. We believe the market has not yet priced the second- and third-order effects: impaired fundraising capacity, reputational contagion to the broader Blue Owl platform, and the probability that software default rates -- currently around 4% in private credit -- could reach 15% according to Marathon Asset Management's chairman. If fundraising decelerates, Blue Owl's fee-related earnings -- the primary valuation driver -- will inflect downward, and the stock will re-rate sharply lower.

## What the Market Is Missing

1. **The software concentration is an existential risk, not a portfolio tilt.** Blue Owl has over 70% of its direct lending book concentrated in software and SaaS companies. This is not diversifiable without years of portfolio rotation. The typical private credit loan has a 5-7 year maturity. Blue Owl cannot quickly de-risk this exposure even if it wanted to. Every quarter that AI adoption accelerates is a quarter in which Blue Owl's borrower credit quality deteriorates. Goldman Sachs BDC (GSBD), by contrast, has already reduced its software ARR exposure from 39% to 11%. Blue Owl has not disclosed a comparable reduction.

2. **Fundraising is the fee engine, and it is at risk.** Blue Owl raised a record $56 billion in 2025, generating $2.87 billion in revenue and a 58.3% FRE margin. But the fundraising franchise depends on institutional and retail investor confidence. The OBDC II redemption halt sent a signal to the retail wealth channel -- the fastest-growing distribution avenue for alternative asset managers -- that private credit liquidity is fragile. Bloomberg reported investors yanked more than 15% of net assets from one of Blue Owl's tech-focused funds. Saba Capital and other activist funds are circling the BDC complex. When retail advisors lose confidence in a platform, fundraising does not decline linearly; it collapses.

3. **The "permanent capital" narrative masks fragility.** Blue Owl's bull case rests on $187.7 billion in fee-paying AUM, with 86% of management fee growth coming from permanent capital vehicles. Permanent capital is supposed to be sticky -- no redemptions, no mark-to-market pressure. But the OBDC II redemption halt demonstrates that "permanent capital" is only permanent until it isn't. If software defaults spike and NAVs are marked down, even permanent capital vehicles face pressure: investors stop allocating new capital, existing commitments are questioned, and the flywheel reverses.

4. **Management's 30% LTV claim provides false comfort.** Blue Owl management has stated its software loans are at 30% loan-to-value, implying a 70% equity cushion. But loan-to-value in private credit is calculated on enterprise value, which is itself a function of revenue multiples. Software company revenue multiples have compressed 20-40% in the past 12 months as AI disruption accelerates. A loan that was 30% LTV at a 10x revenue multiple becomes 50% LTV at a 6x multiple -- and at a 4x multiple, it is approaching par. The equity cushion is evaporating in real time.

5. **Marathon's 15% default warning is not priced.** Marathon Asset Management's chairman Bruce Richards warned that software companies in private credit are approximately 10x levered and that default rates could reach 15%. Blue Owl's current non-accrual rate across its BDC vehicles is under 2%. If defaults reach even half of Marathon's estimate -- say 7-8% -- the impact on Blue Owl's earnings, NAV, and fundraising capacity would be severe. The stock at $10.55 is pricing a normalized environment with modest stress, not a sector-wide software credit event.

## Accounting and Fundamental Concerns

### Fee Revenue Sustainability -- Q4 2025 Earnings

Blue Owl reported Q4 2025 revenue of $755.6 million, full-year revenue of $2.87 billion, and FRE of $0.96/share. The company guided to a 58.5% FRE margin in 2026 and declared an annual dividend of $0.92/share. These numbers assume continued fundraising momentum and stable management fees. But management fees are a function of fee-paying AUM, which is a function of fundraising and capital deployment. If fundraising slows -- as it will if retail advisors pull back from private credit -- FRE margins can be maintained only by cutting headcount, which further impairs the franchise.

### AUM Not Yet Paying Fees -- $28.4 Billion

Blue Owl disclosed $28.4 billion in AUM not yet paying fees, representing over $325 million in expected annual management fees. This is presented as a growth backlog. But if underlying credit quality deteriorates, deployment of this capital may be delayed or reduced. Uninvested capital does not generate fees. If Blue Owl slows deployment due to credit concerns, this $325 million in expected fees becomes a mirage.

### Retail Fund Structure Creates Liquidity Mismatch

Blue Owl's retail-facing funds (OBDC, OBDC II) hold illiquid private credit assets but offer periodic liquidity to investors. This is the classic maturity mismatch that has caused financial stress throughout history. When retail investors request redemptions that exceed the liquidity of the underlying assets, the manager must either gate redemptions (damaging reputation), sell assets at a discount (impairing NAV), or both. Blue Owl has now done both. The structural vulnerability remains.

## Valuation

### Bull Case (What Needs to Go Right for Longs)

Fundraising resumes at record pace, software defaults remain below 3%, OBDC II redemption halt is treated as idiosyncratic, and Blue Owl's diversification into digital infrastructure and GP-led secondaries offsets any credit headwinds. In this scenario, FRE grows 15-20% and the stock re-rates to 14-16x forward earnings: $13.50-$15.50.

### Bear Case (Our Thesis)

Software defaults accelerate to 7-10%, fundraising decelerates 30-40% from 2025 levels, retail wealth channel pulls back from private credit broadly, FRE growth stalls or turns negative, and the dividend ($0.92/share) is at risk. In this scenario, the stock trades to 7-9x impaired FRE of $0.70-$0.80/share: $5.00-$7.00.

### Base Case

Software stress is real but contained, fundraising slows modestly (10-15% decline), Blue Owl manages through with portfolio rotation and targeted asset sales. FRE grows low-single-digits. Stock trades to 10-11x FRE: $9.50-$10.50 -- roughly flat from here, meaning the risk/reward is asymmetrically to the downside given the tail risks.

## Key Risk to the Short

The primary risk is that Blue Owl's "permanent capital" model is genuinely resilient and the software credit stress remains contained to a small number of borrowers. If Blue Owl reports strong Q1 fundraising and minimal incremental non-accruals, the stock could squeeze to $13-14 given the consensus Buy rating and $17-18 average price targets. The stop loss at $13.00 limits this risk. Additionally, Blue Owl's diversification into digital infrastructure (data centers) and GP-led secondaries could provide earnings growth that offsets credit headwinds -- but these segments are smaller and earlier-stage than the core direct lending franchise.

High.
