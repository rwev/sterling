## Regime Assessment Update

The regime call from this morning's outlook -- **growth deceleration with sticky policy uncertainty** -- remains correct, but the inflation leg is now worse than framed. The December PCE print (released Feb 20) showed core PCE at 3.0% YoY, up from 2.8% in November. This is the Fed's preferred gauge, and it is moving in the wrong direction. Combined with the hot February PPI, we now have two data points suggesting tariff pass-through is real and accelerating. Rate cut expectations have been pushed from June to July 2026 for the first cut, with only two cuts priced for the full year.

This changes the tenor of the macro environment. We are no longer simply in "deceleration with disinflation." We are in **deceleration with re-accelerating inflation** -- a mild stagflationary setup. This is not 1970s stagflation, but it is the worst combination for equity multiples: slowing growth removes earnings support while sticky inflation removes the Fed put.

## What Changed: Three Material Developments

### 1. Nvidia's "Beat and Dump" Confirms AI Capex Fatigue Is Structural

Nvidia reported Q4 FY2026 results on February 26: adjusted EPS $1.62 vs. $1.53 est., revenue $68.13B vs. $66.21B est., data center revenue up 75% YoY to $62.3B. Guidance for Q1 FY2027 revenue was $78B, above consensus. By every traditional metric, this was an excellent quarter.

The stock fell 5.5% post-earnings, erasing roughly $260 billion in market value.

This is the clearest signal yet that the market has moved past the "AI buildout = automatic stock appreciation" phase. Investors are now demanding proof of return on the hundreds of billions in AI capex, not just proof of demand for chips. The NVDA reaction is consistent with the MSFT Azure deceleration story flagged in this morning's outlook: the market is repricing the entire AI infrastructure trade.

**Implication for positioning**: The underweight on U.S. mega-cap tech from the morning outlook is reinforced. The rotation out of AI infrastructure names and into tangible-economy sectors has further room to run. However, a subtlety is emerging: while AI chip/infrastructure demand may be peaking in terms of market enthusiasm, AI application and efficiency plays are just beginning to get bid. See the Block/AI displacement theme below.

### 2. Core PCE at 3.0% -- Inflation Is Re-Accelerating

The morning outlook cited January CPI at 2.4% headline / 2.5% core, framing disinflation as "intact." That framing needs revision. The December PCE -- released February 20 but not incorporated into this morning's analysis -- showed:

- Headline PCE: 2.9% YoY (up from 2.8%)
- Core PCE: 3.0% YoY (up from 2.8%)

Core PCE at 3.0% is 100 basis points above the Fed's 2.0% target and moving in the wrong direction. January PCE (due March 13) is expected at 2.5-2.6% core, which would be a welcome decline, but the December print combined with hot February PPI creates a credible case that tariff pass-through is beginning to bite at the consumer level.

**Implication for positioning**: This strengthens the case for real assets (gold, commodities, energy) and weakens the case for duration-sensitive equities. It also means the "eventual Fed easing" that supports growth stocks has been pushed further out. The 10-year at 3.97-4.02% may find a floor here if inflation expectations reset higher.

### 3. Block Cuts 40% of Workforce Citing AI -- A Potential Inflection Point

Block (XYZ) announced it is cutting approximately 4,000 employees -- nearly half its workforce -- explicitly citing AI-driven productivity gains. CEO Jack Dorsey stated that "a significantly smaller team using the tools can do more and do it better" and predicted most companies will reach the same conclusion within the next year.

The market's response: Block shares surged 24%.

This is not an isolated event. Tech layoffs in 2026 have already reached 49,000+ workers globally, with AI cited as a factor in a growing share. ServiceNow simultaneously launched its "Autonomous Workforce" product suite, claiming 90% of its own employee IT requests are now handled by AI with 99% faster resolution. UiPath's revenue growth accelerated to 16% last quarter on agentic AI orchestration demand.

**This is an investable theme, not just a labor market story.** The market is rewarding companies that demonstrate AI-driven margin expansion through headcount reduction. This creates two distinct opportunities:

- **Long side**: Companies providing AI workflow automation tools (the "picks and shovels" of labor displacement)
- **Short side**: Labor-intensive service companies that are slow to adopt AI and face margin compression as competitors cut costs

# Idea Brief: AI Workflow Automation -- Long NOW, PATH

To: Long Analyst
Priority: High

## The Setup (one sentence)

Block's 40% AI-driven workforce cut and 24% stock surge signals that enterprise AI labor displacement is moving from theory to execution, creating a structural demand cycle for the companies providing the automation tooling.

## Why It's Interesting

The market just told us something important: it will aggressively reward companies that use AI to cut headcount. Block's announcement is the highest-profile case, but the trend is broader -- 49,000 tech layoffs in early 2026, with AI cited as a factor in a growing share. Jack Dorsey predicts most companies will follow within a year. If even partially correct, the demand for AI workflow automation platforms is about to accelerate dramatically.

This is the "application layer" of AI -- distinct from the chip/infrastructure trade that is fatiguing. While NVDA sells shovels to build AI data centers, companies like ServiceNow and UiPath sell the software that lets enterprises actually replace workers with AI agents. ServiceNow just launched "Autonomous Workforce" and claims 90% of its own IT requests are AI-handled. UiPath's Maestro platform is seeing early traction with 16% revenue growth acceleration.

The crucial distinction: AI infrastructure stocks are being sold on "show me the ROI." AI application stocks are beginning to show the ROI -- in the form of headcount reduction and margin expansion at their customers.

## What I Know

- ServiceNow (NOW): Market leader in IT service management, now pivoting aggressively to AI-powered autonomous workflows. "Autonomous Workforce" launched Feb 26. Enterprise-grade customer base provides distribution advantage. Stock added 3% on the announcement day.
- UiPath (PATH): Leading RPA/automation platform repositioning around agentic AI orchestration via Maestro. Revenue growth accelerating to 16%. Forward P/S of 3.5x and P/E below 15x -- remarkably cheap if the agentic AI thesis plays out. Partnerships with major AI providers.
- The "buyer" side is confirmed: Block demonstrated that boards and CEOs are willing to execute massive AI-driven workforce reductions. The question is whether this is a one-off or the start of a wave.

## What Needs Verification

- ServiceNow's AI revenue contribution and growth rate -- how much of total bookings is AI-driven vs. legacy ITSM?
- UiPath's competitive positioning relative to ServiceNow and new entrants; the automation space is getting crowded.
- Whether Block's 24% stock surge is replicable across other companies making similar announcements, or whether the market was rewarding Block specifically for prior operational bloat.
- Regulatory risk: could AI employment impact become a political liability that slows enterprise adoption?
- Customer retention risk: do aggressive workforce cuts impair service quality and revenue?

## Suggested Direction

Long. ServiceNow as the higher-conviction, larger-cap play with enterprise distribution and a new product cycle. UiPath as the higher-beta, cheaper option with more upside if agentic AI orchestration takes off. Consider both, sized for the stage of the theme (early).

## Catalyst / Time Sensitivity

ServiceNow and UiPath both report Q1 2026 earnings in April-May. Booking acceleration and AI-specific revenue disclosures will be the key data points. Near-term, watch for other major companies announcing AI-driven workforce reductions -- each announcement validates the theme and creates demand for automation tooling. This is early-stage but the Block catalyst makes it timely now.

# Idea Brief: Labor-Intensive Service Companies Vulnerable to AI Margin Compression -- Short Screen

To: Short Analyst
Priority: Normal

## The Setup (one sentence)

If Block's prediction is correct that most companies will execute AI-driven workforce cuts within a year, labor-intensive service businesses that are slow to adopt will face margin compression as competitors cut costs, creating a structural short opportunity.

## Why It's Interesting

The Block announcement creates a competitive dynamic: companies that aggressively adopt AI and cut headcount will see margin expansion, while their competitors who maintain legacy staffing models will face relative margin compression. This is most acute in industries where:

1. Labor is the dominant cost (50%+ of revenue)
2. The work is largely routine/cognitive (not physical)
3. Competitors are demonstrably adopting AI faster
4. The company has limited pricing power to offset cost disadvantage

Traditional business process outsourcing (BPO), staffing agencies, call center operators, and back-office service providers are most directly exposed. If AI can handle 90% of IT requests (per ServiceNow's own data), the value proposition of outsourced service providers built on human labor is fundamentally challenged.

## What I Know

- 77% of CEOs expect AI to lead to layoffs within five years; 44% anticipate reductions of 10% or more.
- The BPO and staffing industries are built on labor arbitrage -- their margins depend on the spread between what they charge clients and what they pay workers. AI eliminates the need for the worker entirely.
- Companies in this space include names like Teleperformance, Concentrix, TaskUs, Genpact, Robert Half, and Kforce.
- An Oxford Economics report noted some "AI washing" in layoff announcements -- not all cuts are genuine AI displacement. Need to distinguish real disruption from narrative.

## What Needs Verification

- Which specific BPO/staffing companies are most exposed based on revenue concentration in automatable functions?
- Are any of these companies successfully pivoting to AI-augmented service delivery, which would invalidate the short?
- Timeline: is this a 6-month thesis or a 2-year thesis? The speed of enterprise AI adoption will determine the pace of disruption.
- Valuation: some of these names may already trade at distressed multiples, limiting downside.

## Suggested Direction

Short screen. The Short Analyst should evaluate the BPO, staffing, and outsourced services space for companies with high labor cost ratios, limited AI adoption, and competitors that are moving faster. This is a thematic screen, not a single-name call.

## Catalyst / Time Sensitivity

Q1 2026 earnings season (April-May) will be revealing -- watch for margin divergence between AI-adopters and laggards within the same industry. The Block announcement may accelerate enterprise decision-making on AI workforce cuts, compressing the timeline for disruption. Not urgent, but the theme is crystallizing now.
