## What Changed: Three Material Developments

### 1. Private Credit Stress Is a New Risk Vector

Blue Owl Capital halted quarterly withdrawals from its retail-focused fund (Blue Owl Capital Corp II) and sold $1.4 billion in loans to institutional buyers at 99.7 cents on the dollar. The stock has fallen for 11 consecutive sessions to a 2.5-year low. Keefe Bruyette cut the price target to $12.50.

Why this matters beyond Blue Owl:

- The private credit market has grown to roughly $2 trillion globally and is concentrated in software and technology lending.
- Jamie Dimon has drawn parallels to pre-2008 dynamics. Mohamed El-Erian called Blue Owl a "canary in the coal mine."
- Software company defaults are rising as AI disrupts the revenue models of the very companies that private credit lenders backed during the low-rate era.
- If private credit stress spreads, it could create forced selling across software, SaaS, and mid-market tech -- sectors that are already under pressure from the AI displacement theme flagged in outlook-2.

This is not yet systemic. Brookfield's CEO has pushed back, calling it "not that big of a deal." But the reflexive loop -- AI disruption causes software defaults, which causes private credit stress, which restricts capital to software -- deserves dedicated monitoring.

**Implication for positioning**: This reinforces the underweight on U.S. tech, particularly mid-cap SaaS and software names with leveraged balance sheets funded by private credit. It also creates a potential short opportunity in BDC stocks and private credit managers with heavy software exposure. See Idea Brief #1 below.

### 2. Dell's AI Earnings Complicate the "Capex Fatigue" Narrative

Dell reported Q4 FY2026 results on February 26: revenue $33.4B (+39% YoY), EPS $3.89 (+45%), and -- critically -- a $43 billion AI server backlog, up from $18.4B entering the quarter. AI orders in Q4 alone were $34.1B. Full-year AI orders totaled $64.1B. The stock surged 22% to $148.08. FY2027 revenue guidance of $138-142B crushed consensus of $124.9B.

This matters because it introduces an important nuance to the "AI capex fatigue" thesis from our prior outlooks:

- **NVIDIA's "beat and dump" (-5.5%)** shows the market is fatigued with AI chip valuations.
- **Dell's surge (+22%)** shows the market is enthusiastic about AI infrastructure hardware at the right price and with visible demand.
- **Microsoft's -20% YTD** shows the market is punishing AI capex when ROI is unclear.

The distinction: investors are not fatigued with AI spending itself. They are repricing *where in the AI stack* the value accrues. The market is rotating from AI chips and cloud hyperscalers toward AI hardware/infrastructure plays with tangible backlogs and lower valuations. Dell trades at roughly 14x forward earnings versus NVDA at 25x+.

**Implication for positioning**: The morning outlook's underweight on "U.S. Mega-Cap Tech" needs refinement. The underweight applies specifically to AI cloud/chip names where valuation exceeds near-term earnings visibility (NVDA, MSFT). AI hardware infrastructure (DELL, potentially others in the server/networking stack) is a distinct subsector where the market is willing to pay for growth. The Long Analyst should evaluate DELL and adjacent names. See Idea Brief #2 below.

### 3. End-of-Month Capital Flow Signals

Two data points frame how corporates and institutions are positioning:

**Record February buyback authorizations: $233.3 billion.** This is the largest February on record. Companies are choosing capital return (buybacks, dividends) over headcount investment. This aligns perfectly with the Block/AI displacement theme from outlook-2: boards are cutting workers, reinvesting savings into buybacks, and the market is rewarding both actions. This is a self-reinforcing cycle that benefits shareholders at the expense of labor.

**International divergence is widening.** Canada's Q4 GDP contracted 0.6% annualized (well below flat consensus), reinforcing the case for further Bank of Canada easing and CAD weakness. Germany's CPI fell below 2%, and money markets now price a 70% chance of a 25bp ECB cut in June (up from 50% pre-data). The easing cycle outside the U.S. is accelerating while the Fed remains on hold due to tariff-driven inflation. This divergence supports continued dollar weakness and international equity outperformance.

# Idea Brief: Private Credit / BDC Software Exposure -- Short Screen

To: Short Analyst
Priority: High

## The Setup (one sentence)

Blue Owl Capital's redemption halt and 11-day share price decline signal that private credit's heavy concentration in software lending is becoming a liability as AI disrupts the revenue models of borrower companies.

## Why It's Interesting

The private credit market grew to $2 trillion during the low-rate era, with a disproportionate share of lending directed at software and SaaS companies -- businesses valued for recurring revenue and high margins. Blue Owl, the most concentrated player (70%+ of loans in software), has now halted redemptions from a retail fund, sold $1.4B in loans, and seen its stock fall for 11 straight sessions.

The reflexive loop is what makes this compelling: AI is disrupting enterprise software (Block just cut 40% of its workforce because AI can do the work), which threatens the revenue of private credit borrowers, which threatens the loan books of private credit managers. Jamie Dimon has compared the situation to pre-crisis dynamics. El-Erian calls it a "canary in the coal mine."

If software defaults accelerate -- and the AI displacement trend suggests they will -- BDCs with heavy software exposure face NAV markdowns, dividend cuts, and potential liquidity stress. The first cut has already been flagged: Keefe Bruyette cut Blue Owl's price target to $12.50.

## What I Know

- Blue Owl (OWL / OBDC): 70%+ software concentration. Redemption halt on BOCC II. Stock at 2.5-year lows. 11 consecutive down days.
- The private credit market broadly is ~$2T globally, with software/tech as a major lending vertical.
- Software company defaults are rising as AI disrupts business models.
- Bloomberg reported today (Feb 28) that "private credit stocks signal more pain is coming."

## What Needs Verification

- Which other BDCs have significant software/SaaS concentration? Screen ARCC, FSK, GSBD, BXSL, TPVG for sector exposure.
- What are the actual default rates in private credit software lending? Distinguish between "stressed" and "defaulted."
- Is the Blue Owl situation idiosyncratic (retail fund structure, liquidity mismatch) or systemic (credit quality deterioration across the sector)?
- Are any BDCs trading below NAV by enough to already price in the stress? If so, the short may be late.
- Regulatory response: could the SEC or Fed step in to contain private credit stress?

## Suggested Direction

Short screen. Evaluate BDCs and private credit managers with concentrated software/tech lending books. Blue Owl is the most visible but may already be priced for stress. The better shorts may be managers where the market has not yet recognized the software concentration risk.

## Catalyst / Time Sensitivity

BDC earnings season (March-April) will force NAV marks and loan loss provision disclosures. Any additional redemption halts or dividend cuts will accelerate the repricing. This is time-sensitive: the market is just beginning to focus on this risk.

# Idea Brief: AI Hardware Infrastructure -- Long DELL

To: Long Analyst
Priority: Normal

## The Setup (one sentence)

Dell's 22% post-earnings surge on a $43 billion AI server backlog and $138-142B FY2027 revenue guidance demonstrates that the market will aggressively pay for tangible AI demand at reasonable valuations, even as it punishes AI chip and cloud plays.

## Why It's Interesting

The "AI capex fatigue" narrative is real for chips (NVDA -5.5% on a beat) and cloud (MSFT -20% YTD). But Dell just proved that AI hardware infrastructure is a distinct subsector where:

1. Demand is massive and accelerating ($34.1B in AI orders in Q4 alone; $64.1B for the full year)
2. Backlog provides multi-quarter revenue visibility ($43B)
3. Valuation is reasonable (~14x forward earnings vs. NVDA at 25x+)
4. The customer base is diversified across hyperscalers, enterprises, and sovereigns

Dell is positioned as the "arms dealer" of the AI buildout -- supplying the physical servers that house NVDA's GPUs. As the market rotates from "who makes the chips" to "who deploys the infrastructure," Dell sits at the value-capture point.

The 342% AI server revenue growth and the raised FY2027 guidance ($138-142B vs. $124.9B consensus) suggest the street is still underestimating the hardware demand cycle.

## What I Know

- Dell (DELL): $148.08 as of Feb 28. Q4 revenue $33.4B (+39%). AI server backlog $43B. FY2027 guidance $138-142B, well above consensus.
- The stock has surged 22% post-earnings, so the entry point is less favorable than a week ago.
- Dell's ISG (Infrastructure Solutions Group) is the primary beneficiary; Client Solutions is less interesting.
- Competitors in AI server infrastructure include Super Micro (SMCI), HPE, and Lenovo.

## What Needs Verification

- Margin profile on AI servers: are these high-margin or commodity-margin builds? The bull case requires margin expansion, not just revenue growth.
- Customer concentration: how dependent is the $43B backlog on a few hyperscalers vs. diversified enterprise demand?
- Supply chain risk: Dell depends on NVDA for GPUs. Any allocation constraints could delay backlog conversion.
- Competitive dynamics: Super Micro has been the market's preferred AI server play. How does Dell's offering and margin structure compare?
- Post-22% gap-up: is there a better entry point, or does the guidance revision support the current price?

## Suggested Direction

Long consideration. The fundamental setup is strong, but the 22% gap-up makes timing tricky. The Long Analyst should model the margin trajectory and assess whether the FY2027 guidance supports the current valuation. If margins expand alongside revenue, there is further upside. If this is a revenue-only story with flat margins, the stock may consolidate.

## Catalyst / Time Sensitivity

Dell's Q1 FY2027 report (late May) will be the next major catalyst -- specifically AI server margins and backlog conversion rates. In the interim, any hyperscaler capex announcements (April-May earnings) that confirm continued infrastructure spending support the thesis. Not urgent given the gap-up, but the structural demand story is durable.
