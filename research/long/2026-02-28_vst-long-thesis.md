# Long Thesis: Vistra Corp (VST)

## Investment Summary

Vistra is the largest competitive power generator in the United States, operating a diversified fleet of nuclear, natural gas, coal, and solar assets totaling approximately 41 GW of capacity. The company sits at the intersection of two structural tailwinds: AI-driven electricity demand growth and U.S. grid modernization. FY2025 delivered record adjusted EBITDA of $5.9B and adjusted FCF of $3.6B, both above the high end of original guidance. Management has guided FY2026 adjusted EBITDA to $6.8-7.6B, representing 22%+ growth. At $171 per share, the stock trades at approximately 14x forward earnings on 2026 estimates -- a significant discount to its growth profile and contracted revenue visibility. We recommend initiating a long position on pullback weakness, targeting $230-240.

## Business Overview

Vistra operates through two primary segments: (1) Vistra Corp, comprising competitive retail electricity and wholesale generation across ERCOT (Texas), PJM (Mid-Atlantic/Midwest), and other ISO markets, and (2) Vistra Vision, which houses the company's zero-carbon nuclear and renewable assets. The nuclear fleet, expanded through the 2024 Energy Harbor acquisition, totals approximately 6.4 GW across plants in Texas, Pennsylvania, and Ohio.

Key assets:
- **Nuclear fleet**: ~6.4 GW across Comanche Peak (TX), Beaver Valley (PA), Davis-Besse (OH), and Perry (OH)
- **Natural gas fleet**: ~28 GW providing dispatchable capacity and peaking revenue
- **Retail business**: TXU Energy and other brands serving ~5 million customers, providing a natural hedge against wholesale price volatility
- **Solar and storage**: Growing portfolio providing optionality on renewables expansion

The vertically integrated model (generation + retail) provides earnings stability that pure wholesale generators lack. Nuclear provides baseload, low-marginal-cost power with zero carbon emissions, while the gas fleet captures scarcity pricing during demand spikes.

## Investment Case

### 1. Contracted Nuclear Revenue Provides Multi-Decade Visibility

Vistra has executed long-term power purchase agreements (PPAs) with major hyperscalers that fundamentally change the company's earnings profile:
- **Amazon Web Services**: 20-year agreement for 1,200 MW at Comanche Peak
- **Meta**: 20-year agreements covering 2,176 MW of operating capacity plus 433 MW of planned upgrades at PJM nuclear plants

These contracts total approximately 3.8 GW of nuclear capacity under long-term agreements, providing revenue visibility extending to the mid-2040s. This transforms Vistra from a merchant power company subject to commodity price cycles into a contracted infrastructure provider with utility-like revenue stability but higher growth.

### 2. Structural Electricity Demand Growth

U.S. electricity consumption reached an all-time peak of approximately 4,200 TWh in 2025. Management projects continued load growth of 3-5% annually in ERCOT and low-single digits in PJM through 2030, driven by:
- Data center buildout (AI training and inference workloads)
- Electrification of transportation and industrial processes
- Reshoring of manufacturing capacity

This is not speculative demand -- it is showing up in interconnection queues and signed contracts today.

### 3. FCF Generation and Capital Return

FY2025 adjusted FCF of $3.6B represents a 10.2% FCF yield on the current enterprise value. Management targets adjusted FCF before growth per share of $12.60 in 2026, representing 30% growth from FY2025's $10.39. The company has been an aggressive buyer of its own stock and targets net debt to adjusted EBITDA of approximately 2.3x by year-end 2027.

### 4. Valuation Disconnect

At $171 per share, Vistra trades at:
- ~14x forward P/E on FY2026 consensus estimates
- ~12.6x trailing EV/EBITDA
- ~8.5x EV/EBITDA on 2026 guidance midpoint ($7.2B)

For a company guiding to 22%+ EBITDA growth with contracted, multi-decade revenue visibility, this is cheap. The market appears to still price Vistra as a volatile merchant power company rather than a contracted clean energy infrastructure provider. The consensus price target of $234 implies 36% upside.

## Earnings Quality

Vistra's adjusted EBITDA metric excludes mark-to-market gains/losses on hedging positions and one-time items, which is standard for merchant power companies. The key quality signal is FCF conversion: FY2025 adjusted FCF of $3.6B on $5.9B EBITDA implies a 61% conversion rate, which is strong given the capital-intensive nature of the business. The nuclear fleet requires periodic refueling outages (capex lumpy), but maintenance capex is well-understood and predictable.

GAAP net income of $944M in FY2025 is substantially lower than adjusted earnings due to non-cash mark-to-market hedging adjustments and depreciation. This is structural for merchant generators and does not indicate earnings manipulation -- the cash is real.

Working capital can swing with commodity price movements affecting collateral postings, but the retail book provides a natural offset to wholesale exposures.

## Risk Factors

1. **Regulatory risk**: Changes to PJM capacity market rules, nuclear production tax credit (PTC) modifications, or adverse regulatory treatment of hyperscaler PPAs could impact contracted revenue.
2. **Nuclear operational risk**: Unplanned outages at key plants (particularly Comanche Peak) would reduce generation and revenue. Nuclear incidents anywhere globally could trigger political backlash.
3. **Natural gas price volatility**: A sustained collapse in power prices due to low gas prices would reduce merchant revenue from the gas fleet, partially offset by retail margin stability.
4. **Leverage**: Net debt of ~$17B is manageable at current EBITDA levels (2.9x) but leaves limited margin for error if EBITDA disappoints.
5. **AI demand disappointment**: If hyperscaler capex plans are revised down, electricity demand growth projections could prove optimistic. However, the 20-year PPAs are signed and binding.
6. **Interest rate sensitivity**: Higher-for-longer rates increase the cost of refinancing the debt stack and reduce the present value of long-duration contracted cash flows.
