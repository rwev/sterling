# Macro Outlook -- AI Reshoring, Infrastructure Buildout, and the Power Supply Deficit

**Context**: This document complements the existing AI Infrastructure Supercycle outlook (2026-03-01) and AI Cloud Hyperscaling outlook (2026-03-02) by drilling into three specific structural themes that are investable on a multi-year horizon: (1) semiconductor reshoring and the CHIPS Act production ramp, (2) grid modernization and the engineering/construction super-cycle, and (3) the widening gap between AI data center power demand and available generation capacity. The March 4 tariff escalation is a near-term catalyst that interacts directly with these themes.

---

## Regime Assessment

**The US is attempting to simultaneously reshore its semiconductor supply chain, electrify its AI ambitions, and modernize a grid built for a different era. The capital required is staggering -- north of $2 trillion across these three vectors over the next five years -- and the bottlenecks are real, investable, and largely underappreciated by the market.**

The macro backdrop remains challenging (see 2026-03-01_macro-outlook-2.md for the Iran/tariff overlay): GDP growth at 1.4% annualized in Q4, core PCE at 3.0%, Fed funds at 3.50-3.75%, and the VIX at ~19 heading into the March 4 tariff deadline. But the structural investment cycle underway in reshoring, power, and grid infrastructure operates on a distinct logic -- driven by national security imperatives, hyperscaler capex commitments, and regulatory mandates that are not contingent on the business cycle.

The critical insight: **tariffs are not headwinds to the reshoring thesis -- they are the mechanism.** The January 15 executive order imposing 25% tariffs on advanced logic semiconductors, combined with Secretary Lutnick's threat of up to 100% tariffs on TSMC and Samsung if they do not expand US production, is the stick that complements the CHIPS Act carrot. This is industrial policy operating on both levers simultaneously.

## Theme 1: AI Reshoring and the CHIPS Act Production Ramp

### What Is Happening

The United States is in the early innings of the most aggressive semiconductor reshoring effort since the industry was born. The CHIPS Act allocated $52.7 billion in subsidies and tax credits, and progress is now visible:

**TSMC Arizona** -- The clear leader. Fab 1 (N4 process) is operational and producing chips. Fab 2 (N3/N2 process) begins equipment installation in Q3 2026, ahead of schedule, targeting 2027 production. Fab 3 (A16/1.6nm) has broken ground with a target by end of decade. TSMC has committed $100 billion total to its Arizona complex. This is the most consequential foreign direct investment in US manufacturing in a generation.

**Intel** -- Mixed picture. Fab 52 in Arizona has entered high-volume manufacturing on the Intel 18A (1.8nm-class) process, a genuine milestone -- the first US fab to surpass the 2nm threshold. However, the Ohio "Silicon Heartland" project has been delayed to 2030, reflecting Intel's financial challenges and the reality that building fabs is easier than filling them with competitive process technology.

**Samsung Taylor, TX** -- The laggard. The $44 billion fab is 90%+ complete structurally but was stalled by lack of customers. The Tesla AI chip contract (signed late 2025) and March 2026 EUV tool installation have revived the project. Risk production planned for 2026; volume production dependent on securing additional customers beyond Tesla. Samsung received $4.75 billion in CHIPS Act funding.

### The Tariff Accelerant

The January 15, 2026 Section 232 tariff of 25% on advanced logic semiconductors is the new policy tool. Secretary Lutnick's public threat of 100% tariffs on TSMC and Samsung unless they increase US production capacity is the clearest signal yet that the administration views reshoring as a national security priority, not just industrial policy.

**March 4 interaction**: The broader March 4 tariff escalation (25% on Canada/Mexico, 20% on China) does not directly target semiconductors but creates three relevant dynamics:

1. **Input cost inflation for fabs under construction** -- Construction materials, specialty chemicals, and some equipment sourced from tariffed countries will see cost increases. This raises the all-in cost of fab buildout but does not stop it.
2. **Accelerated front-loading of equipment imports** -- Fab builders are pulling forward purchases of equipment and materials before tariff deadlines, creating a short-term demand spike in the semiconductor equipment supply chain.
3. **Narrative reinforcement** -- Every tariff escalation strengthens the political case for reshoring and makes CHIPS Act spending easier to defend. The tariffs and the subsidies are two sides of the same policy coin.

### Investable Implications

The reshoring theme creates durable demand across several supply chain layers:

- **Semiconductor equipment**: The picks-and-shovels play. ASML, Applied Materials, Lam Research, KLA Corp, and Tokyo Electron are the direct beneficiaries of every new fab. Equipment lead times are extending.
- **Semiconductor construction and engineering**: Specialty construction firms, clean room builders, and industrial gas suppliers (Air Products, Linde) benefit from the physical buildout.
- **Domestic semiconductor production**: Intel Foundry Services and the eventual ramp of TSMC Arizona create opportunities in the domestic semiconductor ecosystem.
- **Raw materials and specialty chemicals**: Silicon wafer suppliers, photoresist makers, and specialty gas companies see sustained demand.

## Theme 2: Grid Modernization and the Infrastructure Super-Cycle

### What Is Happening

The US grid is fundamentally unprepared for the demands being placed on it. Nearly 70% of North American grid infrastructure is older than 25 years. The simultaneous pressures of AI data center load growth, electrification of transport and heating, and renewable energy integration are forcing the largest grid investment cycle in history.

**The numbers**: S&P Global projects utility capex of $228 billion in 2026 and $233 billion in 2027. The Edison Electric Institute estimates over $1.1 trillion in grid investment over the next five years. Distribution spending dominates (33% of total), followed by generation (24%) and transmission (20%).

**The bottleneck is interconnection, not generation**: The US grid interconnection queue contains approximately 2,600 GW of capacity -- a massive backlog of generation projects (solar, wind, gas, storage, nuclear) waiting to connect to the transmission system. Average wait times in constrained regions like PJM (Virginia, the data center capital) have stretched to 4-7 years. Network upgrade costs have climbed above $240/kW in constrained regions, up nearly tenfold from 2018 levels, rendering many projects economically unviable.

**FERC Order 2023** attempted to address this by mandating a "first-ready, first-served" cluster study approach, but implementation is slow. The DOE has directed FERC to accelerate interconnection for data centers specifically, signaling that this is now a federal priority.

**PJM capacity pricing has exploded**: The latest PJM capacity auction ballooned from $2.2 billion to $14.7 billion in a single year -- a 6.7x increase driven by tight supply-demand balances and slow interconnection. Consumers in the PJM footprint are paying up to $7 billion more in higher rates. This is the market screaming that power scarcity is real.

### The Engineering and Construction Backlog

The companies that physically build the grid are operating at capacity:

| Company | Backlog | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Quanta Services | $39.2B (record) | Electric transmission and distribution |
| Fluor | $28.2B | Industrial and infrastructure EPC |
| AECOM | $24.8B (record) | Infrastructure design and engineering |
| MasTec | $15.9B | Clean energy, communications, utilities |

These backlogs represent 2-3 years of revenue visibility. The constraint is not demand -- it is labor, permitting, and physical capacity to execute projects simultaneously.

### Investable Implications

- **Utility capex beneficiaries**: Quanta Services (PWR), MasTec (MTZ), AECOM (ACM), and Fluor (FLR) are the direct plays on grid modernization. Quanta is the dominant franchise.
- **Transmission equipment**: Transformers (lead times now 2-4 years), switchgear, conductors, and power electronics. Eaton, Hubbell, and nVent are in the supply chain.
- **Utility stocks with data center exposure**: Utilities in data center corridors (Dominion Energy in Virginia, AES in multiple markets) benefit from load growth but face capex headwinds.
- **Grid-edge technology**: Smart grid software, grid-scale storage, and power management systems gain urgency as the grid becomes more complex.

## Theme 3: The Power Supply Deficit -- The Binding Constraint on AI

### What Is Happening

AI data center power demand is growing faster than any source of new generation can come online. This is the single most important bottleneck constraining the AI buildout, and it is creating a multi-year structural deficit that reprices every asset in the power value chain.

**Demand**: US data centers are expected to consume approximately 260 TWh (6% of total US electricity) in 2026. Morgan Stanley projects US data center demand reaching 74 GW by 2028. Deloitte estimates AI data center power demand alone could reach 123 GW by 2035, up from 4 GW in 2024 -- a 30x increase in eleven years.

**Supply response -- Natural gas bridge**: Gas-fired generation is the only technology that can be deployed at scale within the 2-4 year timeframe needed. But gas turbine manufacturers are sold out:

- GE Vernova: Production slots filled through 2029
- Siemens Energy: Multiyear backlog through 2028
- Mitsubishi: Similar constraints

If all planned gas power projects start operating in 2026, it would exceed the previous record of 100 GW added in 2002. Behind-the-meter gas generation is becoming standard practice -- Chevron and GE Vernova announced a partnership targeting 4 GW of on-site data center power by 2027. Hyperscalers are building their own gas plants because the grid cannot serve them fast enough.

**Supply response -- Nuclear renaissance**: The nuclear story is real but operates on a longer timeline:

- **Palisades (Michigan)**: Became the first US nuclear plant to restart after decommissioning. A landmark precedent.
- **Three Mile Island Unit 1 (Crane Clean Energy Center)**: Constellation Energy, backed by a $1 billion DOE loan and a 20-year Microsoft PPA, plans to restart by 2027. Total cost ~$1.6 billion.
- **SMRs**: NuScale has NRC design certification. ENTRA1 and TVA have an agreement for up to 6 GW of NuScale SMR capacity. But realistic deployment dates are 2028-2030 for first-of-a-kind, with most clustering in the early 2030s. China's Linglong One may be the world's first commercial onshore SMR in H1 2026.
- **15 reactors globally** expected to come online in 2026, adding ~12 GW of capacity (BloombergNEF).

**Supply response -- Renewables**: Solar and wind are cost-competitive but face the same interconnection queue bottleneck. The 2,600 GW interconnection backlog is predominantly renewable projects. Even where projects are approved, transmission constraints limit deliverability.

### The Structural Gap

The math does not work on any reasonable timeline. Demand is growing at 25-30% annually. Gas turbines are sold out for years. Nuclear restarts and SMRs are 2-5 years away from meaningful contribution. Renewables are stuck in interconnection queues. Grid transmission upgrades take 5-10 years.

**This means power scarcity pricing is structural, not cyclical.** PJM's 6.7x capacity auction increase is the leading indicator. Power prices in data center corridors will remain elevated, and companies that own or control generation assets near data center load centers have durable pricing power.

### Investable Implications

- **Nuclear operators**: Constellation Energy (CEG) is the dominant play -- largest US nuclear fleet, TMI restart, Microsoft PPA. Vistra (VST) and Public Service Enterprise Group (PSEG) also own nuclear assets with data center proximity.
- **Gas turbine and power equipment**: GE Vernova (GEV) has a multi-year backlog with pricing power. Siemens Energy benefits from the same dynamics.
- **Behind-the-meter power**: Bloom Energy (fuel cells), Enchant Energy (carbon capture on gas), and distributed generation plays.
- **Natural gas producers**: Structural demand growth from power generation supports gas prices. EQT, Coterra, and Antero are Appalachian producers near PJM load centers.
- **Nuclear fuel**: Cameco (uranium), Centrus Energy (enrichment), and the broader uranium supply chain benefit from the nuclear renaissance.

## Themes for Long Analyst

1. **Constellation Energy (CEG)** -- Largest US nuclear fleet operator with the TMI restart catalyst, 20-year Microsoft PPA, and $1B DOE loan. Nuclear is the only carbon-free baseload that can serve data center demand at scale. The stock has re-rated but the earnings power from scarcity pricing is still underestimated.

2. **Quanta Services (PWR)** -- Dominant franchise in electric transmission and distribution construction. $39B record backlog with multi-year visibility. The grid modernization super-cycle is their addressable market. Labor scarcity creates barriers to entry for competitors.

3. **GE Vernova (GEV)** -- Gas turbine production sold out through 2029. Pricing power is extraordinary. The Chevron partnership for 4 GW of data center power is a template for the industry. Also positioned in grid equipment and services.

## Themes for Contrarian Analyst

1. **Intel (INTC)** -- The consensus view is that Intel is a broken company. The contrarian case: Intel 18A is now in high-volume manufacturing at Fab 52 (the first US fab below 2nm), Intel Foundry Services is the only domestic alternative to TSMC, and the CHIPS Act makes Intel a strategic national asset that the government cannot allow to fail. The Ohio delay is real, but the Arizona success changes the narrative. If IFS wins even modest external foundry business, the stock is deeply mispriced.

2. **Samsung (via supply chain)** -- The Taylor, TX fab was written off by the market, but the Tesla AI chip contract and EUV tool installation in March 2026 signal revival. Companies supplying Samsung's ramp (equipment, gases, materials) may be overlooked.

3. **Utilities in constrained corridors** -- Dominion Energy (D) and other utilities serving Northern Virginia are caught between massive data center demand and the cost of grid upgrades. The contrarian angle: regulated utilities with large rate base growth driven by data center load may be undervalued if the market is pricing in only the capex burden without the earnings growth from rate base expansion.

## Themes for Growth Analyst

1. **Applied Materials (AMAT) / Lam Research (LRCX) / KLA Corp (KLAC)** -- The semiconductor equipment trio benefits from every fab being built globally. The reshoring cycle adds a domestic demand layer on top of the existing global capacity buildout. Equipment lead times are extending, supporting pricing.

2. **Cameco (CCJ) / Uranium equities** -- The nuclear renaissance is early-stage but the supply-demand dynamics in uranium are compelling. Years of underinvestment in mining, reactor restarts, SMR pipeline, and hyperscaler PPAs create a structural deficit. Cameco is the Western world's largest producer.

3. **Vertiv (VRT) / Eaton (ETN)** -- Power management and cooling for data centers (Vertiv) and electrical equipment for grid modernization (Eaton) are in the sweet spot of both the AI buildout and grid investment themes. Both have multi-year backlogs.

## Themes for Small-Cap Analyst

1. **Centrus Energy (LEU)** -- Sole US-licensed uranium enrichment company. Beneficiary of nuclear renaissance and the push to reduce dependence on Russian enrichment services. Small-cap with national security strategic value.

2. **Bloom Energy (BE)** -- Solid oxide fuel cell technology for behind-the-meter data center power. Already deployed at multiple AI data centers. Addresses the power gap that the grid cannot fill. Revenue inflection if BTM adoption accelerates.

3. **NuScale Power (SMR)** -- Only SMR with NRC design certification. The TVA partnership for up to 6 GW is transformational if it converts. High-risk/high-reward on a 3-5 year timeline. First commercial revenue not expected until late decade, but the policy tailwinds and hyperscaler interest could drive re-rating before revenue materializes.

4. **nVent Electric (NVT)** -- Electrical connection and protection solutions for data centers and grid infrastructure. Benefits from both the data center buildout and grid modernization. Mid-cap with growing data center revenue mix.

## Risks to Current Positioning

1. **March 4 tariff escalation causes broader economic damage** -- If 25% tariffs on Canada/Mexico and 20% on China trigger retaliatory actions and consumer spending pullback, the entire equity market suffers regardless of structural themes. Infrastructure spending could be deferred if recession fears intensify.

2. **Hyperscaler capex deceleration** -- If AI revenue monetization disappoints, hyperscalers could slow capex growth. The 62% YoY growth rate is expected to decelerate to ~25% by end-2026. A sharper-than-expected deceleration would ripple through the entire power and infrastructure chain.

3. **Permitting and regulatory delays** -- Grid modernization and new generation projects face permitting timelines of 5-10 years. FERC reforms are slow to implement. Political opposition to transmission lines remains fierce at the local level.

4. **Interest rate sensitivity** -- Utilities and infrastructure stocks are rate-sensitive. If the Fed is unable to cut in 2026 due to tariff/energy-driven inflation, these sectors face valuation headwinds even as fundamentals improve.

5. **Iran crisis energy price shock** -- Sustained oil above $90+ and LNG price spikes would increase the cost of natural gas bridge generation, potentially slowing data center power deployment and raising operating costs across the infrastructure chain.

6. **CHIPS Act political risk** -- A future administration could slow or redirect CHIPS Act disbursements. Samsung's delay in Taylor, TX shows that subsidies alone do not guarantee timely execution.
