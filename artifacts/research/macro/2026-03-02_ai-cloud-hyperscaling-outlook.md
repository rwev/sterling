# Macro Outlook -- AI Cloud Infrastructure and Hyperscaling: The $690 Billion Buildout

**Context**: This document extends and deepens the 2026-03-01 AI Infrastructure Supercycle outlook with a dedicated focus on the cloud/hyperscale layer of the AI buildout. Where the prior document mapped the full AI investment stack (power, cooling, silicon, photonics), this outlook drills into the hyperscaler capex machine itself, the second-derivative supply chain beneficiaries, geographic buildout dynamics, and the risk that this cycle peaks or overextends. This is the most capital-intensive infrastructure buildout in modern economic history, and the investable surface area is wider than most market participants appreciate.

## Regime Assessment

**The hyperscaler capex cycle is not slowing -- it is compounding. But the nature of the opportunity is shifting from first-derivative plays (GPU makers) to second-derivative plays (the physical infrastructure that enables the compute).**

The updated numbers are staggering. The Big Four hyperscalers (Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft) have committed to approximately $630 billion in combined 2026 capex, a 62% increase from the record $388 billion spent in 2025. Adding Oracle brings the total to $660-690 billion. Goldman Sachs projects cumulative hyperscaler capex from 2025-2027 will reach $1.15 trillion -- more than double the $477 billion spent from 2022-2024.

Individual commitments:

| Hyperscaler | 2025 Capex | 2026 Capex Guidance | YoY Growth |
|---|---|---|---|
| Amazon (AWS) | $125B | ~$200B | +60% |
| Alphabet (Google Cloud) | $91B | $175-185B | +95% |
| Meta | $72B | $115-135B | +75% |
| Microsoft (Azure) | $90B | $110-120B | +28% |
| Oracle | ~$20B | $40-50B | +100%+ |
| **Total** | **~$398B** | **~$660-690B** | **~66%** |

Approximately 75% of this aggregate spend is directed specifically at AI infrastructure -- data centers, GPUs, networking, cooling, and power. The non-AI portion covers traditional cloud, enterprise IT, and other business lines.

The critical regime observation: **capex growth is expected to decelerate from the current 62% pace to roughly 25% by end-2026.** This is still strong growth, but the rate of change is slowing. For investors, this means the easy money in the "capex acceleration" trade is maturing. The next phase of the trade is about identifying which supply chain nodes remain bottlenecked and which companies capture durable margin as the buildout moves from planning to execution.

## The Hyperscaler Capex Machine: What They Are Actually Buying

Understanding where the $690 billion is going is essential for identifying the second-derivative beneficiaries. The hyperscaler capex budget breaks down roughly as follows:

**1. Compute Hardware (~35-40% of AI capex): ~$160-180B**
- GPUs (NVIDIA Blackwell/Rubin, AMD MI450), custom ASICs (Broadcom, Marvell)
- HBM memory (SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron)
- Server assembly and integration (Celestica, Foxconn, Quanta Computer)

**2. Data Center Construction and Land (~25-30%): ~$115-135B**
- New facility construction, land acquisition, site preparation
- Electrical infrastructure: transformers, switchgear, UPS, PDUs (Eaton, Schneider Electric, ABB)
- Mechanical infrastructure: HVAC, liquid cooling systems (Vertiv, Alfa Laval)
- Construction services (Quanta Services, Comfort Systems USA)

**3. Networking (~15-20%): ~$70-90B**
- Optical transceivers (Coherent, Lumentum, Fabrinet)
- Switches and routers (Arista Networks, Cisco, Broadcom)
- Cabling and structured connectivity (Corning, CommScope)

**4. Power Infrastructure (~10-15%): ~$45-70B**
- Grid interconnection and transmission upgrades
- On-site power generation (gas turbines, fuel cells)
- Nuclear PPAs and SMR investments (Constellation Energy, Vistra, NuScale)
- Battery storage and backup power

This breakdown reveals the investment thesis: **the compute layer (GPUs) gets the headlines, but 60-65% of hyperscaler capex flows to the physical infrastructure that surrounds the compute.** That is where the second-derivative opportunity lives.

## Second-Derivative Plays: The Supply Chain That Enables the Buildout

The prior AI Supercycle outlook (2026-03-01) covered the first-derivative plays thoroughly: NVIDIA, Broadcom, AMD, TSMC for compute; Coherent, Lumentum, Tower Semiconductor for photonics; Constellation Energy, Vistra for power; Vertiv for cooling. This section focuses on names and themes that are less covered but equally leveraged to the buildout.

### A. Electrical Power Infrastructure: The Gatekeeper Layer

**Eaton Corporation (ETN) -- $362**
Eaton is the clearest second-derivative play in the entire AI infrastructure stack. You cannot build a data center without transformers, switchgear, UPS systems, and power distribution units -- and Eaton is the dominant supplier. The numbers speak for themselves:

- Data center order growth: **+200% YoY** in Q4 2025
- 2025 revenue: $7.1B in Q4 alone, $28B+ full year
- 2026 guidance: 7-9% organic growth, $13.00-$13.50 adjusted EPS
- Strategic move: acquired Boyd Thermal for $9.5B -- Boyd provides thermal components for data centers with $1.7B in forecasted sales (70% YoY growth)
- Backlog: record levels and expanding

The Boyd acquisition is particularly significant. Eaton is positioning itself as a full-spectrum provider of both power delivery and thermal management -- the two physical constraints on AI data center density. Transformer and switchgear lead times remain extended (12-24 months), which gives Eaton pricing power and revenue visibility that few industrial companies enjoy.

**Schneider Electric (EPA: SU) -- EUR 267**
Schneider is Eaton's primary global competitor in data center electrical infrastructure. FY2025 revenue hit EUR 40.2B, with triple-digit YoY growth in pure data center segment demand. Data centers represent 20% of company sales and growing. 2026 guidance calls for 7-10% organic revenue growth and 10-15% adjusted EBITA growth. Trading at EUR 267 with analyst target of EUR 291. As a European-listed name, Schneider offers geographic diversification for the AI infrastructure theme -- it is a direct beneficiary of the European and Middle Eastern data center buildout, not just the US.

### B. Power Semiconductors: The Efficiency Layer

**onsemi (ON) -- $66**
This is a name the market is just beginning to understand in an AI context. onsemi's core competency is silicon carbide (SiC) and gallium nitride (GaN) power semiconductors -- the technologies that make power conversion more efficient at the chip level. The NVIDIA partnership for 800V DC power solutions is the key catalyst:

- AI data centers are transitioning from 400V AC to 800V DC power distribution
- onsemi's EliteSiC platform provides the power conversion devices that enable this transition
- Even a 1-2% improvement in power efficiency across a hyperscale data center saves millions of dollars annually
- Stock is up 53% YTD (from ~$43 to $66), but still well below its 2024 highs
- 2026 revenue growth estimated at 4.8%, but EPS growth of 24% reflects margin expansion from the product mix shift toward higher-value AI power components

The contrarian angle on onsemi is that the market still prices it primarily as an automotive semiconductor company (EVs). The AI data center power opportunity is a new secular growth vector that consensus estimates have not fully captured.

### C. Networking Infrastructure: Beyond Optical

**Arista Networks (ANET) -- $134**
Arista is the dominant provider of data center Ethernet switching, and it is the primary networking partner for hyperscaler AI clusters. The company raised its AI data center revenue target from $1.5B to $2.75B in 2025, reflecting the scale of demand. Trading at $134 with analyst consensus target of $174 (30% upside). Arista's competitive advantage is its EOS (Extensible Operating System) software stack, which provides the programmability and telemetry that hyperscalers need for AI workload orchestration. As AI clusters scale from single-rack to multi-building deployments, the networking layer becomes more complex and more valuable. Arista is a direct beneficiary of every dollar of hyperscaler capex that flows through networking.

**Cisco Systems (CSCO)**
Cisco launched its Silicon One G300 -- a 102.4 Tbps switch silicon -- specifically for gigawatt-scale AI clusters. Cisco claims a 28% improvement in job completion time versus previous generations. While Cisco is not the pure-play that Arista is, its installed base and enterprise relationships give it a meaningful position in the AI networking buildout, particularly for enterprise AI deployments that complement the hyperscaler wave.

### D. Server and Rack Assembly: The Volume Layer

**Celestica (CLS) -- $264**
Celestica is the contract manufacturer that assembles the servers, networking equipment, and rack-scale systems that hyperscalers deploy. It is to the AI hardware stack what Fabrinet is to the optical stack -- a volume play on total deployment regardless of which silicon vendor wins. The numbers are impressive:

- Raised 2026 revenue target to $17B (from $16B)
- Adjusted EPS guidance raised to $8.75 (from $8.20)
- Analyst consensus target: $315 (20% upside from $264)
- Strong demand visibility from its largest hyperscaler customers

Celestica is less covered than the semiconductor names but equally leveraged to the capex wave. Every GPU that ships needs a server to house it, a rack to hold it, and a system integrator to put it together.

### E. Construction and Electrical Services: The Physical Build Layer

**Quanta Services (PWR) -- $565**
Quanta is the largest electrical and infrastructure services contractor in North America. FY2025 revenue hit $28.5B (+20% YoY) with record free cash flow of $1.7B. Management projects double-digit growth across revenues and earnings for 2026, with adjusted EPS growing 20%+. Data center construction is a growing segment, but Quanta's real leverage to the AI buildout comes from the grid transmission and distribution upgrades that utilities must complete before data centers can connect. Every hyperscaler data center announcement triggers years of utility upgrade work -- and Quanta is the primary contractor for that work.

## Geographic Buildout Dynamics

The AI data center buildout is not evenly distributed. Understanding geographic patterns reveals where the next wave of infrastructure spending will concentrate and which companies are positioned to benefit.

### United States: Dominant but Constrained

- The US represents approximately 50% of global data center capacity
- 106 new data center sites announced in 2025-2026
- Traditional hubs (Northern Virginia, Dallas, Phoenix, Silicon Valley) are hitting power and land constraints
- **New markets emerging**: North Dakota, Wyoming, Missouri, Pennsylvania, Carolinas, central Washington, New Jersey, Massachusetts
- These non-traditional markets are winning deals because they have available power capacity, favorable regulatory environments, and lower land costs
- The shift to non-traditional markets benefits construction services companies (Quanta, EMCOR, Comfort Systems) and regional utilities

### Europe: Cautious but Diversifying

- Slower buildout pace than the US, with a pullback in speculative building
- Moving beyond traditional FLAP-D markets (Frankfurt, London, Amsterdam, Paris, Dublin)
- New hotspots: Zaragoza (Spain), Eastern Europe (Romania, Hungary, Czech Republic)
- Schneider Electric is the primary European electrical infrastructure beneficiary
- Regulatory environment is more restrictive (EU data sovereignty rules, energy efficiency mandates)
- The UK and France have announced mega-projects signaling a strategic catch-up effort

### Asia-Pacific: The Fastest Growth Region

- APAC data center capacity has doubled from 5 GW to 10 GW in just two years
- Japan and Australia remain core hubs, but power constraints in Tokyo are pushing development to tier-two markets
- India is the fastest-growing market, potentially surpassing Tokyo in capacity
- Emerging hotspots: Melbourne, Chennai, Bangkok, Vietnam
- APAC demand is characterized by urgency -- AI companies seeking 100 MW+ capacities within 3-6 months
- The speed-to-deployment premium in APAC favors modular and pre-fabricated data center solutions

### Middle East: Emerging AI Hub

- Multi-gigawatt campus plans from sovereign wealth funds and hyperscalers
- Strategic location between Europe and Asia for AI inference workloads
- Attracting investment from Western hyperscalers and Asian technology companies
- Saudi Arabia and UAE are the primary markets, leveraging cheap energy and sovereign capital

**Investment implication**: Companies with global footprints (Schneider Electric, Eaton, Vertiv, Celestica) capture the full geographic breadth of the buildout. US-focused infrastructure services companies (Quanta, Arista) are leveraged to the largest single market but miss the international growth.

## The Bottleneck Map: Where Supply Cannot Meet Demand

Understanding bottlenecks is the key to identifying pricing power and durable competitive advantage. The AI infrastructure stack has several binding constraints, ranked by severity:

### Tier 1 Bottlenecks (12-36 month resolution timeline)

**1. Power Grid Interconnection**
- Power availability is the single most constraining factor in data center development
- Interconnection timelines have extended from 12-18 months to 24-72 months in congested markets
- PJM Interconnection (the largest US grid operator) has $4.3B in data center grid projects approved, but execution timelines stretch years
- Goldman Sachs estimates $720B in total grid spending needed through 2030
- This bottleneck directly benefits: Quanta Services (grid construction), Eaton/Schneider (electrical equipment), utilities with available capacity

**2. Advanced Packaging (CoWoS)**
- TSMC's CoWoS (Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate) packaging is the physical constraint on GPU production
- Capacity is scaling from 75K wafers/month (end-2025) to 130K wafers/month (end-2026), but demand still exceeds supply
- NVIDIA has locked up 60%+ of 2026 CoWoS output; 85%+ is allocated to top customers
- Less than 15% remains for second-tier AI chip companies and startups
- This bottleneck reinforces TSMC's monopoly position and NVIDIA's supply chain advantage

**3. High Bandwidth Memory (HBM)**
- HBM3e and emerging HBM4 supply is the secondary silicon bottleneck
- SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron are all expanding, but supply will remain tight through 2026
- Every AI GPU requires large HBM stacks; the memory content per chip is increasing with each generation

### Tier 2 Bottlenecks (6-18 month resolution timeline)

**4. Transformers and Switchgear**
- Lead times for large power transformers remain 12-24 months
- Eaton and Schneider Electric are the primary Western suppliers; demand outstrips production capacity
- This bottleneck gives established suppliers pricing power and creates a barrier to entry

**5. Liquid Cooling Systems**
- Direct-to-chip liquid cooling is becoming standard for AI racks (120-150 kW per rack)
- Quick disconnect couplings (QDCs) -- specialized fittings for liquid cooling -- are concentrated among a handful of Western suppliers (Staubli, Eaton/CPC, Parker-Hannifin)
- Vertiv's $15B backlog reflects the depth of demand

**6. Optical Transceivers (800G to 1.6T transition)**
- Covered in detail in the 2026-03-01 AI Supercycle outlook
- 800G+ transceiver shipments projected to grow 2.6x YoY (24M to 63M units)
- Coherent, Lumentum, and Fabrinet are the primary beneficiaries

## Risks: The Bear Case for the Hyperscaler Capex Cycle

This section is deliberately thorough because the risks are real and the consensus is heavily positioned long. Sterling must size positions to survive the downside scenarios.

### 1. The Monetization Gap

This is the central risk. Hyperscalers are committing $690B in 2026 capex while enterprise AI generates approximately $100B in actual revenue. An MIT study found that 95% of generative AI pilot programs fail to achieve business value, and only 5% of enterprises report significant EBIT impact from AI investments. If 2026 passes without evidence that AI applications generate revenue commensurate with the infrastructure investment, the "build it and they will come" narrative breaks.

**How to monitor**: Watch hyperscaler cloud revenue growth rates in Q1-Q2 2026 earnings (April-July). Azure, AWS, and Google Cloud revenue must accelerate to justify the capex ramp. Any deceleration in cloud revenue growth while capex accelerates is a red flag.

**Probability assessment**: Low to moderate in 2026. The hyperscalers are competing for AI dominance and cannot afford to slow down unilaterally -- any company that cuts capex risks losing market position permanently. But by mid-2027, the monetization question becomes unavoidable.

### 2. Capex Growth Deceleration

Even without a monetization crisis, capex growth is expected to slow from 62% YoY (current) to 25% by end-2026. Markets price the rate of change, not the absolute level. Infrastructure stocks that have been trading on capex acceleration could de-rate as growth normalizes -- even if absolute spending remains at record levels.

**How to monitor**: Sequential capex guidance revisions in hyperscaler earnings calls. Any downward revision to 2027 capex is a de-rating catalyst for the entire infrastructure complex.

### 3. Regulatory Pushback and Moratoriums

This is the risk that most market participants are underweighting. More than 300 state-level data center bills have been filed across 30+ US states in just the first six weeks of 2026. At least 11 states have filed moratorium bills:

- New York: proposed halt on all data center construction for up to three years
- Oklahoma: moratorium on data centers with 100 MW+ electrical load until November 2029
- South Dakota: one-year moratorium on hyperscale data center construction
- A 230-member environmental coalition is lobbying Congress for a federal data center moratorium

FERC is finalizing rules by April 30, 2026 on how data centers connect to the transmission grid. Ratepayers in PJM states face $4.3B in electricity infrastructure costs attributed to data center connections. The political coalition against data center buildout is bipartisan -- Bernie Sanders and Ron DeSantis have both spoken out against the electricity price impact.

**How to monitor**: State-level legislative calendars, FERC rule finalization (April 30 deadline), PJM interconnection queue data. Any successful moratorium in a major market (Virginia, Texas, Ohio) would materially slow the buildout.

### 4. Power Grid Constraints

Even without regulatory intervention, the physical grid cannot keep pace. Power constraints are extending data center construction timelines by 24-72 months. US data center electricity demand is projected to grow from 183 TWh (2024) to 426 TWh by 2030 -- a 133% increase that the existing grid infrastructure cannot support without massive investment.

**How to monitor**: Utility interconnection queue data, FERC transmission planning reports, natural gas price trends (gas-fired generation remains the marginal source of data center power in most markets).

### 5. Tariff and Trade Risk

Optical transceivers, cooling components, server assemblies, and semiconductor packaging all have significant Asian supply chain exposure. The March 4, 2026 tariffs (25% on Canada/Mexico, 20% on China) could compress margins for companies unable to pass through costs. Hyperscalers are absorbing costs for now, but there is a limit.

### 6. Concentration and Crowding Risk

The AI infrastructure trade is one of the most crowded in global markets. The top 10 AI infrastructure stocks represent a significant share of S&P 500 market cap. Any negative catalyst -- a single hyperscaler cutting guidance, a DeepSeek-style efficiency breakthrough that reduces compute demand, or a broader risk-off event -- could trigger cascading selling as crowded positions unwind.

## Summary

The AI cloud infrastructure buildout is the largest capex cycle in modern economic history. The $690B in hyperscaler spending for 2026 is not speculative -- it is committed, contracted, and accelerating. But the nature of the investment opportunity is evolving. The first-derivative plays (GPU makers) are well-understood and largely priced. The second-derivative plays -- the companies that supply the power delivery, networking, cooling, assembly, and construction services that enable the compute -- are where the market is still catching up.

Eaton and Schneider Electric are the gatekeepers of the electrical infrastructure layer. Arista Networks is the standard for data center networking. Celestica is the volume assembler. Quanta Services builds the grid that powers it all. Onsemi provides the power semiconductors that make the whole system more efficient. These are not speculative bets on AI -- they are infrastructure businesses with record backlogs, multi-year demand visibility, and structural competitive advantages.

The risks are real: monetization gaps, regulatory moratoriums, grid constraints, and crowding. Position sizing must account for these. But the fundamental conclusion is clear: even if the capex growth rate decelerates from 62% to 25%, the absolute dollar volume flowing through the AI infrastructure supply chain in 2026-2027 is unprecedented, and the companies that build, power, cool, and connect the data centers will capture durable revenue regardless of which AI application ultimately wins.

# Idea Brief #11: Eaton Corporation -- The AI Gatekeeper

**To: Long Analyst**
**Priority: Urgent**

## The Setup (one sentence)

Eaton is the dominant supplier of the electrical infrastructure (transformers, switchgear, UPS, PDUs) that every data center must install before a single GPU can turn on, and its 200% YoY data center order growth with extended lead times gives it pricing power and visibility that few industrial companies can match.
