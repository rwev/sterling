# Macro Outlook — AI Infrastructure Super-Cycle Meets Geopolitical Shock

## Regime Assessment

The US economy is in a late-cycle expansion characterized by solid but decelerating growth, a manufacturing recovery, and a new geopolitical risk premium. Real GDP grew 4.4% in Q3 2025, with Q4 2025 advance estimates expected around 2.3% annualized. The labor market remains firm at 4.3% unemployment (January 2026), though payroll additions have moderated to +130K. The Fed holds at 3.50-3.75%, with markets pricing 98% probability of a hold at the March meeting and a terminal rate near 3.00% over the coming year.

The dominant macro feature is a massive private-sector capital expenditure cycle driven by AI infrastructure, now colliding with a sudden geopolitical shock from US-Israel military operations against Iran. This creates a two-track environment: structurally bullish for capex beneficiaries and energy/defense, while introducing near-term volatility and inflation risk from oil supply disruption.

**Regime classification: Late-cycle expansion with capex super-cycle overlay and geopolitical risk escalation.**

## Geopolitical Context: US-Iran Conflict

On March 1-2, 2026, US and Israeli forces launched coordinated strikes on Iranian nuclear infrastructure, missile sites, and command centers. Iran responded with drone and missile attacks on US and Israeli assets in the Gulf. The Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of global daily oil supply (~20M bbl/d) passes — has effectively been closed. Markets are pricing a short-duration conflict, but the risk distribution is heavily skewed: if hostilities persist beyond weeks, oil could move materially higher and reintroduce inflation pressure that constrains Fed easing.

**Key variable to monitor: duration of Strait of Hormuz disruption.** A prolonged closure reprices the entire macro outlook.

## Theme 1: AI Infrastructure and Cloud Buildout

### The Setup

Hyperscaler capital expenditure is approaching $690 billion in 2026, a 60%+ increase over 2025, with roughly 75% ($450B+) directed specifically at AI infrastructure — GPUs, data centers, networking, and cooling systems. This is the largest private-sector infrastructure buildout in history.

### Macro Drivers

- **Demand pull**: Every major cloud customer (enterprise and consumer) is integrating AI workloads; inference demand scaling exponentially
- **Supply constraints**: GPU lead times remain extended; TSMC raised 2026 capex guidance to $52-56B to expand capacity
- **Competitive dynamics**: Amazon ($200B), Alphabet ($175-185B), Meta ($115-135B), and Microsoft ($120B+) are in an arms race where under-investing risks permanent competitive disadvantage
- **Cash flow pressure**: Amazon facing negative FCF of $17-28B in 2026; Meta FCF down ~90% — these companies are all-in on AI and burning through cash reserves to fund it

### What Needs Monitoring

- Any sign of capex deceleration or guidance cuts (would be devastating for supply chain)
- Cloud revenue growth vs. capex growth — the payback ratio
- Enterprise AI adoption rates and willingness to pay for inference

## Theme 2: Energy Infrastructure and Power Generation

### The Setup

The AI buildout has created a structural power deficit. US data center demand is projected to reach 74GW by 2028, against a shortfall of ~49GW in available power access. Gartner estimates 40% of AI data centers will be power-constrained by 2027. Simultaneously, the Iran conflict has introduced a near-term supply shock to global energy markets, with Brent surging 8% to $78.70 on Strait of Hormuz closure fears.

### Macro Drivers

- **Structural demand**: Data centers restarting growth of a stagnant US electrical grid; hyperscalers now directly investing in power generation
- **Supply bottleneck**: Grid interconnection queues are years long; transformer and switchgear lead times extended to 3-4 years
- **Nuclear renaissance**: Microsoft-Constellation $1.6B Three Mile Island restart; Amazon-Talen nuclear partnership; NuScale only NRC-approved SMR design
- **Geopolitical premium**: Iran conflict adds 10-20% risk premium to energy; Strait of Hormuz carries 20M bbl/d
- **Policy tailwind**: BIL allocated $73B to energy and power infrastructure; reauthorization debate in 2026

### What Needs Monitoring

- Strait of Hormuz status — duration of closure is the critical variable for oil
- Grid interconnection reform / permitting acceleration legislation
- Nuclear regulatory timelines for SMR deployment
- Utility rate case outcomes for data center power pricing

## Risks to Current Positioning

1. **Iran conflict escalation / prolonged Strait of Hormuz closure**: If the conflict extends beyond weeks, oil prices could surge well above $100, reintroducing inflation, delaying Fed cuts, and pressuring consumer spending. This is the single largest near-term risk to the entire outlook.

2. **Hyperscaler capex deceleration**: Any signal that the $690B spend is being pulled back would devastate the AI supply chain — semiconductors, data center infrastructure, and power equipment names would all reprice sharply.

3. **Tariff-driven inflation resurgence**: ISM Prices Paid at 70.5 (highest since June 2022) signals that tariffs on steel, aluminum, and imported goods are already feeding through. If CPI reaccelerates, the Fed cannot cut and the late-cycle expansion compresses.

4. **Credit market repricing**: HY spreads at 2.94% vs. 4.9% 20-year average offer minimal cushion. A shock (geopolitical, credit event, or earnings miss) could trigger rapid spread widening.

5. **AI monetization gap**: Hyperscalers are spending $690B while some face negative free cash flow. If enterprise AI adoption disappoints or revenue growth fails to justify capex, the entire AI infrastructure thesis unwinds.

6. **Taiwan/China risk**: TSMC concentration remains an existential risk for the semiconductor supply chain. Any escalation in cross-strait tensions would dwarf the Iran conflict in market impact.
