## Regime Assessment

The U.S. economy is in **late-cycle deceleration with re-accelerating inflation** -- a mild stagflationary setup. This is the worst combination for equity multiples: slowing growth removes earnings support while sticky inflation removes the Fed put.

The data points that define this regime:

- Q4 2025 GDP printed 1.4% annualized, weighed down by the 43-day federal government shutdown. Consensus 2026 full-year growth sits at 1.8-2.5% (SPF: 1.8%, Deloitte: 1.9%, GS: 2.6%).
- Core PCE (December) hit 3.0% YoY, up from 2.8% -- the Fed's preferred gauge is moving in the wrong direction. January CPI was 2.4% headline / 2.5% core, but the hotter PCE and a February PPI surprise (0.5% MoM vs. 0.3% est.) suggest tariff pass-through is biting at the consumer level.
- The Fed holds the effective funds rate at 3.64%, above neutral estimates of 3.00-3.25%. Rate cut expectations have been pushed to July 2026 for the first cut, with only two cuts priced for the full year.
- The 10-year Treasury yield sits at approximately 4.02%, having briefly broken below 4.00% mid-week before settling. A floor may be forming as inflation expectations reset higher.

The dominant policy wildcard remains tariffs. The Supreme Court struck down IEEPA-based tariffs on February 20, but the administration immediately imposed a new 10% global duty under Section 122 with threats of 15%. This ongoing trade policy volatility is the single largest macro uncertainty.

Capital is rotating aggressively out of U.S. mega-cap tech and into tangible-economy sectors, international equities, and real assets. This is the defining market story of early 2026.

## Key Developments This Session

### 1. NVIDIA "Beat and Dump" Confirms AI Capex Fatigue Is Structural

NVIDIA reported Q4 FY2026: adjusted EPS $1.62 vs. $1.53 est., revenue $68.13B vs. $66.21B est., data center revenue up 75% YoY to $62.3B. Guidance for Q1 FY2027 revenue of $78B beat consensus. The stock fell 5.5%, erasing roughly $260 billion in market value. The market has moved past the "AI buildout = automatic stock appreciation" phase. Investors now demand proof of return on AI capex, not just proof of chip demand.

### 2. Dell's AI Earnings Complicate the Narrative

Dell reported Q4 FY2026: revenue $33.4B (+39%), EPS $3.89 (+45%), and a $43B AI server backlog (up from $18.4B). AI orders in Q4 alone were $34.1B. Stock surged 22%. This reveals that the market is not fatigued with AI spending itself -- it is repricing where in the AI stack value accrues. AI hardware infrastructure at reasonable valuations (Dell at ~14x forward) gets rewarded; AI chips and cloud at premium valuations get sold.

### 3. Block Cuts 40% Workforce Citing AI -- Stock Surges 24%

Block (XYZ) announced ~4,000 job cuts, explicitly citing AI-driven productivity. CEO Dorsey predicted most companies will reach the same conclusion within a year. The 24% stock surge signals the market will aggressively reward AI-driven margin expansion through headcount reduction. This creates demand for AI workflow automation tooling and threatens labor-intensive service businesses.

### 4. Private Credit Stress Emerges as New Risk Vector

Blue Owl Capital halted redemptions from its retail credit fund, sold $1.4B in loans, and saw 11 consecutive down sessions to a 2.5-year low. Over 70% of Blue Owl's loan book is concentrated in software companies -- the very sector now facing AI disruption. Jamie Dimon has drawn parallels to pre-2008 dynamics. This creates a reflexive loop: AI disrupts software revenue, private credit loans sour, capital restricted, further starving the sector.

### 5. Core PCE at 3.0% -- Inflation Re-Accelerating

December core PCE printed 3.0% YoY, up from 2.8%. Combined with hot February PPI, two data points now suggest tariff pass-through is real and accelerating. Rate cut expectations pushed from June to July. The stagflationary lean is strengthening.

# Idea Brief #1: U.S. Electrical Infrastructure Services -- PWR, EME, MTZ, FIX

**To: Long Analyst**
**Priority: High**

## The Setup

The U.S. electrical grid is entering a $1.4-2.0 trillion investment super-cycle driven by AI data center power demand, grid aging, and electrification, creating record backlogs and double-digit earnings growth at specialty infrastructure contractors.

## Why It Is Interesting

This is the physical layer of the AI buildout -- the companies that actually build and wire the grid, data centers, and power infrastructure. Unlike software or chip companies, this work requires physical presence, specialized labor, and decades-long customer relationships. Barriers to entry are meaningful. And the demand is not speculative: these companies have record backlogs providing 1.5-2.5 years of revenue visibility.

The numbers are striking:
- Quanta Services (PWR): $44.0B record backlog. 2026 guidance: $33.3-33.8B revenue (+17%), EPS $12.65-13.35 (double-digit growth). The sector leader by scale.
- EMCOR Group (EME): $13.3B backlog with data center RPO of $4.5B (+60% YoY). Record 2025: $17B revenue, $25.87 EPS, 9.4% operating margin. 2026 EPS guided $27.25-29.25.
- MasTec (MTZ): $19.0B 18-month backlog (+33% YoY). 2026 guidance: ~$17B revenue (+19%), $1.45B EBITDA (+26%). Most backlog momentum in the group.
- Comfort Systems USA (FIX): $9B+ backlog. All-time high stock price of $1,468. Data centers now 45% of revenue (from 33% a year ago). Q4 EPS $9.37 (+129% YoY). CEO calls it an "industrial supercycle."

The addressable market: $208B in annual utility grid investment (EEI), with $1.4-2.0T cumulative through 2030. AI data centers are additive to existing grid replacement needs -- 70% of the U.S. grid is approaching end-of-life.

## What Needs Verification

- Valuations are not cheap (PWR ~35-42x, EME ~27-29x, MTZ ~34x, FIX ~35-40x forward P/E). Need to assess whether multi-year earnings growth justifies these multiples or if the super-cycle is already priced.
- Margin trajectory on AI data center work specifically -- are these high-margin or commodity-margin contracts?
- Labor availability: can these companies staff up fast enough to convert backlog to revenue? Skilled electrician and lineworker shortages are a constraint.
- Material cost inflation (copper, steel, transformers) from tariffs could pressure margins if not contractually passed through.
- Customer concentration in backlog -- dependence on a few hyperscalers vs. diversified utility and enterprise demand.

## Suggested Direction

Long. PWR and EME are the highest-conviction names for a multi-year hold based on backlog visibility and competitive positioning. MTZ offers the best risk/reward for a new entry given strong backlog growth (+33%) and relatively lower valuation. FIX offers the most upside if the data center supercycle extends but carries the most valuation risk after its run.

## Catalyst / Time Sensitivity

Q1 2026 earnings (April-May) will provide updated backlog figures and margin commentary. Hyperscaler capex announcements in the same window will confirm or challenge the demand environment. ISM Manufacturing on March 4 will provide an early read on tariff impact. Not urgent -- this is a structural theme -- but the current rotation into tangible-economy assets creates a favorable entry window.

# Idea Brief #3: Salesforce -- Contrarian AI Disruption Fear Play

**To: Contrarian Analyst**
**Priority: High**

## The Setup

Salesforce (CRM) is down 33% YTD to ~$195, near its 52-week low, on pure AI disruption fear despite reporting better-than-expected Q4 results with its Agentforce AI platform gaining traction.

## Why It Is Interesting

This is the kind of setup contrarian investors live for: a high-quality franchise being sold indiscriminately on narrative fear rather than fundamental deterioration. The key facts:

- CRM closed at ~$195 on Feb 28, down 33% YTD. The 52-week low is $175.
- Q4 results beat estimates. Agentforce (its AI platform) is increasingly embedded across the business.
- The selloff deepened after Anthropic launched Claude Cowork plug-ins automating legal/compliance work, and after Block's 40% workforce cut amplified AI disruption fears.
- CNBC headline: "AI disruption didn't show up in Salesforce results. But the fears are hard to shake."
- Mizuho issued a pessimistic price target on Feb 28, adding to the pressure.

The contrarian thesis: Salesforce has 150,000+ enterprise customers, deeply integrated CRM workflows, and proprietary customer data that AI agents need access to rather than replace. If Salesforce successfully positions Agentforce as the enterprise AI platform for customer-facing operations, the current multiple compression is an opportunity. The stock is trading near the cheapest valuation in its public history relative to earnings.

The bear case is real: AI could disintermediate traditional CRM by enabling smaller, cheaper agent-based alternatives. But the installed base, switching costs, and data moat are formidable. The market may be pricing in disruption that takes years to materialize, if it materializes at all.

## What Needs Verification

- Is Agentforce generating meaningful incremental revenue, or is it a rebranding exercise?
- What is the actual churn rate? Are enterprise customers leaving, or is this purely a multiple compression story?
- Competitive positioning vs. Microsoft Dynamics 365 + Copilot, HubSpot AI, and new AI-native CRM entrants.
- At what valuation level does CRM become a takeout candidate for private equity or strategic acquirers?
- The software sector selloff is broad -- is there systematic selling pressure (ETF outflows, private credit forced sales) that creates an overshoot?

## Suggested Direction

Long contrarian. If the fundamentals hold -- enterprise retention stays high, Agentforce adoption accelerates, and revenue continues to grow -- then the current price represents a compelling entry into a durable franchise trading at a fear-driven discount. Size conservatively; the narrative could get worse before it gets better.

## Catalyst / Time Sensitivity

Q1 FY2027 earnings (late May/early June): Agentforce adoption metrics and enterprise retention data will be the key data points. Near-term, any stabilization in the software sector selloff or positive AI platform adoption data from any major SaaS company would provide a sentiment floor. The proximity to the 52-week low ($175) creates a defined risk level. Time-sensitive given the fear-driven nature of the selloff.

# Idea Brief #5: Small-Cap Domestic Infrastructure / Reshoring

**To: Small-Cap Analyst**
**Priority: Normal**

## The Setup

The "Great Rotation" into small caps is being driven by reshoring, domestic infrastructure spending, and tariff-driven import substitution, with the Russell 2000 trading at a 31% P/E discount to large caps and small-cap industrials leading performance.

## Why It Is Interesting

JPMorgan has labeled 2026 the "year of the small-cap." The structural drivers are compelling:

1. Tariffs and supply chain concerns are driving a domestic manufacturing revival. Companies reshoring production capture both demand and margin tailwinds.
2. Federal infrastructure and broadband spending (IIJA, BEAD program) disproportionately benefits small-cap contractors and service providers.
3. Energy efficiency and grid modernization create demand for smaller, specialized firms that serve as subcontractors to the large infrastructure players.
4. The Russell 2000 trades at a 31% P/E discount to the S&P 500. If the rotation from mega-cap tech continues, this discount narrows.

Names worth screening:
- Willdan Group (WLDN, ~$94): Energy efficiency consulting and services. Net revenue +26.8% YTD through Q3 2025. Earnings projected +70% in 2025. Recent $97M contract win with Alameda County. Market cap ~$1.3B.
- Proto Labs (PRLB, ~$63): Domestic digital manufacturing / 3D printing. Expanded U.S. metal 3D printing capacity. Q4 EPS beat ($0.44 vs. $0.35 est.). Revenue $136.5M. 2026 guidance: 6-8% growth. Reshoring beneficiary.
- Dycom Industries (DY, ~$430): Fiber placement and telecom infrastructure. Revenue $4.7B (+12.6% YoY). Reports March 4. Benefits from BEAD broadband program and hyperscaler connectivity. Larger cap but still a specialty small/mid-cap play.

## What Needs Verification

- Willdan's contract pipeline beyond the announced wins. Is the $49M and $97M pattern repeatable, or are these lumpy?
- Proto Labs' competitive moat in domestic 3D printing. 6-8% revenue growth is modest -- is there acceleration potential from reshoring?
- Dycom's exposure to BEAD program timing. Federal broadband spending has been slow to deploy; any further delays would push the revenue catalyst out.
- Balance sheet quality across all three names. Small caps with leveraged balance sheets are vulnerable in a rising-rate environment.
- Whether the small-cap rotation is durable or a tactical trade. If mega-cap tech stabilizes, capital could flow back.

## Suggested Direction

Long screen. The Small-Cap Analyst should evaluate these and adjacent names for quality characteristics: strong balance sheets, high ROIC, domestic revenue exposure, and positioning in infrastructure/reshoring/energy efficiency verticals. This is a thematic opportunity, not a single-name conviction call. Focus on quality within the theme.

## Catalyst / Time Sensitivity

Dycom reports March 4 -- imminent. Proto Labs and Willdan report in April-May. The BEAD program disbursements (expected to accelerate mid-2026) would provide a tailwind to fiber/broadband names. The small-cap rotation thesis strengthens if the mega-cap tech underperformance continues. Not urgent, but the valuation discount and early rotation signal make this a good time to build the watchlist.
