# Macro Outlook -- The AI Infrastructure Supercycle: Power, Pipes, and Photons

**Context**: This document supplements the existing March 1 macro outlooks (tariff escalation and Iran crisis editions) with a dedicated deep-dive into the AI investment landscape. The PM has directed full exposure to the AI upside. This outlook maps the investable layers of the AI infrastructure buildout and routes specific ideas to analyst teams.

---

## Regime Assessment

**AI capex cycle is accelerating into the largest infrastructure buildout in modern history -- and it is not a bubble.**

The macro backdrop is complicated (see 2026-03-01_macro-outlook.md and 2026-03-01_macro-outlook-2.md for tariff and geopolitical overlays), but the AI infrastructure cycle operates on a distinct investment logic that is partially decoupled from the broader economic cycle. The core argument:

1. **Hyperscaler capex commitments are binding and accelerating.** Combined 2026 capex guidance from the top four hyperscalers alone exceeds $625 billion: Amazon $200B (up from $131B in 2025), Google $175-185B (up from $91B), Meta $115-135B (up from $71B), and Microsoft's guidance implies similar magnitude. Bloomberg estimates total Big Tech AI spending at $650B in 2026. Goldman Sachs projects the broader AI company universe may invest over $500B.

2. **This is not speculative -- it is contractual.** Broadcom alone has a $73B AI backlog scheduled for recognition over 18 months. Vertiv has a $15B backlog. These are purchase orders, not aspirations.

3. **The spend is front-loaded and self-reinforcing.** An estimated 75% of aggregate hyperscaler capex in 2026 is directed at AI infrastructure specifically. Each generation of models requires more compute, more interconnect bandwidth, more power, and more cooling. The flywheel is spinning.

4. **The bottlenecks are real and investable.** Power, optical interconnects, and cooling are the three binding constraints on AI scale-out. Companies that solve these bottlenecks command pricing power and durable demand.

Against a macro backdrop of 2.0-2.5% GDP growth, sticky inflation at 2.7% core PCE, and the Fed on hold at 3.50-3.75%, the AI infrastructure theme offers secular growth that is less sensitive to the cycle. Tariff risk exists (components sourced from Asia) but hyperscalers are absorbing costs, not deferring builds.

## Layer 1: AI Power and Energy

### The Problem
Data centers consumed 183 TWh of US electricity in 2024 and are projected to reach 426 TWh by 2030 -- a 133% increase. The IEA estimates global data center electricity demand could exceed 1,000 TWh by 2026, roughly equivalent to Japan's total consumption. AI racks now draw 120-150 kW each, versus 10-15 kW for traditional workloads.

### The Investable Opportunity
Nuclear is the only scalable, baseload, carbon-free power source that can meet AI demand at scale. The Microsoft-Constellation Three Mile Island restart deal ($16B over 20 years) set the template. Google has contracted with Kairos Power for SMRs. Amazon led a $500M round for X-energy.

**Key names:**
- **Constellation Energy (CEG)** -- Largest US nuclear operator; 20%+ earnings growth in 2026; executing the Crane Clean Energy Center restart (target 2027). Premium nuclear PPAs with hyperscalers provide visible, contracted revenue. The stock has volatility from co-location regulatory debates but the fundamental demand story is ironclad.
- **Vistra Corp (VST)** -- Diversified fleet with nuclear assets; Meta partnership announced early 2026; $6.6B in buybacks since 2021 demonstrates capital return discipline alongside AI demand growth.
- **NuScale Power (SMR)** -- Only NRC-certified SMR design in the US (77 MW modules, approved May 2025). Pre-revenue and speculative, but if SMRs work, NuScale has a first-mover monopoly on the technology. Hyperscalers are actively evaluating modular nuclear for new builds.

## Layer 2: AI Infrastructure -- Cooling and Physical Plant

### The Problem
At 120-150 kW per rack, traditional air cooling is physically impossible for AI clusters. The industry is at a tipping point: liquid cooling penetration is moving from ~15% to a projected 50%+ of all new builds in 2026.

### The Investable Opportunity
- **Vertiv Holdings (VRT)** -- Pure-play data center power and cooling. Deep NVIDIA partnership (co-developed reference architectures for GB200 NVL72 systems). $15B backlog as of early 2026. Trading around $162-165. Revenue exceeded $10.2B in 2025, up from $8B in 2024. Liquid cooling revenue growing 25-40% annually. This is the picks-and-shovels play for AI physical infrastructure.

## Layer 3: AI Semiconductors -- GPUs, ASICs, Memory

### The Landscape
The AI silicon market is a three-horse race: NVIDIA dominates training GPUs (Blackwell now, Rubin H2 2026), Broadcom leads custom ASICs (70-80% market share, 5 major hyperscaler customers including Google, Meta, ByteDance, OpenAI, and Anthropic), and AMD is emerging as the credible third option (6 GW, $60B Meta deal for MI450 GPUs).

**Key names:**
- **NVIDIA (NVDA)** -- Trading around $187 with a median analyst target of $250 (33% upside). Still the gravitational center of AI compute. Rubin platform launching H2 2026 extends the product cycle. Supply constraints persist, which supports pricing power.
- **Broadcom (AVGO)** -- Trading around $343 with analyst targets averaging $421 and Bank of America at $500. AI revenue growing 150%+ YoY. $73B AI backlog. Q1 2026 revenue guided to $19.1B (+29% YoY). The ASIC business is structurally advantaged as hyperscalers diversify away from sole reliance on NVIDIA.
- **AMD (AMD)** -- The Meta partnership validates AMD as a credible alternative for hyperscale inference. The MI450 + EPYC Venice platform is a full-stack offering. AMD trades at a discount to NVIDIA on every metric; if execution continues, the re-rating opportunity is significant.
- **TSMC (TSM)** -- Monopoly on leading-edge AI chip fabrication (90%+ share at 3nm/5nm/7nm). Every AI chip from every vendor flows through TSMC. The most defensible position in the entire stack.

## Layer 4: Photonics -- The PM's Priority

### Why Photonics Matters Now

This is the layer the PM should focus on most intensely. Here is the thesis:

**Copper is hitting a physics wall.** As AI clusters scale from single-rack to multi-rack to multi-building deployments, electrical interconnects (copper) cannot deliver the bandwidth, latency, or energy efficiency required. The transition from 800G to 1.6T optical transceivers is not incremental -- it is a fundamental architecture shift from electrical to optical data movement. TrendForce projects 800G+ transceiver shipments will leap from 24 million units in 2025 to 63 million in 2026 -- a 2.6x increase in one year.

**The silicon photonics market is inflecting.** From $2.86B in 2025 to a projected $28.75B by 2034 -- a 10x expansion. The near-term catalyst is the 1.6T networking supercycle tied to NVIDIA's next-gen Rubin platform (Spectrum-X Ethernet switches with 1.6T ports, launching H2 2026).

**The supply chain is consolidating around a few key players.** Marvell's $5.5B acquisition of Celestial AI (completed February 2026) signals that the largest semiconductor companies view optical interconnects as essential, not optional. Celestial's Photonic Fabric chiplet delivers 16 Tbps -- 10x the bandwidth of state-of-the-art 1.6T ports. Revenue expected to ramp to $500M annualized by Q4 FY2028, doubling to $1B by Q4 FY2029.

### Key Photonics Names

**Coherent Corp (COHR)** -- The volume leader with ~25% market share in optical transceivers. FY2025 revenue $5.81B, up 23% YoY. The company's 6-inch Indium Phosphide wafer line in Sherman, Texas (ramped late 2025) is a manufacturing moat -- lowering per-unit laser chip costs significantly versus competitors still on 4-inch wafers. Datacenter revenue growing double digits sequentially. Fourth consecutive earnings beat (Q2 FY2026: $0.74 EPS vs. $0.61 expected). Trading around $210 with analyst consensus target of $300 (43% upside). **This is the highest-conviction name in photonics.**

**Lumentum Holdings (LITE)** -- Second-largest pure-play optical component company. Manufactures lasers, modulators, photodiodes, and full photonic engines for AI data center interconnects. Fiscal 2026 EPS consensus $5.42 (estimates revised up 10.6% in 30 days). Revenue projected at $2.57B, implying 56% growth. Trading around $52 with analyst target of $65 (25% upside). The stock surged 219% in the prior year, so some of the move is priced, but the 1.6T ramp provides continued momentum.

**Tower Semiconductor (TSEM)** -- This is the foundry play on silicon photonics. Tower does not sell transceivers -- it manufactures the photonic integrated circuits that go into them. Partnerships announced with both NVIDIA (1.6T silicon photonics for Spectrum-X) and Salience Labs (optical circuit switches for AI data centers). Record Q4 2025 revenue of $440M, full-year $1.57B. SiPho-driven infrastructure segment grew 69% in FY2024 and continues to accelerate. Trading around $125 with analyst target of $145.50. **This is a less obvious, potentially undervalued way to play the photonics buildout.**

**Fabrinet (FN)** -- Contract manufacturer for optical transceivers (manufactures for both Coherent and Lumentum). Revenue growing 28.5% this year. Trading around $237 with analyst target of $275. A leveraged play on total optical transceiver volumes regardless of which component vendor wins share.

**Marvell Technology (MRVL)** -- The Celestial AI acquisition positions Marvell as the optical interconnect platform company. The Photonic Fabric technology (16 Tbps per chiplet) is next-generation. Revenue contribution begins H2 FY2028, but the strategic positioning is being established now. Marvell also has a strong existing data center networking business.

### Private Companies to Monitor (Pre-IPO)
- **Lightmatter** -- $4.4B valuation, $850M raised. Shipping Passage M1000 photonic superchip. If this company IPOs, it will be the purest photonic computing play available.
- **Ayar Labs** -- Backed by AMD, Intel, and NVIDIA. Valued above $1B. Optical I/O chiplets for in-package photonics.

## Risks to Current Positioning

1. **AI revenue monetization gap.** Hyperscalers are spending $650B in 2026 on AI infrastructure. If AI application revenue does not begin to justify this spend within 12-18 months, capex guidance could be pulled. This is the bubble argument. We do not subscribe to it, but we must monitor it.
2. **Tariff escalation on Asian components.** Optical transceivers, semiconductors, and cooling equipment have significant Asian supply chain exposure. The March 4 tariffs (20% on China) and potential escalation could compress margins for companies that cannot pass through costs.
3. **Iran/Hormuz energy shock.** If the Strait of Hormuz remains disrupted (see 2026-03-01_macro-outlook-2.md), energy costs for data centers spike, potentially slowing buildout timelines.
4. **Concentration risk.** The AI infrastructure trade is crowded. If any single hyperscaler cuts capex guidance (even for non-AI reasons), the entire sector re-rates lower.
5. **Interest rate sensitivity.** If the Fed holds at 3.50-3.75% longer than expected (or raises), growth/high-multiple names in this basket compress. Nuclear and utility names are particularly rate-sensitive.
6. **Technology risk in photonics.** Silicon photonics is winning today, but alternative technologies (co-packaged optics architectures, new modulator materials) could shift the competitive landscape. Monitor closely.

## Summary

The AI infrastructure buildout is the most consequential capital expenditure cycle since the internet. It is not a bubble -- it is backed by $73B in Broadcom backlog alone, $15B at Vertiv, and $625B+ in hyperscaler capex commitments for this year. The investable layers are: power (nuclear), physical infrastructure (cooling), semiconductors (GPUs and ASICs), and -- most critically for Sterling's differentiation -- photonics.

Photonics is where the market is still early. The 800G-to-1.6T transition is a multi-year supercycle. Coherent, Lumentum, Tower Semiconductor, and Fabrinet are the public vehicles. Marvell's Celestial AI acquisition signals that optical interconnects are becoming standard infrastructure, not niche components. This is where Sterling should build its largest concentrated positions.
