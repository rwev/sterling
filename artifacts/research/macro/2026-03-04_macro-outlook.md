# Macro Outlook -- Hormuz Aftershocks, Services Strength, and the Warsh Wildcard

## Context

This outlook builds on the March 2-3 reports, which covered AI infrastructure, energy/power, defense, photonics, tanker shipping, GLP-1, housing, cybersecurity, building products, the SaaS selloff, and the metals super-cycle. Those themes remain intact and are not revisited here except where new data has altered the picture. This document replaces the earlier March 4 interim update (15:00 UTC) with end-of-day data and several critical developments that emerged during the session.

This report addresses: (1) the LNG supply dislocation from the Strait of Hormuz closure, now intensified by the Ras Tanura refinery attack; (2) the accelerating US manufacturing reshoring cycle reinforced by tariffs and geopolitical insecurity; (3) the emerging food/fertilizer inflation dynamic; (4) the surprisingly strong ISM Services PMI at 56.1; and (5) the formal Warsh Fed Chair nomination and its policy implications.

## Regime Assessment

The macro regime presents a striking internal contradiction. On the one hand, domestic economic activity is running hot: ISM Services PMI printed 56.1 in February (the strongest since July 2022), ISM Manufacturing remained in expansion at 52.4, and the Atlanta Fed GDPNow estimate for Q1 2026 is 3.0% annualized -- a sharp acceleration from the 1.4% Q4 2025 print. On the other hand, cost-push inflation from twin supply shocks (Hormuz oil/LNG disruption + 15% universal tariff) is building rapidly, credit spreads remain dangerously complacent, and the gold market experienced a violent 5.5% intraday correction suggesting positioning fragility.

Equities rebounded on March 4 after the March 3 selloff, with the S&P 500 closing at 6,874 (+0.84%) and the Nasdaq Composite gaining 1.63% to 22,884. But the rebound was led by megacap tech (Amazon, Tesla each +3%, Micron and AMD each +5%) rather than broad-based participation. The Russell 2000 lagged at 2,593. This is a market being carried by AI capex narrative momentum while underlying macro risks accumulate.

The Senate's 47-53 rejection of the war powers resolution on Iran removes the last domestic political constraint on the conflict's duration, making a prolonged Hormuz disruption the base case rather than a tail risk.

**Regime classification: Late-cycle expansion with unusual strength in services, building stagflationary pressure from energy/tariff cost-push, and a trapped Fed. Growth data is hotter than consensus expects (GDPNow 3.0%), but the composition is inflationary (ISM Prices Paid 70.5). The market is underpricing the duration of the Iran conflict following the war powers vote failure.**

### Critical Developments on March 4

1. **ISM Services PMI surged to 56.1**, the highest reading since July 2022 and the 20th consecutive month of expansion. Business Activity hit 59.9, and New Orders jumped to 58.6 (+5.5 points from January). All 10 reported sub-indexes were in expansion for the first time since March 2021. This corresponds to a 2.5% annualized GDP growth rate per ISM's historical relationship. The domestic economy is stronger than the stagflation narrative implies -- but services strength also means the Fed has even less room to cut rates.

2. **Kevin Warsh formally nominated for Fed Chair.** Trump officially sent the nomination to the Senate, where it will be taken up by the Banking Committee. Warsh has echoed Trump's calls for lower rates, arguing that AI-driven productivity gains enable faster growth without inflation. However, confirmation faces a hurdle: Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) has said he will oppose Warsh until a criminal investigation into Powell is resolved, and the committee has a narrow 13-11 Republican majority. A blocked or delayed Warsh confirmation extends the current Fed policy regime; a confirmed Warsh shifts the policy framework toward rate accommodation. This is a significant medium-term uncertainty.

3. **Senate rejected Iran war powers resolution 47-53.** Only Sen. Rand Paul (R-KY) crossed party lines. With six US soldiers killed so far, the war powers vote failure removes the primary domestic constraint on conflict escalation. This is the single most important signal for energy/LNG/tanker/fertilizer thesis duration: the base case for the Iran conflict is now weeks-to-months, not days.

4. **Ras Tanura refinery attack confirmed.** Iranian drones struck Saudi Aramco's 550,000 bbl/day Ras Tanura refinery, the largest in the Kingdom, causing a precautionary shutdown. This represents a meaningful loss of refining capacity and adds to the supply disruption beyond the Hormuz Strait closure itself. Brent spiked to an intraday high of $84.47 before settling at $81.21. The conflict is widening geographically beyond the Strait.

5. **Gold suffered a 5.5% intraday selloff**, dropping from above $5,300 to $5,118 as some investors rotated into the dollar as a safe haven and profit-taking accelerated. This is the first significant correction after a 100% rally over 12 months. The move does not invalidate the structural gold thesis (central bank buying, geopolitical hedging) but signals positioning fragility and potential for further volatility.

6. **Section 122 universal tariff confirmed at 15%.** The earlier estimate of "10-15%" is now resolved: the tariff was initially announced at 10% on February 20 and raised to 15% on February 22, effective February 24. The statutory 150-day limit means it expires July 24, 2026 unless Congress extends. Combined with China +20% (not +10% as previously reported -- the additional 20% surcharge on China and Hong Kong was increased from the original 10%), this is the most aggressive US tariff regime since Smoot-Hawley. Canada tariffs at 25% took effect March 4, with USMCA-compliant goods exempted March 6.

7. **Atlanta Fed GDPNow for Q1 2026 stands at 3.0% annualized** as of March 2, contradicting the narrative that GDP growth is collapsing after the 1.4% Q4 print. The next update is March 6. This combination -- 3% GDP growth, 56.1 ISM Services, 52.4 ISM Manufacturing -- describes an economy that is too strong for rate cuts, not one sliding into recession. The risk is not growth collapse but rather overheating into tariff/oil cost-push inflation.

## Theme 1: US LNG Exporters -- Structural Beneficiaries of the Hormuz Rerouting

### The Setup (one sentence)

Qatar's LNG production halt and the Strait of Hormuz closure have removed approximately 20% of global LNG supply from the market, the Senate's rejection of war powers constraints extends the probable conflict duration to weeks or months, and the widening Henry Hub ($2.92/MMBtu) vs. TTF (EUR 52.88/MWh) spread translates directly into margin expansion for US LNG exporters.

### Why It Is Interesting

Sterling has exposure to energy through the power generation and tanker themes but no direct LNG position. The LNG dislocation is structurally different from the crude oil shock in two important ways: (1) LNG contracts are increasingly indexed to spot market pricing rather than oil-linked formulas, so the price impact flows directly to exporters' realized margins; (2) the US is already the world's largest LNG exporter, and the Hormuz closure validates the long-standing thesis that European and Asian buyers need to diversify away from Middle Eastern supply.

The numbers are stark. European TTF natural gas futures surged to over EUR 60/MWh at peak before pulling back to EUR 52.88 -- still massively elevated. Henry Hub is at $2.92/MMBtu. Converting to comparable units, the TTF premium over Henry Hub is roughly 5-6x, compared to a normal 2-3x ratio. This differential flows directly into export margin expansion for US LNG terminals. The war powers vote failure makes this a durable dynamic, not a one-week blip.

### What I Know

- Qatar confirmed production halt at its two main LNG facilities on March 3 (source: CNBC)
- Strait of Hormuz handles approximately 20% of global LNG trade, nearly all from Qatar (source: EIA)
- Iran's IRGC officially confirmed the Strait is closed on March 2, threatening any vessel attempting passage (source: Al Jazeera)
- European TTF gas peaked above EUR 60/MWh, currently at EUR 52.88/MWh -- still massively elevated vs. pre-conflict
- Henry Hub at $2.92/MMBtu -- a 5-6x spread differential vs. TTF (normally 2-3x)
- US Senate rejected war powers resolution 47-53 on March 4, removing domestic constraint on conflict duration
- Cheniere Energy (LNG): shares traded $248-$275 range; ~$252 recent; largest US LNG exporter with 45 mtpa capacity
- Venture Global (VG): trading at $10.96, down 6.5% on the day; recently IPO'd; Plaquemines LNG Phase 1 producing
- US LNG export infrastructure operating near capacity, limiting short-term volume upside but sustaining pricing power
- Trump has pledged military convoy escorts and political risk insurance for Hormuz transit but provided no timeline (source: CNBC)
- Iran has said it could disrupt Hormuz with drones "for months" (source: US News & World Report)

### What Needs Verification

- Duration of Qatar production halt -- is this precautionary or damage-driven?
- Cheniere and Venture Global contract structures: what percentage of volumes are spot vs. long-term, and how do long-term contracts reprice?
- Whether US DOE LNG export permit approvals (paused under Biden, resumed under Trump) will accelerate new capacity timelines
- Potential for demand destruction in Europe if TTF sustains above EUR 50/MWh for an extended period
- Whether other LNG suppliers (Australia, Mozambique, Nigeria) can ramp to offset Qatar's absence
- Timeline for Trump's proposed military convoy escort program for Hormuz shipping

## Theme 2: Reshoring and Industrial Automation -- The Tariff Accelerator

### The Setup (one sentence)

Over $3 trillion in US reshoring-related investments have been announced since early 2025, the 15% universal Section 122 tariff (confirmed, not 10%) combined with geopolitical supply chain risk from the Iran conflict and a 500,000-worker manufacturing labor shortage is accelerating the shift toward automation-led domestic manufacturing, and the ISM data (Manufacturing 52.4, Services 56.1, all sub-indexes expanding) confirms this is not a narrative -- it is showing up in the data.

### Why It Is Interesting

This is a multi-year structural theme that has hit a sudden acceleration point. The tariff regime is now confirmed at 15% universal (not the previously uncertain 10-15% range), on firmer legal footing via Section 122 after the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA-based tariffs. The 150-day expiration (July 24, 2026) creates urgency for Congress to act on a permanent framework, and the administration has signaled intent to maintain or increase protection.

The ISM data released this week paints a picture of an industrial economy that is genuinely accelerating. Manufacturing PMI at 52.4 with New Orders at 55.8 confirms the production recovery. But the Services PMI at 56.1 is the more significant number -- it shows the broader economy is running well above trend, which means the reshoring demand driver (domestic consumption) is robust. The 500,000 unfilled manufacturing job vacancy means this reshoring wave must be automation-intensive.

ISM Prices Paid at 70.5 (highest since June 2022) is the tariff pass-through showing up in real time. Domestic producers with pricing power benefit; importers without substitution options absorb costs.

Sterling's current portfolio captures some of this through power generation exposure (VRT, CEG, VST), but has no direct exposure to industrial automation, factory equipment, or the reshoring infrastructure buildout.

## Theme 3: Food and Fertilizer Inflation -- The Underappreciated Third Leg of Cost-Push Pressure

### The Setup (one sentence)

The combination of surging energy costs (fertilizer input), elevated tariff-driven operating costs for farmers (+4% for corn, +6% for soybeans vs. 2025), and a 10-year high gap between farm costs and prices received is creating food inflation pressure that markets are not yet pricing, and the Henry Hub ($2.92/MMBtu) vs. international gas spread gives US nitrogen fertilizer producers the same margin expansion dynamic as LNG exporters.

### Why It Is Interesting

Most market commentary on inflation focuses on oil and tariffs. Food inflation is the third leg of the cost-push stool that very few investors are discussing. The ISM Services PMI at 56.1 is telling us the economy is strong enough to absorb higher food prices without demand destruction -- which means the inflationary pressure passes through rather than being offset by volume declines.

Natural gas is the primary input for nitrogen-based fertilizers. US producers like CF Industries buy domestic gas at Henry Hub ($2.92/MMBtu) and sell fertilizer into a global market where international competitors are paying LNG-linked prices (TTF at EUR 52.88/MWh). This is the same margin expansion dynamic as US LNG exporters, applied to fertilizer.

### What I Know

- Gap between farm costs and prices received hit 10-year high (source: Farm Policy News, Jan 2026)
- Operating costs projected 4% higher for corn, 6% higher for soybeans in 2026 vs. 2025
- Fertilizer prices 10-15% higher than 2025; jumped 18% in 2025 (source: World Bank)
- Global food prices climbed 1.3% in January 2026 alone
- Henry Hub at $2.92/MMBtu; TTF at EUR 52.88/MWh -- domestic gas cost advantage for US fertilizer producers
- CF Industries (CF): nitrogen fertilizer producer; benefits from low Henry Hub gas costs vs. global competitors
- Nutrien (NTR): world's largest potash producer; diversified across nitrogen, phosphate, and potash
- USDA midpoint forecast: grocery prices +1.7% in 2026 (likely understated given energy developments)
- Fertilizer tariff rollback: most fertilizers exempt from tariffs via executive order (Nov 2025)

### What Needs Verification

- CF Industries and Nutrien current stock prices and recent quarterly results

- The pass-through timing from fertilizer costs to food prices (typically 2-3 quarters lag)
- Impact of USDA spring planting intentions data (March 31 release) on fertilizer demand outlook
- Whether Ras Tanura refinery shutdown affects fertilizer-grade feedstock availability

### Suggested Direction

CF Industries is the purer play on the nitrogen fertilizer margin expansion thesis. The Henry Hub/international gas spread at current levels is extraordinarily favorable for domestic producers. Nutrien is more diversified and offers exposure to the broader agricultural input cycle.

## Theme 4: The Warsh Fed Nomination -- Policy Regime Uncertainty

### The Setup (one sentence)

Trump's formal nomination of Kevin Warsh as Fed Chair introduces a new medium-term policy variable: Warsh's dovish inclinations and belief that AI productivity gains justify lower rates could shift the Fed's reaction function in 2027, but his confirmation faces a genuine obstacle in Sen. Tillis's opposition, creating uncertainty that markets have not yet priced.

### Why It Is Interesting

This is not a trade idea -- it is a regime-level observation that affects every position in the portfolio. Warsh's policy views differ from the current Fed consensus in two important ways: (1) he believes AI-driven productivity gains allow faster growth without inflation, enabling lower rates; (2) he has echoed Trump's explicit desire for rate cuts. If confirmed, Warsh would not take office until Powell's term expires (February 2028), but markets would begin pricing in the policy shift well before that date.

The confirmation uncertainty is real: Sen. Tillis sits on the Banking Committee (13-11 Republican majority), and his stated opposition to Warsh until a criminal investigation into Powell is resolved could delay or block the nomination. This creates a binary: either Warsh is confirmed and the market begins pricing in a more dovish 2027-2028 Fed, or the confirmation stalls and the current policy framework persists.

For Sterling's positioning, a confirmed Warsh is positive for growth/duration assets and negative for short-duration/commodity positions. A stalled Warsh is neutral (continuation of current policy). Neither outcome is currently priced with high conviction.

### What Needs Verification

- Senate Banking Committee hearing timeline
- Whether Tillis's opposition is genuine or a negotiating posture
- Market pricing of Fed policy differences between Powell and Warsh (check 2027 and 2028 Fed funds futures)
- Whether Trump would withdraw Warsh and nominate an alternative if confirmation stalls
