# Macro Outlook — Hormuz Aftershocks: LNG, Reshoring Acceleration, and the Inflation-Rate Paradox

## Context

This outlook builds on the March 2-3 reports, which covered AI infrastructure, energy/power, defense, photonics, tanker shipping, GLP-1, housing, cybersecurity, building products, the SaaS selloff, and the metals super-cycle. Those themes remain intact and are not revisited here except where new data has altered the picture.

This report addresses three fresh themes driven by evolving market conditions: (1) the LNG supply dislocation from the Strait of Hormuz closure, (2) the accelerating US manufacturing reshoring cycle now reinforced by both tariffs and geopolitical insecurity, and (3) the emerging food/fertilizer inflation dynamic that complicates the Fed's path forward.

## Regime Assessment

The macro regime has sharpened meaningfully since March 3. The dominant feature is a collision between cost-push inflation (oil shock + tariffs) and a labor market showing early signs of cooling -- a stagflationary lean that constrains Fed flexibility. Markets are adapting: equities rebounded on March 4 after the March 3 selloff, but the character of leadership is rotating toward real-asset and supply-side beneficiaries.

**Regime classification: Late-cycle expansion with stagflationary risk escalation. Cost-push inflation from twin supply shocks (Hormuz + tariffs) colliding with cooling labor market. Fed trapped between mandates.**

## Theme 1: US LNG Exporters -- Structural Beneficiaries of the Hormuz Rerouting

### The Setup (one sentence)

Qatar's LNG production halt and the Strait of Hormuz closure have removed approximately 20% of global LNG supply from the market, driving European gas prices up 76% in a week and positioning US LNG exporters as the primary alternative supplier -- even though US export infrastructure is near capacity, this creates sustained pricing power and accelerates the investment case for capacity expansion.

### Why It Is Interesting

Sterling has exposure to energy through the power generation and tanker themes but no direct LNG position. The LNG dislocation is structurally different from the crude oil shock in two important ways: (1) LNG contracts are increasingly indexed to spot market pricing rather than oil-linked formulas, so the price impact flows directly to exporters' realized margins; (2) the US is already the world's largest LNG exporter, and the Hormuz closure validates the long-standing thesis that European and Asian buyers need to diversify away from Middle Eastern supply.

The numbers are stark. European TTF natural gas futures surged to over 60 euros/MWh, up 76% on the week. US natural gas prices rose only 5% by comparison -- a spread differential that translates directly into export margin expansion for US LNG terminals that buy domestic gas at Henry Hub prices and sell into international markets.

### What I Know

- Qatar confirmed production halt at its two main LNG facilities on March 3 (source: CNBC)
- Strait of Hormuz handles approximately 20% of global LNG trade, nearly all from Qatar (source: EIA)
- European TTF gas prices surged 76% on the week to over 60 euros/MWh (source: CNBC)
- US natural gas prices up only 5% -- the Henry Hub/TTF spread is widening dramatically
- Cheniere Energy (LNG): shares surged ~7% on the week; traded in a range of $248-$275 on March 3; the largest US LNG exporter with 45 mtpa capacity across Sabine Pass and Corpus Christi
- Venture Global (VG): shares surged ~24% on the week; trading around $10.75 on March 4; recently IPO'd; Plaquemines LNG Phase 1 producing first LNG; Calcasieu Pass operational
- US LNG export infrastructure is operating near capacity, limiting short-term volume upside but sustaining pricing power
- New LNG export terminal construction (Golden Pass, Plaquemines Phase 2, Rio Grande) will add capacity over 2026-2028

### What Needs Verification

- Duration of Qatar production halt -- is this precautionary or damage-driven?
- Cheniere and Venture Global contract structures: what percentage of volumes are sold on spot vs. long-term contracts, and how do long-term contracts reprice?
- Whether US DOE LNG export permit approvals (paused under Biden, resumed under Trump) will accelerate new capacity development timelines
- Potential for demand destruction in Europe if TTF stays above 60 euros/MWh for an extended period
- Whether other LNG suppliers (Australia, Mozambique, Nigeria) can ramp to offset Qatar's absence

### Suggested Direction

Cheniere Energy is the blue-chip US LNG play with proven operational capacity, strong free cash flow, and a track record of capital returns. The stock has run 7% in a week but the structural case for elevated LNG pricing extends well beyond a single-week trade if Hormuz disruption persists. Venture Global is the higher-beta, earlier-stage play with more volume growth ahead but also more execution risk from its recent IPO and ongoing capacity ramp.

## Theme 2: Reshoring and Industrial Automation -- The Tariff Accelerator

### The Setup (one sentence)

Over $3 trillion in US reshoring-related investments have been announced since early 2025, and the combination of 10-15% universal tariffs, geopolitical supply chain risk from the Iran conflict, and a 500,000-worker manufacturing labor shortage is accelerating the shift toward automation-led domestic manufacturing at a pace that exceeds consensus expectations.

### Why It Is Interesting

This is a multi-year structural theme that has hit a sudden acceleration point. The Iran conflict demonstrates in real time why supply chain diversification away from geopolitically exposed chokepoints matters. The tariff regime -- now on firmer legal footing via Section 122 after the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA-based tariffs -- makes imported goods structurally more expensive, improving the economics of domestic production. And the 500,000 unfilled manufacturing job vacancy means this reshoring wave must be automation-intensive, creating an equipment capex supercycle on top of the AI infrastructure buildout.

ISM Manufacturing PMI at 52.4 with new orders at 55.8 confirms the manufacturing recovery is real. ISM's own survey shows 56% of manufacturers expect higher 2026 revenues, with a 4.4% net increase expected vs. 2.5% in 2025. Manufacturing capacity utilization has risen to 82.4%, up 3.2 percentage points from May 2025.

Sterling's current portfolio captures some of this through power generation exposure (VRT, CEG, VST), but has no direct exposure to industrial automation, factory equipment, or the reshoring infrastructure buildout.

### What I Know

- $3T+ in reshoring-related investments announced since early 2025 (source: industry data)
- 500,000 manufacturing jobs currently unfilled (source: multiple industry reports)
- ISM Manufacturing PMI: 52.4, second consecutive expansion; new orders 55.8; capacity utilization 82.4%
- ISM Prices Paid: 70.5, highest since June 2022 -- tariff pass-through creating pricing power for domestic producers
- Universal 10% Section 122 tariff effective Feb 24; China +10% effective March 4; Canada 25% effective March 4
- CHIPS Act and IRA continue to drive semiconductor, battery, and clean energy manufacturing facility construction
- Caterpillar (CAT): shifting production from Japan to Georgia and Texas; $725M investment in Lafayette, IN engine facility
- GE Aerospace (GE): reshoring jet engine and turbine manufacturing; defense-linked demand acceleration
- US industrial automation market forecast to grow 10%+ annually through 2030
- Rockwell Automation (ROK) and Emerson Electric (EMR) are the pure-play US industrial automation leaders

- Specific stock prices and recent earnings for ROK, EMR, CAT
- Whether tariff uncertainty is causing reshoring project delays or accelerating commitments
- Factory order data trends beyond ISM (Census Bureau durable goods, manufacturing shipments)
- Labor market wage inflation in manufacturing -- is the shortage driving wage costs that offset tariff advantages?
- How much of the $3T announced investment has actually broken ground vs. remaining as announcements

This is a multi-year compounding theme rather than a catalyst-driven trade. Rockwell Automation and Emerson Electric offer the most direct exposure to automation-led reshoring. Caterpillar captures both the construction equipment demand from factory building and the power generation equipment cycle. The right approach is to build positions with a 2-3 year horizon rather than timing entry to near-term catalysts.

## Theme 3: Food and Fertilizer Inflation -- The Underappreciated Third Leg of Cost-Push Pressure

### The Setup (one sentence)

The combination of surging energy costs (fertilizer input), elevated tariff-driven operating costs for farmers (+4% for corn, +6% for soybeans vs. 2025), and a 10-year high gap between farm costs and prices received is creating food inflation pressure that markets are not yet pricing, which complicates the Fed's disinflation narrative and benefits fertilizer producers with expanded margins.

### Why It Is Interesting

Most market commentary on inflation focuses on oil and tariffs. Food inflation is the third leg of the cost-push stool that very few investors are discussing. The USDA's midpoint grocery price increase estimate of 1.7% for 2026 appears increasingly stale given: (1) global food prices climbed 1.3% in January alone -- a sharp reversal that analysts warn could foreshadow a spike; (2) fertilizer prices are 10-15% higher than 2025; (3) farm operating costs are rising 4-6% due to tariffs; (4) the Iran oil shock will push energy-linked fertilizer costs higher still.

The natural gas spike is particularly important for fertilizer producers. Natural gas is the primary input for nitrogen-based fertilizers. US producers like CF Industries and Nutrien buy domestic gas at Henry Hub prices (up modestly) and sell fertilizer into a global market where prices are now being bid up by the energy-driven cost structure of international competitors paying LNG-linked prices. This is the same margin expansion dynamic as US LNG exporters, but in the fertilizer market.

### What I Know

- Gap between farm costs and prices received hit 10-year high (source: Farm Policy News, Jan 2026)
- Operating costs projected 4% higher for corn, 6% higher for soybeans in 2026 vs. 2025
- Fertilizer prices 10-15% higher than 2025; jumped 18% in 2025 (source: World Bank)
- Global food prices climbed 1.3% in January 2026 alone -- analysts warning of "calm before the storm"
- Fertilizer tariff rollback: most fertilizers exempt from tariffs via executive order (Nov 2025), easing direct cost pressure on US farmers but not addressing energy-driven production costs
- CF Industries (CF): nitrogen fertilizer producer; benefits from low Henry Hub gas costs vs. global competitors paying higher LNG-linked prices; margins expand when international gas prices spike
- Nutrien (NTR): world's largest potash producer; diversified across nitrogen, phosphate, and potash; retail distribution network provides channel visibility
- USDA midpoint forecast: grocery prices +1.7% in 2026 (likely understated given energy developments)
- ERS full forecast: overall food prices +2.7% in 2026, down from 3.0% in 2025

### What Needs Verification

- CF Industries and Nutrien current stock prices and recent quarterly results
- Whether China's relaxation of nitrogen/phosphate export limits will offset the energy-driven fertilizer price increase
- The pass-through timing from fertilizer costs to food prices (typically 2-3 quarters lag)
- Impact of USDA spring planting intentions data (March 31 release) on fertilizer demand outlook
- Whether the Iran oil shock duration is sufficient to materially alter fertilizer production economics

### Suggested Direction

CF Industries is the purer play on the nitrogen fertilizer margin expansion thesis -- it produces nitrogen-based fertilizers using domestic natural gas as feedstock, so it benefits directly from the widening spread between US gas prices and international gas prices. Nutrien is more diversified and offers exposure to the broader agricultural input cycle. The thesis strengthens if Henry Hub gas stays low while international gas prices remain elevated, and if the Iran conflict persists long enough to affect spring 2026 planting economics.

## Risks to Current Positioning

1. **Rapid ceasefire collapses energy/LNG/tanker trades.** If Iran-US hostilities de-escalate and Hormuz reopens within days rather than weeks, the energy supply premium unwinds rapidly. LNG, tanker, and fertilizer margin expansion theses all depend on sustained disruption. Position sizing must reflect this binary risk.

2. **Stagflation crystallizes.** GDP at 1.4% in Q4 2025 with CPI sticky at 2.4% and oil surging creates the conditions for a stagflationary outcome. If the Fed holds rates while growth declines, credit spreads (currently at 2.94% HY OAS vs. 4.9% 20-year average) could widen sharply, creating broad equity risk.

3. **Tariff escalation triggers demand destruction.** The universal 10% tariff + China 10% + Canada 25% is already the most aggressive trade posture in decades. If the administration escalates further toward the 15% universal target, the hit to consumer purchasing power could outweigh reshoring benefits.

4. **Oil spike pushes mortgage rates back above 6.5%.** The housing thesis (from March 3) depends on sub-6% mortgage rates holding. If oil-driven inflation pushes the 10Y yield above 4.5%, the mortgage rate benefit disappears. The housing stance has been downgraded to Neutral pending spring data.

5. **Credit market repricing.** HY OAS at 2.94% provides almost no cushion for a growth slowdown or a rate shock. A move to the 20-year average of 4.9% would represent significant spread widening and could trigger broader deleveraging.
