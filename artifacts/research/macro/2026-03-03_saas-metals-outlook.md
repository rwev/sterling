## Theme 1: The SaaSPocalypse — A Generational Entry Point in Enterprise Software

### The Setup (one sentence)

Enterprise software stocks have experienced their worst selloff since the dot-com bust, with the IGV ETF down 30% from its September 2025 peak and 73% of software stocks screening as oversold -- the highest reading in Jefferies' recorded history -- creating a potential generational entry point in quality recurring-revenue businesses.

### Why It Is Interesting

The trigger was Anthropic's launch of a legal automation tool in early February 2026, which sparked fears that AI would render entire categories of SaaS software obsolete. The selloff rapidly broadened into a sector-wide panic. An estimated $1 trillion in enterprise software market capitalization was destroyed in weeks. The term "SaaSPocalypse" was coined by Jefferies traders to describe the carnage.

The critical question for Sterling is whether this selloff represents a genuine structural disruption or an irrational pricing error. The evidence is increasingly pointing toward the latter for high-quality, mission-critical platforms:

1. **Revenue growth has not collapsed.** Salesforce grew revenue 12% y/y in Q4 (fastest in two years), ServiceNow grew subscription revenue 21% y/y, Workday grew revenue 14.5% y/y, HubSpot guided above Street expectations. These are not companies being disrupted -- they are growing.

2. **AI is additive, not destructive, for platform companies.** Salesforce's Agentforce exceeded $800M annualized revenue. HubSpot's AI agents are driving higher customer engagement. Datadog's AI observability tools are expanding its TAM. Companies that are platforms for AI integration benefit from the AI wave.

3. **Valuation compression is extreme.** Atlassian's market cap fell from $136B to $15B (an 83% decline from all-time highs). ServiceNow, growing 21%, trades at $104 -- down 44% from its 52-week high. The sector's RSI of 18 is the most oversold since 1990. JP Morgan, BofA, and Goldman Sachs have all published notes calling the selloff overdone.

4. **The "AI replaces SaaS" thesis is logically flawed for workflow software.** AI can automate tasks within SaaS platforms, but enterprise workflow orchestration, compliance, data governance, and system-of-record functions require the infrastructure that SaaS provides. AI is a feature, not a replacement for the platform. BofA's note called the selloff "illogical" and a "paradox."

## Theme 2: Metals and Materials Super-Cycle — Gold, Copper, and Rare Earths

### The Setup (one sentence)

Gold has surged past $5,400/oz (up 100% in 12 months), copper is entering structural deficit at ~$13,000/mt, and China's rare earth export controls are fracturing supply chains -- creating a multi-vector metals super-cycle driven by geopolitics, AI infrastructure demand, and central bank de-dollarization.

### Why It Is Interesting

Sterling already holds Agnico Eagle (AEM) and has Freeport-McMoRan (FCX) in the conditional pipeline. This theme validates and extends that positioning. Three distinct but reinforcing forces are driving metals simultaneously:

**1. Gold: Central Bank De-Dollarization and Geopolitical Hedging**

Gold at $5,400/oz is not a speculative bubble -- it is driven by structural central bank buying as reserve managers diversify away from US dollar holdings. The Iran conflict, tariff uncertainty, and fiscal deficit concerns are accelerating this trend. Bank of America calls gold "the primary hedge and performance driver in 2026." Goldman Sachs targets $4,900 by year-end (conservative given current levels), while Yardeni Research targets $6,000.

The gold miners are generating extraordinary cash flows at these prices. Newmont reported record $7.3 billion in free cash flow for FY2025 -- a 150% y/y increase -- with "tech-like" 40% net profit margins. Agnico Eagle reported its strongest quarter ever: $1.09B adjusted net income on $3.06B revenue. Barrick Mining shares are up 182% in the past year. The miners are not just benefiting from gold prices; they are converting price into cash at expanding margins because their cost structures are largely fixed.

**2. Copper: Structural Deficit from AI and Energy Transition**

Copper is entering a structural deficit driven by three converging demand sources:
- **AI data centers**: Each hyperscale data center requires 30,000-60,000 tons of copper for electrical wiring, busbars, and cooling systems. With $690B in hyperscaler capex in 2026, copper demand from data centers alone is adding millions of tons of annual demand.
- **Energy transition**: Electric vehicles, solar panels, wind turbines, and grid modernization all require significantly more copper per unit than legacy infrastructure. BloombergNEF projects energy-transition copper demand will triple by 2045.
- **Supply constraints**: J.P. Morgan expects a US refined copper deficit of 330,000 metric tons in 2026. Globally, new mine development takes 10-15 years from discovery to production. The supply response cannot match demand growth in the medium term.

Citigroup forecasts copper at a record $13,000/mt by mid-2026. The IMF's benchmark averaged $12,987/mt in January 2026, already near that target. Freeport-McMoRan at $68.29 is near its all-time high of $68.82, with expanded Indonesian mining rights adding to its production capacity.

**3. Rare Earths: China Export Controls Create Western Supply Chain Imperative**

China's rare earth export controls, imposed in April 2025 and tightened in early 2026 with new restrictions targeting Japan, have created the most significant supply chain disruption in critical minerals since China's 2010 rare earth embargo. S&P Global reports rare earth supply bottlenecks will persist throughout 2026.

This creates a structural investment case for non-Chinese rare earth producers:
- **MP Materials (MP)** at $61.40 is the only scaled rare earth producer in the Western Hemisphere, operating the Mountain Pass mine in California. The company has a Department of War Price Protection Agreement guaranteeing a $110/kg NdPr revenue floor from October 2025 -- a government-backed price floor that eliminates downside risk on its primary product.
- **Lynas Rare Earths (LYSDY)** is the largest non-Chinese rare earth producer globally, with operations in Australia and Malaysia. Shares are up 63.5% in three months.

**Steel / Aluminum (Caution)**

The tariff dynamic in steel and aluminum is becoming two-directional. Reports emerged in February that President Trump is considering rolling back steel and aluminum tariffs, which sent Nucor, Cleveland-Cliffs, and Alcoa down 5-6% in a single session. The 10% universal Section 122 tariff provides some baseline protection, but the original 50% steel duties that drove the 2025 rally may be at risk. This creates a more uncertain setup than the pure supply-deficit stories in copper and rare earths.

### What Needs Verification

- **Gold miner all-in sustaining costs (AISC)**: At $5,400 gold, margins are extraordinary, but the analyst team should verify AISC for NEM, AEM, and Barrick to quantify margin expansion precisely. At gold above $5,000, even high-cost producers are extremely profitable.
- **Copper demand from data centers**: The 30,000-60,000 tons per hyperscale facility figure needs sourcing and validation against actual construction data.
- **MP Materials production ramp**: Mountain Pass production volumes and downstream magnet manufacturing timeline need verification. The company is building a rare earth magnet factory in Texas -- this is a multi-year value creation story.
- **Tariff rollback probability for steel**: If 50% steel tariffs revert to 10% universal, the thesis for Nucor and Cleveland-Cliffs weakens materially. This needs close monitoring.
- **Silver supply/demand dynamics**: Silver at $94/oz has both precious metal (monetary/safe haven) and industrial (solar panels, electronics) demand drivers. The analyst team should assess whether silver miners or the metal itself is the better expression.

### Suggested Direction

The metals theme should be expressed through three distinct vectors, each routed to the appropriate analyst:

1. **Gold miners (Long Analyst)**: Newmont at $116 is the highest-quality gold miner in the world, generating $7.3B in FCF at current gold prices. This is not a speculative commodity bet -- it is a free cash flow machine. Agnico Eagle is already in the portfolio and should be sized appropriately for the gold bull market.

2. **Copper (Long Analyst)**: Freeport-McMoRan at $68 is the premier pure-play copper miner. The structural deficit thesis is backed by J.P. Morgan, BloombergNEF, and Citigroup. AI data center demand is a new, incremental demand source that copper supply cannot respond to for years.

3. **Rare earths (Small-Cap / Contrarian Analyst)**: MP Materials at $61 is a strategic asset with government-backed price protection. The China export control regime makes non-Chinese rare earth production a national security imperative. This is a multi-year structural story, not a trade.

4. **Steel/Aluminum (Caution)**: The tariff rollback risk makes Nucor and Cleveland-Cliffs less attractive than copper and rare earths. Wait for clarity on tariff policy before committing.

## Risks to These Themes

1. **AI actually does disrupt SaaS faster than expected.** If AI agents begin replacing enterprise workflow software rather than augmenting it, the SaaS recovery thesis breaks. The key leading indicator is net revenue retention rates -- if NRR starts declining at platform companies, the disruption thesis gains credibility. 2. **Gold price correction.** Gold has doubled in 12 months. A ceasefire in the Iran conflict, a hawkish Fed pivot, or easing trade tensions could trigger profit-taking. Gold miners would see margin compression and FCF decline. The structural central bank buying thesis provides a floor, but the speed of the rally suggests some speculative froth.

3. **Copper demand disappointment from AI capex slowdown.** If hyperscaler capex decelerates (the same risk flagged in the March 2 outlook), copper demand from data centers would fall short of projections. The energy transition demand driver is more durable, but the AI-specific incremental demand is less certain.

4. **Rare earth price volatility.** China could reverse export controls as a diplomatic concession, flooding the market and collapsing prices. MP Materials' $110/kg price floor mitigates this for NdPr specifically, but other rare earth products would be exposed.

5. **SaaS recovery takes longer than expected.** Even if fundamentals are sound, sentiment can remain depressed for quarters. The sector RSI may mean-revert slowly rather than violently. Sterling should be prepared to hold these positions through further volatility.

6. **Tariff policy whipsaw.** The current tariff regime (10% universal + higher for specific countries) is unstable. Further escalation raises input costs for everyone; rollback specifically hurts domestic steel/aluminum producers. The uncertainty itself is a risk factor.
