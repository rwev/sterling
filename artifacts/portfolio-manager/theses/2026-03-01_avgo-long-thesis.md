# Long Thesis: Broadcom Inc. (AVGO)

## Investment Summary

Broadcom is the most defensible business in the AI infrastructure stack. The company operates a duopoly with NVIDIA in AI silicon (custom ASICs for hyperscalers) and owns the VMware software franchise, which provides a stable, high-margin recurring revenue base. With a $73 billion AI backlog scheduled for recognition over 18 months, AI revenue growing 150%+ year-over-year, and adjusted EBITDA margins at 67%, Broadcom combines growth acceleration with financial fortress characteristics. At $343, the stock trades at approximately 17x FY2025 adjusted EBITDA and 27x forward earnings -- a discount to NVIDIA despite arguably superior business model durability. The 12-month analyst consensus target is $421 (23% upside), with Bank of America at $500.

## Business Overview

Broadcom operates across two segments:

**Semiconductor Solutions (~55% of FY2025 revenue):** Custom AI ASICs (XPUs) for hyperscaler customers including Google, Meta, ByteDance, OpenAI, and Anthropic. Broadcom holds 70-80% market share in custom AI silicon. The company also supplies networking ASICs (Memory, Jericho, Ramon switches), storage controllers, and broadband chips. The AI ASIC business is the primary growth engine, with five confirmed hyperscaler design wins and a $73 billion backlog.

**Infrastructure Software (~45% of FY2025 revenue):** The VMware franchise, acquired for $69 billion in November 2023, provides virtualization, cloud management, and networking software to enterprises globally. Broadcom has successfully transitioned VMware to a subscription model and is extracting significant margin expansion. This segment generates 90%+ gross margins and provides ballast against semiconductor cyclicality.

**Competitive Position:** Broadcom's moat in custom ASICs comes from its co-design relationships with hyperscalers. Each ASIC is a multi-year design cycle ($100M+ NRE investments) that creates deep customer lock-in. Once a hyperscaler commits to Broadcom's architecture, switching costs are enormous. The VMware moat is simpler: enterprises have built their entire infrastructure on VMware's hypervisor and management stack, and migration costs are prohibitive.

## Investment Case

1. **AI ASIC demand is structural, not cyclical.** Hyperscalers are diversifying silicon supply away from sole dependence on NVIDIA. Custom ASICs offer better performance-per-watt for specific inference workloads and give hyperscalers control over their silicon roadmap. This trend accelerates as AI inference scales. Broadcom's $73B backlog is contracted revenue, not aspiration.

2. **The VMware integration is delivering ahead of plan.** Broadcom has successfully converted VMware to subscription licensing and is driving EBITDA margins from ~35% pre-acquisition to 67%+ at the consolidated level. The software business generates $2B+ in quarterly recurring revenue with near-zero marginal cost.

3. **Free cash flow conversion is exceptional.** $26.9B FCF on $63.9B revenue = 42% FCF margin. This funds the dividend ($0.59/share quarterly, ~$12B annually), share repurchases, and debt reduction simultaneously. Few companies this large generate cash at this rate.

4. **Networking is the second AI growth vector.** Broadcom's Memory and Jericho switch ASICs are embedded in every major hyperscaler network fabric. As AI clusters scale from single-rack to campus-scale, networking bandwidth requirements grow multiplicatively. The Spectrum-X alternative from NVIDIA validates the opportunity but Broadcom's installed base is massive.

5. **Valuation is reasonable relative to growth and quality.** At 17x trailing EBITDA and ~27x forward P/E, Broadcom trades at a meaningful discount to NVIDIA (36x trailing P/E) despite comparable AI revenue growth rates and superior free cash flow margins.

## Earnings Quality

**GAAP vs. Adjusted Divergence:** Broadcom's non-GAAP adjustments are material but well-understood. The primary adjustments are: (1) amortization of acquired intangibles from VMware (~$4B annually), (2) stock-based compensation, and (3) restructuring charges. The intangible amortization is a real economic cost that will decline over time as the VMware purchase price is fully amortized. Investors should focus on non-GAAP EBITDA for operating performance but be aware that GAAP EPS ($1.40 guided for Q1 FY2026) is meaningfully below non-GAAP ($2.02).

**FCF Conversion:** Outstanding. FCF/EBITDA conversion of ~63% is strong for a company carrying $30B+ in gross debt. Working capital is well-managed -- semiconductor receivables turn quickly given the concentrated hyperscaler customer base.

**Accruals:** Low. The gap between net income and operating cash flow is primarily non-cash amortization, which is a positive signal for earnings quality.

**Capital Allocation:** Disciplined. Broadcom has been aggressively paying down VMware acquisition debt while maintaining its dividend. The dividend yield of ~1.4% provides a floor. Management has historically been excellent capital allocators under CEO Hock Tan.

## Risk Factors

1. **Customer concentration.** Five hyperscaler ASIC customers represent an outsized share of AI semiconductor revenue. Loss of any single customer (especially Google or Meta) would materially impact growth.

2. **VMware debt overhang.** Broadcom carries $30B+ in gross debt from the VMware acquisition. While debt is being paid down aggressively, a credit event or rate spike could pressure the balance sheet.

3. **NVIDIA competitive response.** NVIDIA could cut ASIC pricing or offer more flexible custom silicon options to retain hyperscaler customers. The Rubin platform is designed to be more customizable.

4. **Tariff exposure.** Broadcom's semiconductor manufacturing is concentrated at TSMC in Taiwan and assembly/test in Southeast Asia. Tariff escalation on Asian-sourced components could compress margins.

5. **AI capex deceleration.** If hyperscalers reduce or delay AI infrastructure spending, the $73B backlog could stretch or face renegotiation. This is the sector-wide risk, not Broadcom-specific.

6. **Antitrust scrutiny.** VMware's dominant market position in virtualization has drawn regulatory attention in Europe. Any forced divestiture or behavioral remedies could impair the software segment.
