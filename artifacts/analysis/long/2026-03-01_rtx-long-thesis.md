# Long Thesis: RTX Corporation (RTX)

## Investment Summary

RTX Corporation is the largest defense contractor in the world by revenue ($88.6B in 2025) and the dominant player in counter-drone systems -- the fastest-growing subsector of defense at a 22% CAGR. The February 2026 award of a $5.04 billion Coyote missile system contract from the U.S. Army, combined with a record $268 billion backlog (up 23% YoY), positions RTX at the intersection of two structural tailwinds: the autonomous drone proliferation wave and the counter-drone response it necessitates. Unlike pure-play drone stocks trading at 50-70x earnings, RTX offers this exposure at a reasonable 31x forward P/E with a 1.38% dividend yield and $7.9 billion in annual free cash flow. The Raytheon segment (counter-drone, missiles, integrated air defense) is inflecting with 130 basis points of margin expansion and 6% organic growth, and the $5B Coyote contract provides a multi-year revenue floor. RTX is the large-cap, quality-compounding way to play the autonomous warfare cycle without paying speculative multiples.

## Business Overview

RTX Corporation was formed from the 2020 merger of Raytheon Company and United Technologies Corporation. The company operates through three segments:

**Collins Aerospace** ($30.2B revenue, 2025): The world's largest aerospace systems provider. Supplies avionics, aerostructures, interiors, mechanical systems, and mission systems to both commercial and military customers. Serves every major airframe OEM and airline.

**Pratt & Whitney** ($32.9B revenue, 2025): One of the world's two large commercial jet engine manufacturers. Produces the GTF (Geared Turbofan) engine family for the Airbus A320neo family and military engines including the F135 powering the F-35.

**Raytheon** ($28.0B revenue, 2025): The world's premier missile and integrated defense systems business. Products include the Patriot air defense system, Stinger and Coyote missiles, Tomahawk cruise missiles, advanced radars, and electronic warfare systems. This segment is the primary beneficiary of the counter-drone spending acceleration.

**Key competitive advantages:**
1. **Installed base moat**: RTX products are embedded in every branch of the U.S. military and 80+ allied nations. Switching costs are measured in decades and billions.
2. **Counter-drone dominance**: The Coyote C-UAS family (kinetic Block 2 and non-kinetic Block 3NK) is the Army's primary counter-drone system, now backed by a $5B production contract through 2033.
3. **Diversified revenue**: 60% commercial / 40% defense provides cycle resilience. Defense procurement is counter-cyclical; commercial aerospace is in a multi-year up-cycle.
4. **Scale advantages**: $88.6B in revenue creates procurement leverage, R&D amortization advantages, and the ability to self-fund $4-5B in annual R&D.
5. **Record backlog**: $268B total backlog ($107B defense, $161B commercial) provides multi-year revenue visibility.

## Investment Case

### 1. Counter-Drone is the Highest-Growth Vector in a $268B Backlog

The $5.04 billion Coyote missile system contract awarded in February 2026 is a landmark event. It covers both kinetic interceptors (Coyote Block 2) and non-kinetic swarm-defeat systems (Coyote Block 3NK), with delivery through September 2033. On February 11, 2026, Raytheon successfully demonstrated the Block 3NK defeating multiple drone swarms at Army trials -- a capability no competitor has matched at scale.

The counter-UAS market is projected to grow from $3.9B to $36B over the next decade (22% CAGR). RTX is the incumbent supplier to the U.S. Army and positioned as the primary beneficiary. The DoD launched its first counter-drone marketplace on February 25, 2026, signaling institutional readiness to procure at scale.

### 2. All Three Segments Expanding Margins Simultaneously

For the first time since the merger, all three RTX segments delivered margin expansion in 2025:
- Collins Aerospace: +30 bps YoY
- Pratt & Whitney: +20 bps YoY
- Raytheon: +130 bps YoY (the most significant expansion, driven by volume and pricing)

Consolidated adjusted segment operating margin reached 11.9% in 2025, up from 11.4% in 2024. Management guides continued expansion in 2026, supported by volume growth, pricing, and productivity initiatives. The Raytheon segment in particular is transitioning from supply-chain-constrained production to full-rate manufacturing.

### 3. Free Cash Flow Inflection Confirms Earnings Quality

FCF surged from $4.5B in 2024 to $7.9B in 2025 -- a $3.4B increase (76% growth). This followed the resolution of Pratt & Whitney's GTF powder metal issue, which had depressed cash flow in 2023-2024 through inspection and retrofit costs. The $7.9B FCF represents a 4.8% FCF yield on the current enterprise value, well above the defense sector average of 3-4%.

Management guides $8.25-$8.75B FCF for 2026 -- another step up -- supporting the company's $40B+ capital return commitment (buybacks + dividends) through 2025-2027.

### 4. Iran Crisis Accelerates Defense Procurement

The Middle East escalation (Operation Epic Fury, Strait of Hormuz closure threats) compresses procurement timelines for air defense and counter-drone systems. RTX's Patriot, Stinger, and Coyote systems are the backbone of U.S. and allied air defense. Supplemental defense appropriations are likely, and RTX is the primary beneficiary of any accelerated missile and air defense spending.

### 5. Valuation is Reasonable for a Quality Compounder

At $203, RTX trades at 31x forward P/E ($6.70 midpoint 2026 guidance) and ~20x EV/EBITDA. This is a modest premium to the defense sector median (25-28x forward P/E) but justified by superior FCF generation, the highest backlog in the industry, and the counter-drone growth vector. The 1.38% dividend yield provides a floor, and the $40B+ capital return program supports the share price.

## Earnings Quality

**Strengths:**
- FCF conversion is excellent: $7.9B FCF on $6.29 adjusted EPS x 1.34B shares = $8.4B net income equivalent. FCF/net income > 90% -- a hallmark of high earnings quality.
- Adjusted vs. GAAP divergence is well-disclosed and primarily reflects merger-related amortization and one-time charges, which are declining annually as the 2020 merger ages.
- Working capital management improved materially in 2025 as Pratt & Whitney GTF inspection/retrofit inventory normalized.
- No off-balance-sheet concerns. Pension obligations are well-funded.

**Risks to monitor:**
- Net debt of ~$33B is significant in absolute terms, but leverage at ~2.6x EBITDA is within investment-grade norms and declining.
- The Pratt & Whitney GTF powder metal issue, while largely resolved, carries tail risk if additional inspection scopes are identified.
- Defense contract accounting allows for estimate-at-completion revisions that can shift profits between quarters. Monitor for negative EAC adjustments.

## Risk Factors

1. **Leverage**: $33B+ net debt is manageable at 2.6x EBITDA but limits financial flexibility in a downturn. The company is prioritizing deleveraging, targeting <2.5x by end of 2026.
2. **Pratt & Whitney GTF tail risk**: The powder metal inspection program is largely complete, but any expansion of scope could depress FCF again. Monitor fleet inspection progress and retrofit costs.
3. **Defense budget sequestration risk**: A political shift toward fiscal austerity or budget sequestration (unlikely in the current geopolitical environment but not impossible) would pressure defense revenue growth.
4. **Commercial aerospace cyclicality**: 60% of revenue is commercial. A recession-driven airline downturn would slow Collins and Pratt & Whitney growth, though backlog provides a buffer.
5. **Tariff impact on supply chain**: RTX has extensive global supply chains. The March 4, 2026 tariff escalation (20% on China) could increase costs for electronic components and raw materials, though defense contracts include cost escalation provisions.
6. **Counter-drone competitive risk**: While RTX dominates with Coyote, emerging competitors (directed energy systems, electronic warfare solutions from L3Harris and others) could erode market share in the C-UAS space.
