## Investment Summary

Lockheed Martin is the highest-conviction long in defense following the February 28 US-Israeli "Operation Epic Fury" strikes on Iran and the ensuing regional conflict. This is not a one-day geopolitical trade. LMT sits at the intersection of three structural forces: (1) a multi-year global munitions restocking cycle that was already underway before the Iran crisis, (2) framework agreements signed in January 2026 to quadruple THAAD and more than triple PAC-3 MSE production capacity, and (3) a record $194 billion backlog providing 2.5x revenue coverage. The Iran conflict accelerates and extends demand that was already secured contractually. At ~22x forward earnings with double-digit earnings growth visibility through decade-end, this is a quality compounder entering a structural upgrade cycle.

## Business Quality Assessment

### Competitive Position: Dominant

Lockheed Martin is the world's largest defense contractor and the sole-source or lead producer on the most critical weapons platforms relevant to the current conflict:

- **PAC-3 MSE interceptor** -- the primary missile defense interceptor used by Patriot batteries. Sole source. Record 620 units delivered in 2025. Framework agreement signed January 29, 2026 to scale production from 600 to 2,000 units per year.
- **THAAD interceptor** -- terminal ballistic missile defense. Sole source. Framework agreement signed January 29, 2026 to quadruple production from 96 to 400 interceptors per year over seven years.
- **HIMARS / GMLRS** -- the precision-strike system that redefined modern ground warfare in Ukraine. Production capacity doubled from 48 to 96 launchers annually. GMLRS round production has ramped to 14,000 per year and continues scaling.
- **F-35 Lightning II** -- the Western world's fifth-generation fighter. 191 deliveries in 2025 (record). Production rate at 156/year with further ramp potential.

These are not commodity products. They are sole-source, mission-critical systems with multi-year production lead times and switching costs measured in decades. The Iran conflict does not create demand for these systems -- it accelerates demand that already existed and removes political obstacles to procurement spending.

### Revenue Visibility: Exceptional

The $194 billion backlog (up 17% YoY, fourth consecutive year of growth) provides approximately 2.5 years of revenue coverage at current run rates. This is not soft demand -- these are funded government contracts with delivery schedules.

The Missiles and Fire Control (MFC) segment is the growth engine:
- 2025 MFC operating profit margin: 13.8%
- 2026 MFC revenue growth guidance: 14% YoY at midpoint (pre-Iran crisis)
- Management has guided to "at least double-digit" MFC sales CAGR through the end of the decade
- Book-to-bill consistently above 1.0x across all segments

### Capital Allocation: Shareholder-Friendly

- Free cash flow guided at $6.5-6.8B for 2026
- Dividend: $13.80/share annualized (~2.1% yield), 23 consecutive years of increases
- Share repurchase authorization: $9.1B remaining
- Total capital return in recent quarters: ~$1.5B/quarter
- Net debt of ~$18.6B is manageable at ~2.7x FCF; no near-term refinancing risk

## The Catalyst: Iran Conflict Reprices the Entire Demand Curve

The February 28 US-Israeli strikes on Iran represent the most significant Western military engagement since the 2003 Iraq invasion. The market implications for defense procurement are structural:

1. **Immediate munitions consumption**: US and Israeli forces are expending precision-guided munitions at wartime rates. Every Patriot intercept, every THAAD engagement, every GMLRS salvo depletes inventories that take years to replenish. The restocking cycle begins immediately and extends for 3-5 years minimum.

2. **Allied spending acceleration**: European NATO nations were already moving toward 2%+ GDP defense spending. A hot US war in the Middle East removes the last political barriers to supplemental appropriations. Germany, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and Gulf states will all accelerate procurement.

3. **Congressional support**: Defense spending increases are bipartisan in a conflict environment. Supplemental appropriation bills for munitions restocking have historically moved through Congress within 60-90 days of conflict onset.

4. **Production framework agreements already in place**: The January 2026 framework agreements to quadruple THAAD and triple PAC-3 MSE production were signed before the Iran conflict. These agreements now have political urgency behind them. Funding acceleration is the most likely outcome.

5. **Duration uncertainty favors the long**: Whether this conflict lasts weeks or months, the restocking and modernization cycle extends for years. Even a rapid de-escalation leaves depleted inventories and heightened threat perception that sustains elevated procurement budgets.

The current consensus EPS of ~$30.06 for 2026 reflects pre-crisis guidance. The Iran conflict creates upside through:

- **MFC revenue acceleration**: If MFC growth moves from 14% to 18-20% (plausible given production ramp frameworks already in place), this adds $500M-$1B in incremental revenue at 13-14% margins.

- **Mix shift toward higher-margin munitions**: Missiles and munitions carry segment-leading margins vs. lower-margin Aeronautics (F-35 production).

Base case revised EPS: $31-32 for 2026, rising to $34-36 for 2027 as production ramps hit full stride.

## Bear Case (Why This Could Be Wrong)

1. **Rapid de-escalation**: If a ceasefire is reached within days and the Strait of Hormuz reopens, the geopolitical premium unwinds. Defense stocks could give back 5-10% of any crisis-driven gains. However, the structural restocking cycle still occurs -- inventories were depleted regardless of conflict duration.

2. **Production execution risk**: Quadrupling THAAD and tripling PAC-3 MSE production is operationally complex. Supply chain bottlenecks, labor constraints, or facility construction delays could push revenue recognition into 2028+. The multibillion-dollar facility investment program across 20+ sites carries execution risk.

3. **Valuation already reflects quality**: At ~22x forward, LMT is not cheap relative to its 5-year average of ~17-18x. A portion of the defense premium is already priced. If the conflict resolves quickly and earnings estimates do not move, multiple compression is possible.

4. **Bipartisan risk to defense spending**: While defense spending increases are historically bipartisan during conflict, a prolonged engagement could generate domestic opposition that constrains supplemental appropriations. Fiscal hawks may resist open-ended spending commitments.

5. **Classified program losses in Aeronautics**: The Aeronautics segment reported adjusted margins of only 9.9% in 2025, dragged by classified program losses. If these losses persist or expand, they offset MFC gains at the consolidated level.

## Summary

Lockheed Martin offers the clearest risk-reward in the defense sector following the Iran crisis. The thesis rests not on a geopolitical bet but on an observable and already-contracted production ramp cycle that the conflict accelerates. Record backlog, sole-source positions on the most consumed weapons systems, framework agreements for massive production capacity expansion, and a shareholder-friendly capital allocation policy make this a quality compounder entering a structural upgrade. The bear case requires both rapid de-escalation and no lasting change to allied procurement behavior -- a combination that has no historical precedent following a conflict of this magnitude.

Entry at $650-685, target $725, stop $595.
