# Long Thesis: ConocoPhillips (COP)

## Investment Summary

ConocoPhillips is the highest-quality large-cap independent E&P, generating extraordinary free cash flow at $89 WTI against a mid-$40s pre-dividend breakeven. The stock trades at 18.5x trailing earnings on consensus estimates calibrated to $60 WTI -- a price environment that is $29/bbl below current spot and $25/bbl below the base case for the next several months given the Strait of Hormuz closure and Trump's unconditional surrender demand against Iran. Marathon Oil integration synergies of $1B+, the Willow project approaching first oil, and a committed 45% of CFO shareholder return policy provide multiple layers of value creation beyond the commodity price windfall. At $89 WTI, COP's annualized FCF yield approaches 10-12%, making it among the most attractive risk/reward setups in the large-cap universe.

## Investment Case

The investment case rests on three pillars:

**1. Structural oil supply deficit with no diplomatic off-ramp.** The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed for seven consecutive days, removing approximately 20% of global crude and LNG supply. Trump's demand for "unconditional surrender" from Iran, combined with Iran's refusal to negotiate, eliminates the visible diplomatic off-ramp. Base case is $85-95 Brent sustained for weeks to months. COP's production is entirely onshore North America (post-Marathon integration: Lower 48, Alaska, Canada), giving it zero Hormuz exposure on the supply side and full upside to the global pricing impact.

**2. Analyst estimates massively understate the earnings power.** Consensus forward EPS estimates were built on $60 WTI assumptions per COP's own 2026 guidance framework. At $89 WTI, every $10/bbl above the base case adds approximately $2.5-3.0B in incremental pre-tax cash flow annually, given COP's 2.33-2.36 Mboe/d production guidance and roughly 60% oil/liquids weighting. The forward P/E of 24.9x on consensus is misleading -- at $89 WTI strip, the effective forward P/E is closer to 12-14x.

**3. Best-in-class capital return program with structural growth catalysts.** COP returns 45% of CFO to shareholders through dividends ($3.36/share, 2.86% yield) and buybacks. At $89 WTI, CFO could reach $18-20B, implying $8-9B returned to shareholders annually. Beyond the commodity windfall, COP has identified $1B+ in Marathon Oil integration synergies, the Willow project (on track, 50% complete, first oil expected 2029 with $4B FCF step-up), and LNG project completion (NFE startup H2 2026).

## Business Overview

ConocoPhillips is the largest independent E&P company globally by production and market capitalization ($143.6B). Following the 2024 Marathon Oil acquisition ($22.5B), COP operates a diversified portfolio of onshore North American assets:

- **Lower 48**: Permian Basin, Eagle Ford, Bakken, and legacy Marathon acreage. The core of near-term production and FCF generation.
- **Alaska**: North Slope legacy operations plus the Willow project (5 drilling pads, 50% complete). Expected to contribute $4B in incremental FCF by 2029.
- **Canada**: Oil sands and conventional operations. Stable, long-life assets with low decline rates.
- **International**: LNG-focused (APLNG in Australia, Qatar LNG expansion). NFE LNG startup expected H2 2026.

2026 guided production: 2.33-2.36 Mboe/d. Capital expenditures: $12B. Adjusted operating costs: $10.2B (down $400M from 2025 guidance due to Marathon synergies and operational efficiencies).

COP does not operate refineries, chemicals, or retail -- it is a pure upstream play. This means higher leverage to oil prices (both up and down) compared to integrated majors like XOM and CVX, but also a cleaner FCF profile without downstream capital demands.

## Earnings Quality

**GAAP vs. Adjusted divergence**: COP reported GAAP EPS of $6.35 for FY 2025. Adjusted EPS strips out Marathon integration charges and asset impairments. The divergence is well-explained and declining as integration completes. No red flags.

**FCF conversion**: FCF/EBITDA conversion of 32% in FY 2025 reflects the $12B capex program. This is appropriate for a company building Willow and integrating Marathon. As capex normalizes post-Willow (management targets lower capex intensity), FCF conversion should improve to 40%+.

**Accruals and working capital**: COP's upstream-only model generates clean cash flows with minimal working capital complexity. No receivable/inventory concerns. DD&A runs at ~$11B annually, which is substantial but reflects the asset base scale post-Marathon.

**Capital allocation discipline**: The 45% of CFO return commitment is the right balance -- generous enough to reward shareholders, conservative enough to fund Willow and LNG growth. COP does not stretch its balance sheet to fund returns, unlike some smaller E&Ps.

## Risk Factors

1. **Ceasefire or Iranian capitulation (probability: 10-15% near-term).** A sudden de-escalation could cause oil to drop $20-30/bbl in days, compressing COP's near-term earnings power by 30-40%. The mid-$40s breakeven provides a floor, but the stock would re-rate to $90-100 rapidly. This is the primary risk and must be managed through position sizing.

2. **SPR release or price cap intervention.** The Trump administration could draw down the Strategic Petroleum Reserve or impose domestic price controls to cap gasoline prices. This would be bearish for WTI but not necessarily for COP's international-linked volumes.

3. **Execution risk on Willow and LNG projects.** Arctic construction and LNG facility completion carry schedule and cost overrun risk. Willow is 50% complete; delays would push back the $4B FCF step-up.

4. **Roth/MKM downgrade reflects legitimate concern.** Bank of America noted COP's "relatively high breakeven" versus smaller peers like FANG and DVN. While COP's mid-$40s breakeven is well below current prices, it is higher than Diamondback's low-$30s, meaning COP has less downside cushion in a price collapse.

5. **Production hedging at lower prices.** COP historically does not hedge significantly (unlike smaller E&Ps), which means full exposure to price upside but also full exposure to downside. At $89 WTI, the absence of hedging is a tailwind.
