# Long Thesis: DHT Holdings (DHT)

## Investment Summary

DHT Holdings is a pure-play VLCC (Very Large Crude Carrier) owner-operator with a fleet of 23+ vessels, benefiting from the most extreme tanker rate environment in history. VLCC spot rates have surged to $423,000-$481,000 per day following the Strait of Hormuz closure, approximately 20-26x the company's spot P&L breakeven of $18,300/day. DHT has locked in three one-year time charters at $90,000-$105,000/day, providing earnings visibility even if spot rates normalize. For Q1 2026, 76% of available spot days are booked at $78,900/day and 86% of total available days are booked at $62,300/day -- implying Q1 EPS well above the $0.41 earned in Q4 2025 at lower rates. At $18.07, the stock trades at 14.2x trailing P/E and 9.5x forward P/E, with a 5.4% dividend yield and 100% payout of ordinary net income. The company has prepaid all debt maturities through end of 2026, has $189M in total liquidity, and is taking delivery of four newbuild VLCCs in H1 2026 that arrive into the strongest rate environment in shipping history. The thesis is high-upside but explicitly binary: a ceasefire or Hormuz reopening would collapse spot rates from $400K+ to $50-80K within days. Sterling already holds FRO at 4%; DHT offers fleet-specific diversification with stronger charter-rate protection.

## Investment Case

### 1. All-Time Record VLCC Rates With Structural Support

VLCC spot rates hit $423,736-$481,170 per day on Middle East-Asia routes in the first week of March 2026, shattering all historical records. This is not a speculative spike -- it is driven by physical supply removal. The Strait of Hormuz, which handles 20% of global oil trade, has seen traffic drop from 98 cargo ships on February 28 to a single vessel on March 3, with zero tanker transits. Iraq has shut the 1.5M bpd Rumaila field because tankers cannot leave. Kuwait is shutting fields as onshore storage fills. Saudi Arabia is rerouting through the East-West Pipeline at limited capacity.

The key structural dynamic is vessel supply absorption. Rerouting global shipping around the Cape of Good Hope adds 10-14 days to every voyage, effectively removing 15-20% of available VLCC tonnage from the market through longer transit times alone. This tightening extends to non-Middle East routes as well -- the vessel supply crunch is global, not regional.

DHT management has characterized the current environment as a "perfect storm" driven by robust crude oil transport demand, geopolitical risk premiums, and an aging global VLCC fleet with limited newbuild deliveries expected through 2027.

### 2. One-Year Time Charters Provide Downside Protection

Unlike pure spot-exposed tanker operators, DHT has locked in earnings protection through three one-year time charter agreements:

- DHT Redwood: $105,000/day (beginning March 2026)
- DHT Taiga: $94,000/day
- DHT Opal: $90,000/day

These charters are at rates 5-6x the company's breakeven, providing a floor under earnings even if spot rates normalize. The one-year duration implies the counterparties (major energy companies) expect the disruption to persist well beyond a few weeks. Management plans to increase spot exposure to approximately 75% of fleet capacity by Q2 2026, balancing upside capture with charter-rate protection.

### 3. Fleet Renewal Into the Strongest Market in History

DHT is taking delivery of four newbuild VLCCs in H1 2026: DHT Antelope (delivered January 2, 2026, already trading spot), DHT Addax, DHT Gazelle, and DHT Impala. These are fuel-efficient modern vessels that command a premium in the charter market. Simultaneously, the company sold three 2007-built VLCCs for $150M+ in proceeds with $60M in expected gains.

This fleet renewal strategy -- selling old vessels at cycle-high secondhand prices and taking delivery of newbuilds financed at 1.32% SOFR-plus margins -- is textbook capital allocation for a cyclical shipping company. The fleet will grow from 22 VLCCs (Q4 2025) to approximately 24 VLCCs by mid-2026, all deployed into the strongest rate environment in shipping history.

### 4. Conservative Balance Sheet and 100% Net Income Payout

DHT maintains a disciplined capital structure:

- Interest-bearing debt: $364M (as of Q1 2025)
- Financial leverage: 17.6%
- All scheduled debt installments prepaid through end of 2026
- Total liquidity: $189M ($79M cash + $110.5M revolving facility)
- Newbuild financing: $308.4M credit facility at SOFR + 1.32% weighted average margin, 12-year maturity

The company has declared dividends for 64 consecutive quarters, paying 100% of ordinary net income. The Q4 2025 dividend was $0.41/share. At current spot rates, Q1 2026 dividends could be materially higher. Trailing twelve-month dividends of $0.98/share represent a 5.4% yield at the current price.

### 5. Earnings Power at Current Rates Is Extraordinary

A simple earnings sensitivity illustrates the opportunity:

|---|---|---|---|
| Q4 2025 actual | $60,300 | ~$485 | $1.64 (annualized) |
| Q1 2026 booked | $62,300 (86% days) | ~$525 | ~$2.00 (annualized) |

The current spot rates are not sustainable at $400K+ and should not be modeled as steady-state. The more relevant scenario is the 1-year charter rate of $90,000-105,000, which the market is pricing as the expected average over the next 12 months. At that rate, DHT would earn approximately $3.50-4.50 per share annually -- implying a P/E of 4-5x on the current stock price.

## Business Overview

DHT Holdings, Inc. is a crude oil tanker company headquartered in Hamilton, Bermuda, with integrated management across Monaco, Norway, Singapore, and India. The company operates exclusively in the VLCC segment, the largest class of oil tankers with capacity of approximately 2 million barrels per voyage.

As of March 6, 2026, the fleet consists of approximately 24 VLCCs following recent newbuild deliveries. The company employs a blend of spot market and time charter employment, with management targeting approximately 75% spot exposure in Q2 2026 to maximize upside from elevated rates.

DHT's operating model is asset-light relative to integrated shipping companies -- it owns and operates VLCCs, with commercial and technical management conducted in-house. The spot P&L breakeven is approximately $18,300/day, reflecting the company's low cost structure.

## Earnings Quality

- **GAAP vs. Adjusted divergence:** Minimal. DHT reports clean earnings with limited adjustments. The main non-cash items are vessel depreciation and amortization. The $60M gain from sale of three 2007-built VLCCs will be recorded in upcoming quarters and is transparent asset recycling, not earnings manipulation.
- **FCF Conversion:** Operating cash flow for the first nine months of 2025 totaled $203.7M. FCF is somewhat compressed by newbuild installment payments, which are investment spending, not operating costs. Excluding newbuild capex, FCF conversion from EBITDA exceeds 80%.
- **Accruals and Working Capital:** Shipping companies have simple working capital structures. Receivables cycle with voyage settlement (30-60 days). No inventory concerns. Working capital is not a source of earnings distortion.
- **Balance Sheet Durability:** All debt maturities through end of 2026 are prepaid. The $308.4M newbuild facility carries a 12-year maturity with a 20-year amortization profile. No near-term refinancing risk. Financial leverage at 17.6% is conservative for the shipping sector.
- **Dividend Sustainability:** The 100% ordinary net income payout is aggressive but appropriate for a capital-light shipping company with low debt. Dividends are variable -- they move with earnings, which move with rates. This is a feature, not a bug: shareholders receive cash flow directly when rates are high.

## Risk Factors

1. **Ceasefire Binary Risk (PRIMARY):** This is the single largest risk. VLCC spot rates at $400K+/day are entirely conflict-driven. A diplomatic breakthrough, Iranian capitulation, or Hormuz reopening would collapse rates from $400K to $50-80K within days. DHT's 1-year charters at $90-105K provide some cushion, but spot-exposed vessels (75% of fleet by Q2) would see immediate rate compression. The stock could fall 15-25% in a single session.

2. **Concentration Risk with Existing FRO Position:** Sterling holds Frontline (FRO) at 4% portfolio weight. Adding DHT would concentrate tanker exposure in a highly binary setup. The combined tanker weight should not exceed 6-7% of portfolio, per the macro outlook's guidance.

3. **VLCC Orderbook Risk:** While the current orderbook is historically low, any surge in newbuild orders triggered by current record rates could weigh on medium-term rate expectations. However, newbuild deliveries take 2-3 years, so this is not a near-term threat.

4. **Insurance and War-Risk Cost Pass-Through:** Marine war-risk insurance premiums have spiked to 1.0-1.5% of hull value. While these costs are typically passed through to charterers, any delay in pass-through or coverage gaps could temporarily impact margins.

5. **Chinese Demand Risk:** China is the largest destination for VLCC cargoes. A Chinese economic slowdown or demand reduction could pressure rates even in a supply-constrained environment. The current rate spike is supply-driven, not demand-driven -- removing supply disruption would reveal the underlying demand picture.

6. **Analyst Price Targets Do Not Reflect Current Environment:** The consensus analyst PT is $16.33, below the current price of $18.07. BTIG's PT is $18.00. Analyst targets were set before the Hormuz closure and likely have not been updated to reflect $400K+ spot rates. This cuts both ways -- it means the street has not caught up, but it also means there is limited traditional sell-side support at current levels.
