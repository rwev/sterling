# Long Thesis: D.R. Horton, Inc. (DHI)

## Investment Summary

D.R. Horton is the largest homebuilder in the United States by volume, uniquely positioned to benefit from the 30-year mortgage rate falling below 6% for the first time since September 2022. The company has been spending heavily on mortgage rate buydowns (subsidizing buyer rates to stimulate demand), compressing gross margins from 22.7% to 20.4% over the past year. With market rates now naturally below 6%, this margin headwind converts to a tailwind — every 50bps decline in the rate buydown expense flows directly to gross profit. Q1 FY2026 results (reported January 2026) showed revenue of $6.9B (beat by 2.7%), EPS of $2.03 (beat by 3.6%), and net sales orders up 3% to 18,300 homes, confirming demand stabilization. At 13.5x trailing P/E with a $153 stock price, DHI trades at a reasonable valuation for the largest player in a sector entering a cyclical recovery. The thesis depends on mortgage rates remaining at or below 6% through the spring selling season.

## Business Overview

D.R. Horton, Inc., headquartered in Arlington, Texas, is America's largest homebuilder by closings volume, operating in 125 markets across 36 states. The company operates through four segments:

- **Homebuilding** (~94% of revenue): Entry-level and move-up homes under the D.R. Horton, Emerald Homes, Express Homes, and Freedom Homes brands. Average selling price approximately $365,000.
- **Forestar Group** (~4%): Residential lot development subsidiary; supplies lots to DHI and third-party builders.
- **Financial Services** (~1.5%): Mortgage origination and title services for DHI homebuyers.
- **Rental** (~0.5%): Single-family and multi-family rental communities.

D.R. Horton's competitive advantage is scale: the company's purchasing power, land optioning strategy (60%+ of lots controlled via options rather than owned outright), and geographic diversification enable industry-leading cost positions. The company closed 89,690 homes in FY2025, more than any other US builder.

Market cap: ~$48.9B. Shares outstanding: ~319M.

## Investment Case

### 1. Mortgage Rate Buydown Margin Recovery

This is the core of the thesis. For the past two years, D.R. Horton and other major builders have been subsidizing buyer mortgage rates — typically buying down market rates from 6.5-7.0% to an effective 4.5-5.5% for the buyer. This comes at a direct cost to the builder's gross margin, typically 200-400 basis points.

The 30-year fixed mortgage rate averaged 5.98% for the week of February 26, 2026 (Freddie Mac), the first sub-6% reading since September 2022. If market rates remain at or below 6%, the incentive to offer rate buydowns diminishes significantly, because buyers can obtain competitive rates without builder subsidies.

The math is straightforward:
- Q1 FY2026 home sales gross margin: 20.4%
- Q1 FY2025 home sales gross margin: 22.7%
- Difference: 230bps, largely attributable to increased rate buydown and incentive costs
- If rate buydowns are reduced by even 100-150bps of margin recovery, gross margin returns to ~21.5-22.0%, adding $0.50-0.80/share in quarterly EPS

This is not a speculative projection — it is a mechanical reduction in a known expense line item that depends on observable mortgage rate data.

### 2. Spring Selling Season Demand Catalyst

January existing home sales dropped 8.4% month-over-month (the largest decline since 2022), but this pre-dated the rate decline below 6%. The spring selling season (March-May) will be the real test of housing demand. Several indicators support a constructive outlook:

- Housing permits "shattered expectations" in early 2026 data
- NAR forecasts a 14% increase in existing home sales for 2026
- Zillow predicts continued mortgage rate improvement
- DHI's Q1 FY2026 net sales orders increased 3% YoY to 18,300 homes, with order value of $6.7B
- The sub-6% rate unlocks "rate-lock" sellers who have been unwilling to list homes purchased at sub-4% rates during 2020-2021

### 3. Volume Leadership and Scale Advantage

D.R. Horton's scale provides several structural advantages:
- **Purchasing power:** The largest buyer of lumber, concrete, and building products in the US commands volume discounts that smaller builders cannot match.
- **Land optioning:** 60%+ of lots are controlled through options rather than owned outright, reducing balance sheet risk and capital intensity.
- **Geographic diversification:** Operations in 125 markets across 36 states provide natural hedging against regional oversupply (e.g., Dallas and Phoenix are oversupplied, but Northeast/Midwest markets remain supply-constrained).
- **Affordability focus:** DHI's average selling price of ~$365,000 positions the company squarely in the first-time and entry-level buyer segment, which is most rate-sensitive and benefits most from declining mortgage rates.

### 4. Tariff-Insulated Domestic Operations

The 10% universal Section 122 tariff (effective February 24) and 25% tariff on Canadian lumber create a more favorable competitive environment for domestic homebuilders relative to imported building materials. D.R. Horton's domestic supply chain and purchasing scale position it to absorb or pass through tariff-driven cost increases more effectively than smaller builders.

### 5. Capital Return and Balance Sheet Strength

D.R. Horton generated $3.4B in operating cash flow in FY2025 (10% of revenue, 95% of net income). Free cash flow was $3.3B, up 62% from FY2024. The company returned capital through:
- Dividends: ~1.1% yield at current price
- Share repurchases: ongoing program

The balance sheet is conservatively managed with an investment-grade credit rating. Net debt is modest relative to the asset base (land, homes under construction, and finished inventory).

## Earnings Quality

D.R. Horton's earnings quality is generally good, with some cyclical considerations:

- **GAAP vs. adjusted:** DHI reports primarily on a GAAP basis with minimal adjustments. There is no significant GAAP-adjusted divergence. This is a positive quality indicator.
- **FCF conversion:** FY2025 FCF of $3.3B significantly exceeded net income of $3.6B due to working capital management (controlled inventory build). FCF conversion was 92% of net income.
- **Inventory accounting:** Homebuilder inventory is carried at the lower of cost or net realizable value. There are no impairment concerns at current price levels. The land option strategy limits write-down risk (options can be abandoned rather than land written down).
- **Revenue recognition:** Revenue is recognized at home closing, which is observable and verifiable. There is no subscription or deferred revenue complexity.
- **Working capital:** Inventory management is the key working capital variable. DHI's Q1 FY2026 homes in inventory was well-managed, with the company actively moderating starts to match demand.

- **Base case (rates 6.0-6.5%, moderate spring):** Gross margin stabilizes at 20.5-21%. FY2026 EPS of $10-11. At 13x, stock trades to $130-143. Current price of $153 implies some recovery is already priced in.
- **Bear case (rates rise above 6.5%, oil shock):** Gross margin declines to 19-20%. FY2026 EPS of $8-9. At 10x P/E, stock trades to $80-90.

## Risk Factors

1. **Oil shock reverses mortgage rate decline (PRIMARY RISK):** If Brent crude sustains above $90-100/barrel (currently ~$79.40 and rising), CPI could reaccelerate, pushing the 10Y yield above 4.5% and mortgage rates back above 6.5%. This would invalidate the margin recovery thesis and reignite the rate buydown headwind. The tanker and housing theses are partially offsetting within a portfolio context.

2. **Spring selling season disappoints:** If March-April housing data shows continued weakness despite lower rates, the demand thesis fails. The January 8.4% decline in existing home sales is a cautionary signal, though it pre-dated the rate drop.

3. **Regional oversupply:** Dallas and Phoenix markets show rising vacancy and inventory. DHI has significant exposure to Texas and the Sunbelt, where oversupply risk is concentrated.

4. **Tariff cost pass-through:** The 10% universal tariff and 25% Canadian lumber tariff increase input costs. While DHI's scale provides purchasing leverage, some cost inflation will compress margins if it cannot be passed through to buyers.

5. **DOGE federal workforce layoffs:** Federal government layoffs concentrated in the Washington DC metro could reduce housing demand in that region. DHI has moderate exposure to the mid-Atlantic market.

6. **Earnings already reflect partial recovery expectations:** At 13.5x P/E (above peer median and above DHI's own historical average), the market has already priced in some margin recovery. Upside requires above-consensus execution.
