# Long Thesis: Cheniere Energy (LNG)

## Investment Summary

Cheniere Energy is the largest US LNG exporter and the primary structural beneficiary of the Strait of Hormuz closure and Qatar's LNG production halt, which together have removed approximately 20% of global LNG supply from the market. The company operates 45 mtpa of liquefaction capacity across Sabine Pass and Corpus Christi, buys feedstock gas at Henry Hub prices (~$4/MMBtu), and sells into international markets where European TTF gas has surged 76% in a single week to over EUR 60/MWh. This widening spread flows directly to Cheniere's export margins. The stock trades at 7.3x EV/EBITDA on FY2025 results -- a discount to its own 5-year average and well below the S&P 500 -- while generating 20%+ ROIC and returning capital aggressively through a $10B share repurchase authorization targeting 175M shares outstanding (from ~210M today). The combination of structural supply dislocation, proven operational execution, and disciplined capital returns creates a compelling risk/reward at current levels.

## Business Overview

Cheniere Energy operates the largest LNG export platform in the United States through two facilities:

- **Sabine Pass LNG** (Cameron Parish, Louisiana): Six operational trains with approximately 30 mtpa of nameplate capacity. Operated through subsidiary Sabine Pass Liquefaction, LLC.
- **Corpus Christi LNG** (Texas): Three operational trains plus Stage 3 expansion (first train achieved first LNG production in December 2024, first cargo in February 2025). Full Stage 3 buildout will add approximately 10 mtpa, bringing total Corpus Christi capacity to approximately 25 mtpa.

The business model is straightforward: Cheniere purchases natural gas at US domestic prices (indexed to Henry Hub), liquefies it, and sells LNG under a combination of long-term sale and purchase agreements (SPAs) and spot/short-term contracts. Long-term SPAs typically include fixed liquefaction fees (~$3.50/MMBtu) plus a variable component linked to Henry Hub, providing downside protection. Spot and short-term sales capture the full international price spread, providing upside when global gas prices spike.

In 2025, Cheniere produced a record 670 LNG cargoes totaling over 46 million tons -- establishing operational leadership in the global LNG market. The US is now the world's largest LNG exporter, ahead of Qatar and Australia.

## Investment Case

### 1. Structural Supply Dislocation Creates Multi-Quarter Pricing Tailwind

The Strait of Hormuz closure and Qatar's LNG production halt represent the most significant disruption to global LNG supply since the market's inception. Qatar accounts for approximately 20% of global LNG trade, and nearly all of it transits the Strait. Maritime insurers (Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, London P&I Club, American Club) have cancelled war risk cover effective March 5, creating a de facto blockade. Even if hostilities end, insurance normalization and production restart timelines suggest weeks-to-months of disrupted supply.

European TTF gas prices surged 76% in a single week to over EUR 60/MWh. US Henry Hub prices rose only ~5%. This spread differential translates directly to Cheniere's export margins: the company buys feedstock at ~$4/MMBtu and sells into markets where equivalent prices are $15-20+/MMBtu. The Henry Hub/TTF spread is at multi-year wides.

### 2. Near-Capacity Operations Sustain Pricing Power

Unlike commodity markets where supply can ramp to meet demand, US LNG export infrastructure is operating near capacity. Cheniere cannot materially increase volumes in the short term -- but this is a feature, not a bug. Capacity constraints mean sustained pricing power rather than a volume chase. New capacity additions (Golden Pass, Plaquemines Phase 2, Rio Grande) will come online over 2026-2028, but Cheniere's own Stage 3 expansion positions it to capture incremental volumes as they become available.

### 3. Proven Capital Allocation and Shareholder Returns

Cheniere completed its "20/20 Vision" capital allocation plan, deploying over $20B since H2 2022 while achieving run-rate distributable cash flow of over $20/share. The plan funded:
- $7B in share repurchases
- $6B+ in growth capital expenditures
- $5B+ in debt repayment
- ~$1.5B in declared dividends

The company has now authorized a new $10B share repurchase program through 2030, targeting approximately 175M shares outstanding (down from ~210M currently -- a ~17% reduction). The dividend has grown ~10% annually with management committed to maintaining that trajectory through decade-end. Management has set a long-term goal of $30/share in run-rate distributable cash flow.

### 4. Corpus Christi Stage 3 Expansion Provides Visible Growth

The first train of CCL Stage 3 achieved first LNG production in December 2024 and shipped its first cargo in February 2025. The full Stage 3 buildout will add approximately 10 mtpa of capacity, providing a visible growth trajectory through 2027-2028. This incremental capacity enters a market characterized by structural undersupply, ensuring strong utilization and pricing.

## Earnings Quality

Cheniere's financial reporting is among the cleanest in the energy sector. Key quality indicators:

- **DCF conversion is strong.** FY2025 distributable cash flow of ~$5.3B represents ~76% conversion from adj. EBITDA of $6.94B, reflecting the low-maintenance-capex nature of liquefaction operations once trains are built.
- **GAAP vs. adjusted divergence is modest.** The primary adjustment is unrealized mark-to-market gains/losses on derivative instruments, which are standard for commodity hedging programs. Q4 2025 GAAP EPS of $10.68 beat estimates by 180%, driven partly by favorable mark-to-market on LNG forward contracts -- a reflection of the current pricing environment.
- **Working capital is stable.** Inventory and receivables move with commodity prices but do not show signs of channel stuffing or aggressive revenue recognition.
- **Debt management is disciplined.** The company repaid over $5B in debt under its 20/20 Vision plan while maintaining investment-grade credit ratings. Net debt/EBITDA at ~2.5x is manageable for an infrastructure business with long-duration contracted cash flows.
- **Accruals are low.** Capital expenditure is lumpy (tied to train construction) but transparent and separately disclosed.

## Risk Factors

1. **Rapid ceasefire and Hormuz reopening.** If the Iran-US conflict de-escalates quickly and Qatar restarts LNG production within days, the pricing premium unwinds rapidly. The stock has already moved ~7% on the disruption news, and a reversal could take the stock back to the $230-240 range.

2. **Long-term contract structure limits upside capture.** A significant portion of Cheniere's volumes are sold under long-term SPAs with fixed liquefaction fees. The spot/short-term exposure that captures the full TTF spread may be 20-30% of total volumes, limiting the magnitude of near-term earnings upside.

3. **Stage 3 execution risk.** Construction delays or cost overruns on remaining Stage 3 trains would delay the capacity growth trajectory. However, first-train production in December 2024 and first cargo in February 2025 suggest execution is on track.

4. **Commodity price sensitivity.** While Cheniere benefits from the international gas price premium, a sustained collapse in global gas prices (e.g., from demand destruction, mild winter, or alternative supply sources ramping) would compress margins.

5. **Regulatory and policy risk.** Changes to US LNG export policy -- while unlikely under the current administration, which has resumed DOE export permit approvals -- could affect long-term capacity expansion plans.

6. **Elevated net debt.** Net debt/EBITDA at ~2.5x is manageable but leaves less room for error in a downturn. The company's investment-grade rating and long-duration contracted cash flows mitigate this risk.
