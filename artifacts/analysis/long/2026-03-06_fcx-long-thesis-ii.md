# Long Thesis: Freeport-McMoRan (FCX) -- Updated

## Investment Summary

Freeport-McMoRan has pulled back 15% from its February 25 all-time high of $69.75 to $59.36, entering the $58-64 entry range defined in our March 2 thesis. The pullback was driven by the broader equity selloff (S&P -1.33%, VIX 25.26) and commodity price volatility, not by any deterioration in FCX's fundamentals. Since the original thesis, two material developments have strengthened the investment case: (1) the Section 232 tariff on semi-finished copper products (50% duty, effective August 2025) has created a structural US copper premium that directly benefits FCX's 1 billion pound/year North American rod segment, and (2) FCX announced a life-of-resource extension MOU for Grasberg operating rights on February 18, removing the single largest long-term overhang on the stock. Morgan Stanley raised its price target to $70 (from $53) specifically on the tariff benefit. The stock now trades at 19.8x forward P/E on depressed Grasberg production, compressing to 13-15x on normalized 2027-2029 earnings. With net debt/EBITDA at 0.5x, $4.3B in cash, and $425M in EBITDA sensitivity per $0.10/lb copper, FCX offers asymmetric upside to copper price appreciation and Grasberg production restoration, with structural tariff protection as a margin floor.

## Investment Case

### 1. Section 232 Tariff Creates a Structural US Copper Premium (New Since March 2 Thesis)

The August 2025 Section 232 tariffs imposed 50% duties on semi-finished copper products while exempting raw copper and cathodes. This created a sustained arbitrage: analysts estimate 730,000-830,000 tonnes of copper are now economically trapped in the US, swelling CME warehouse stocks while tightening global LME availability. FCX is the dominant US copper producer, supplying approximately 70% of total US refined copper production through its integrated domestic mining and processing facilities. The company sells approximately 1 billion pounds annually through its North American copper rod segment, which captures the full tariff-advantaged premium.

This is not a temporary dislocation. A Commerce Department review due June 30, 2026 will determine whether additional phased tariffs on refined copper (15% in 2027, 30% in 2028) will be implemented. If enacted, the arbitrage premium extends for years. Morgan Stanley raised its FCX price target from $53 to $70 on January 27 specifically because of this tariff benefit.

Critically, the tariff thesis survives a Hormuz ceasefire. Unlike tanker rates or oil prices, the Section 232 copper tariff is a US trade policy instrument that has no connection to Middle East geopolitics. This makes FCX a lower-binary-risk way to play the current macro environment compared to energy or shipping names.

### 2. Grasberg Life-of-Resource Extension Removes the Largest Overhang

On February 18, 2026, FCX announced an MOU with the Indonesian government for a life-of-resource extension of PTFI's operating rights in the Grasberg minerals district. FCX will transfer a 12% share interest in PTFI to government interests in 2041 (maintaining 48.76% through 2041, then ~37% from 2042). In exchange, PTFI's IUPK license will be amended to cover the full life of the resource, not just the current license period.

This addresses the key long-term political risk flagged in our March 2 thesis: Indonesia sovereign risk. While regulatory conditions remain (export permits, smelter requirements, community support commitments), the formal agreement to extend operating rights through the life of the resource is a structural de-risking event for the stock.

### 3. Stock Has Pulled Back Into Entry Range on Macro Selloff

FCX peaked at $69.75 on February 25 and has fallen 15% to $59.36 as of March 6 close. Our March 2 thesis defined an entry range of $58-64, noting that "a 10% pullback to $61-62 would offer a materially better risk/reward." The stock is now below that level. The pullback is attributable to three factors:

- Broader equity selloff: S&P 500 dropped 1.33% on March 6 as Brent surged to $92.69 and NFP printed -92K
- Basic materials sector selloff: the sector fell 1.65%, with copper-exposed names disproportionately hit on recession fears
- Copper price volatility: LME copper pulled back from the January record of $13,387/ton to approximately $12,500/ton

None of these factors impair FCX's structural thesis. The NFP miss introduces cyclical demand risk, but structural copper demand from electrification, AI data centers, and EVs is not GDP-sensitive in the same way as construction or industrial copper demand.

### 4. Grasberg Restart Remains on Track for Q2 2026

The September 2025 mud rush killed seven workers and displaced 800,000 metric tons of wet material into the block cave. FCX has guided for a phased restart of Production Blocks 2 and 3 beginning in Q2 2026, with 85% of district production restored by H2 2026. Full-rate production of 1.6B lbs copper and 1.3M oz gold annually is targeted for 2027-2029. This production restoration represents a step-change in FCX's earnings power.

2026 Grasberg production is guided at approximately 1.0B lbs copper and 0.9M oz gold (similar to 2025 depressed levels), with the ramp occurring primarily in H2. The Q1 2026 earnings report (April 23, 2026) will be the first update on restart progress.

### 5. Copper Price Sensitivity Provides Asymmetric Earnings Leverage

FCX discloses that each $0.10/lb change in copper price impacts annual EBITDA by $425M and operating cash flow by $330M. Year-to-date 2026 LME copper prices have averaged approximately 30% higher than the 2025 average. At current prices near $5.67/lb ($12,500/ton), FCX's unit net cash costs of $1.75/lb for 2026 (declining to $1.25/lb in H2 2026 as Grasberg restarts) provide a margin of nearly $4/lb on every pound of copper produced. This is an extraordinary margin for a commodity producer.

## Business Overview

Freeport-McMoRan is the world's largest publicly traded copper producer, headquartered in Phoenix, Arizona. The company operates through three segments:

- **Grasberg (Indonesia):** One of the world's largest copper-gold deposits. Operated through PT Freeport Indonesia (PTFI), in which the Indonesian government holds a 51% interest. Currently in phased restart after September 2025 mud rush.
- **Americas (Arizona, New Mexico, Colorado, Chile, Peru):** Open-pit copper mines including Morenci (largest in North America), Cerro Verde (Peru), and El Abra (Chile). Includes the North American copper rod segment that benefits from Section 232 tariffs.
- **Atlantic Copper (Spain):** Custom copper smelting and refining.

FY2025 production: approximately 4.1B lbs copper, 1.5M oz gold, 80M lbs molybdenum. Revenue mix: ~75% copper, ~15% gold, ~5% molybdenum, ~5% other.

## Earnings Quality

- **GAAP vs. Adjusted divergence:** The primary adjustments relate to the Grasberg mud rush incident charges in Q4 2025. These are discrete, non-recurring items. The company's adjusted EBITDA is otherwise clean.
- **FCF Conversion:** Operating cash flow of $5.6B versus EBITDA of $10B implies roughly 56% conversion. The gap is capex-driven ($4.3-4.5B annually) rather than working capital-driven. As Grasberg remediation capex normalizes, FCF conversion should improve to 60-65% of EBITDA.
- **Accruals and Working Capital:** $1.3B in working capital uses in FY2025 is elevated. This deserves monitoring but is not unusual for a mining company managing inventory and receivables during a production disruption.
- **Balance Sheet Durability:** Net debt/EBITDA of 0.5x with $4.3B in cash and $4.85B in available credit facilities. The balance sheet can comfortably absorb a copper downturn to $4/lb for multiple years.
- **Conservative Accounting:** FCX uses standard mining industry accounting. Indonesia's PTFI is consolidated, providing full visibility into Grasberg economics. No off-balance-sheet structures of concern.

## Risk Factors

1. **Grasberg Restart Execution:** The Q2 2026 phased restart is the defining catalyst. Any delay or additional geological incidents would de-rate the stock. The 2025 production was approximately 35% below pre-incident estimates. Management execution is critical.

2. **Copper Price Cyclicality:** Current copper prices near $12,500/ton ($5.67/lb) are elevated. Goldman Sachs forecasts $10,000-11,000/ton for 2026 ($4.54-5.00/lb), and Morgan Stanley's base case is $10,650/ton ($4.83/lb). If these forecasts prove correct, FCX's EBITDA would be materially below current run-rate. The NFP print of -92K reinforces the cyclical demand risk.

3. **Indonesia Political Risk:** Despite the Grasberg extension MOU, the 12% share transfer in 2041 dilutes FCX's future ownership. Regulatory changes (export permits, environmental standards, fiscal terms) remain an ongoing risk. The MOU requires formal government issuance of an amended IUPK to become binding.

4. **Section 232 Review Risk:** The Commerce Department review due June 30, 2026 is binary. While additional tariffs would extend the US copper premium, an adverse ruling or policy reversal could compress the arbitrage. The tariff benefit to the rod segment is real but policy-dependent.

5. **Recession Impact on Industrial Copper Demand:** The NFP miss of -92K and rising oil prices create stagflationary risk. While structural electrification demand is secular, cyclical industrial and construction demand -- still a majority of copper consumption -- is GDP-sensitive. A US recession would pressure copper prices despite tariff support.

6. **Cost Inflation:** Unit net cash costs guided at $1.75/lb for 2026 with H2 improvement to $1.25/lb. If mining input costs (diesel, labor, explosives) rise with inflation, the cost improvement may not materialize as guided.
