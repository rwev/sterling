## Thesis Summary

Ducommun is a $1.85B market cap Tier 2 defense sub-contractor with direct exposure to the most conflict-relevant missile programs in the U.S. arsenal -- THAAD, Tomahawk, AMRAAM, PAC-3, SM-3, and MIR. The company just reported record Q4 2025 results with missile bookings exceeding $130M at a book-to-bill above 4x, and management disclosed approximately 30% spare factory capacity available for DoD production ramps with only modest incremental capital expenditure required.

The February 28 US-Israeli strikes on Iran and the ensuing regional conflict create an immediate and sustained procurement catalyst for exactly the missile systems Ducommun supplies. This is not a speculative play on geopolitics -- it is a fundamental acceleration of an order cycle that was already building before the crisis. Ducommun's largest customers, RTX (Raytheon) and Lockheed Martin, are the prime contractors on the missile programs that will see the most urgent restocking demand. Long-term framework agreements are already in place.

With only 5 analysts covering the stock, significant insider ownership, record margins, a clean balance sheet, and spare capacity to absorb production ramps, DCO offers a rare combination: a high-quality, under-followed defense compounder with an imminent geopolitical catalyst that the market has not yet priced into a $1.85B name.

## Company Overview

Ducommun Incorporated, founded in 1849 and headquartered in Santa Ana, California, provides engineering and manufacturing services for aerospace and defense applications. The company operates through two segments:

- **Electronic Systems:** Manufactures complex electronic and electromechanical products including missile guidance assemblies, radar subsystems, and electronic warfare components.
- **Structural Systems:** Produces aerostructures, including missile body components, aircraft fuselage assemblies, and composite structures.

The company serves as a critical Tier 2 supplier, manufacturing specialized components that flow into prime contractor weapons systems. Its customer base is concentrated among the largest U.S. defense primes, with RTX (Raytheon) as the largest customer and Lockheed Martin as the second largest. Both relationships are governed by long-term framework agreements that provide visibility into future order flow.

## Investment Case

### 1. Direct Exposure to Conflict-Relevant Missile Programs

Ducommun's missile franchise -- approximately 25% of defense segment revenue -- is tied to the specific weapons systems most relevant to the Iran conflict:

- **THAAD** (Terminal High Altitude Area Defense) -- the primary U.S. missile defense system deployed to the Middle East
- **Tomahawk** -- the precision strike cruise missile used in Operation Epic Fury
- **AMRAAM** (Advanced Medium-Range Air-to-Air Missile) -- air superiority weapon
- **PAC-3** (Patriot Advanced Capability) -- point defense against Iranian ballistic missiles
- **SM-3** (Standard Missile 3) -- Aegis ballistic missile defense, deployed on Navy vessels in the Persian Gulf
- **MIR** (Miniature Interceptor for Rockets) -- a newer program with a single order exceeding $80M, one of the largest in company history

Q4 2025 missile bookings exceeded $130M with book-to-bill above 4x. This order surge preceded the Iran crisis. The conflict will accelerate and extend this demand cycle as missile inventories require replenishment across the U.S., Israel, and allied nations.

### 2. Spare Capacity Is the Critical Differentiator

Management disclosed approximately 30% spare factory capacity available for anticipated DoD production ramps, requiring only modest incremental capital expenditure to scale. This is the key differentiator versus other defense sub-contractors: Ducommun can convert accelerated orders into revenue without the 12-24 month lead time typically required for capacity expansion. In a crisis environment where speed of production matters, suppliers with existing spare capacity receive preferential order flow.

### 4. Vision 2027 Targets Provide Medium-Term Upside

Management's Vision 2027 targets: $950M-$1B in revenue and 18% adjusted EBITDA margin. At the midpoint ($975M revenue, 18% EBITDA margin), this implies ~$175M in adjusted EBITDA by 2027. At a conservative 12x EV/EBITDA multiple -- below the defense sub-contractor peer average of 14-16x -- this implies an enterprise value of $2.1B and an equity value of approximately $1.9B, or ~$128 per share. At peer multiples of 14-15x, the equity value rises to $2.2-2.4B, or $148-$161 per share.

The Iran crisis materially increases the probability of exceeding these targets. Management's 2026 guidance of mid-to-high single digit revenue growth was issued before the conflict escalation. Missile order acceleration could push growth into double digits.

### 5. Minimal Analyst Coverage Creates Information Asymmetry

The lack of coverage means: (a) earnings revisions flow through slowly as few analysts update models, (b) institutional awareness is limited relative to the opportunity, and (c) conference call commentary -- particularly around missile orders and capacity utilization -- receives less scrutiny than it deserves.

## Risks

1. **Geopolitical de-escalation:** A rapid ceasefire or Hormuz reopening would reduce the urgency of missile restocking, compressing the order acceleration timeline. However, even in a de-escalation scenario, missile inventories still need replenishment -- the timeline just extends.

2. **Litigation settlement overhang:** The $150M settlement ($56M insurance-funded, remainder from Ducommun) is a one-time cash outflow that produced a GAAP net loss of $33.9M in 2025. The cash impact is real but manageable given $390M in liquidity. Investors may need time to look through the GAAP noise.

3. **Customer concentration:** RTX and Lockheed Martin represent a significant portion of revenue. While framework agreements provide stability, any procurement slowdown or program cancellation at the prime level flows through to DCO.

4. **Revenue guidance back-half weighted:** Management guided 2026 growth as back-half weighted. If missile orders take longer than expected to convert to revenue, the first half of 2026 may disappoint on a quarterly basis even if the full-year trajectory is strong.

5. **Commercial aerospace softness:** The commercial aerospace segment is expected to be relatively flat, with recovery from destocking not accelerating until H2 2026 and into 2027. Defense is carrying the growth burden.

6. **Tariff risk on Mexico operations:** Ducommun operates a facility in Guaymas, Mexico for TOW missile case production. The March 4 tariffs of 25% on Mexican imports could increase costs, though defense procurement typically includes cost pass-through provisions.

7. **Small-cap risk-off flows:** The macro regime has shifted to risk-off following the Iran crisis. Small caps broadly face selling pressure regardless of fundamentals. DCO could decline with the Russell 2000 even as its fundamental thesis strengthens.

## Why This Thesis Is Differentiated

The large-cap defense primes -- LMT, RTX, NOC, GD -- will see immediate analyst upgrades and institutional flows on Monday. Every sell-side desk will publish a defense sector note. But the Tier 2 sub-contractor supply chain, where companies like Ducommun operate, will receive far less attention despite facing similar or greater percentage earnings upside.

When RTX and Lockheed ramp missile production, Ducommun must ramp first. The sub-contractor order flow follows prime contractor procurement by 30-90 days. This lag creates a window where the fundamental catalyst is building but has not yet been reflected in DCO's stock price or analyst estimates.

With only 5 analysts, minimal institutional awareness, and a $1.85B market cap, DCO is the type of name that institutional investors discover 2-3 quarters after the catalyst begins -- precisely the setup that creates the largest returns for early-positioned small-cap investors.
