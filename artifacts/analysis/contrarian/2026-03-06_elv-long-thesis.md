# Contrarian Long Thesis: Elevance Health (ELV)

## Investment Summary

Elevance Health trades at $289.64, down 37% from its 52-week high of $458.75, at 11.5x trailing earnings and 11.1x forward earnings -- the cheapest valuation in a decade for the second-largest managed care company in the United States. The stock sits 6% above its 52-week low of $273.71 after absorbing three distinct shocks in rapid succession: (1) the CMS January 26 proposal of a near-flat 0.09% Medicare Advantage rate increase for 2027, which triggered an $80 billion sector-wide sell-off; (2) Q4 2025 results showing a benefit expense ratio of 93.5%, up 110bps year-over-year, confirming medical cost trend pressure; and (3) CMS sanctions announced February 27 threatening to suspend new Medicare Advantage enrollment starting March 31 over historical risk adjustment data submission issues. The market has priced all three headwinds as permanent impairments to a business that generates $197.6 billion in annual revenue, $4.3 billion in operating cash flow, has 45.2 million medical members, and is owned by Seth Klarman (8.43% of Baupost's portfolio), Leon Cooperman, and five other superinvestors. At 11x forward earnings for a non-cyclical healthcare franchise in a stagflationary macro, the margin of safety is compelling and the catalyst path is clear: the CMS final 2027 rate decision, due by April 6, has historically come in materially above the advance notice (the 2026 final rate was 5.06% vs. a 2.23% proposal -- a 2.83 percentage point improvement). Any meaningful increase from the 0.09% proposal will trigger a re-rating.

## Why the Market Is Wrong

The consensus bear case on Elevance rests on four pillars. Each is either overstated, temporary, or already priced to perfection on the downside.

1. **"The 0.09% Medicare Advantage rate proposal means permanently impaired margins."** The proposed rate is not the final rate. CMS has a well-documented pattern of raising rates between the advance notice and the final announcement. In 2026, the advance notice proposed 2.23% and the final rate came in at 5.06% -- a 2.83 percentage point improvement driven by updated fee-for-service expenditure data. The improvement in 2025 and prior years followed the same pattern. The 0.09% advance notice for 2027 includes a 1.53% haircut from eliminating "unlinked" chart reviews and full V28 risk adjustment model implementation. Even if these remain in the final rate, the effective growth rate component will be updated with newer expenditure data that captures the same medical cost trend pressures the insurers are citing. A final rate of 2-4% is plausible and would represent a massive improvement from the level the market has priced. The final rate is due by April 6, 2026 -- exactly one month away.

2. **"CMS sanctions will cripple Medicare Advantage membership growth."** The sanctions relate to a data submission compliance issue -- Elevance was submitting risk adjustment data via encrypted USB drives rather than through CMS electronic systems. This is an administrative process failure, not a fraud allegation. CMS itself stated that if Elevance submits corrections by March 30 for data from November 2018 through October 2025, the enrollment suspension will not take effect. The company revised its practices in April 2023 after receiving additional regulatory guidance and has stated it is working cooperatively with CMS. The 1.9 million existing Medicare Advantage members are unaffected. The enrollment freeze would take effect after the annual open enrollment period has already ended, limiting its near-term membership impact. This is a resolvable compliance issue being priced as existential risk.

3. **"Medical cost trend is out of control -- the MLR of 93.5% in Q4 shows structural margin compression."** The Q4 2025 benefit expense ratio of 93.5% was elevated by two specific factors: heightened ACA health plan utilization and Part D seasonality from Inflation Reduction Act changes. The full-year 2025 MLR of 90.0% is the more representative figure, and management guided 2026 to approximately 90.2%, plus or minus 50 basis points. Elevance is addressing medical cost pressure through multiple levers: commercial pricing increases (premium yields drove operating revenue growth of 13% in 2025), deliberate exits from unprofitable Medicaid and commercial risk-based books (reducing membership by a projected low-double-digit percentage in 2026), benefit design adjustments, and Carelon vertical integration capturing service revenue that previously leaked to third parties. The company explicitly traded membership for margin by guiding to operating revenue declining in the low-single-digit range while maintaining adjusted EPS guidance of at least $25.50 -- demonstrating margin discipline over growth.

4. **"The guidance cut from $30.29 in 2025 to at least $25.50 in 2026 signals deterioration."** This comparison is misleading. The 2025 adjusted EPS of $30.29 included approximately $3.75 per share in favorable nonrecurring items. Stripping those out, normalized 2025 EPS was approximately $26.54. The 2026 guidance of at least $25.50 represents a 4% decline from normalized earnings -- modest and expected given the deliberate membership reduction and Medicaid rate lag. Management simultaneously reaffirmed at least 12% adjusted EPS growth beginning in 2027, signaling that 2026 is the trough year. The market is pricing the trough as permanent.

## Business Overview

Elevance Health is the second-largest managed care organization in the United States by revenue ($197.6 billion in 2025), serving 45.2 million medical members across four segments: Health Benefits ($167.1 billion operating revenue, +11% YoY), CarelonRx (pharmacy benefit management), Carelon Services (healthcare services and analytics), and Corporate. The company is the exclusive Blue Cross and Blue Shield licensee in 14 states, providing a regulated-market moat that no competitor can replicate -- an insurer cannot enter an Elevance Blue Cross territory without building an entirely new network from scratch.

The company operates a two-pillar strategy: Health Benefits (the legacy insurance core) and Carelon (the high-growth healthcare services arm). Carelon reached $71.7 billion in revenue in 2025, a 33% increase year-over-year, with adjusted operating gain of $3.4 billion (+10% YoY). The Carelon vertical integration strategy captures margin on pharmacy, behavioral health, post-acute care, and infusion services that Elevance previously paid to third-party providers. This is the same strategic playbook UnitedHealth Group executed with Optum, but Elevance is earlier in the monetization curve -- meaning more upside remains.

The membership mix is diversified: Commercial (employer risk and fee-based, plus Individual ACA), Medicare Advantage (approximately 1.9 million members), Medicaid (subject to redetermination attrition), and Federal Employee Program. The company's 14-state Blue Cross franchise provides structural competitive advantage in network depth, provider relationships, and brand recognition that protects commercial share regardless of the Medicare environment.

## Bear Case Rebuttal

**"Medicare Advantage is a broken business -- the CMS is structurally underpaying."** The 0.09% advance notice for 2027 is alarming, but the advance notice is not the final rate. In 2026, the advance notice proposed 2.23% and the final rate was 5.06% -- a 2.83 percentage point improvement. The increase came from updated fee-for-service expenditure data that captured the same rising medical costs the insurers are reporting. If medical costs are genuinely running at 7-10% (as all managed care companies claim), then the FFS baseline used to calculate MA benchmark rates will also reflect that inflation, producing a higher final rate. The market is pricing the advance notice as if it were the final rate. History says it will not be.

**"The CMS sanctions prove Elevance has compliance problems."** The sanctions relate to data submission method -- USB drives instead of electronic upload -- not to fraudulent coding or inflated risk scores. CMS offered Elevance a clear path to avoid the sanctions: submit corrections by March 30. The company has already revised its practices (in April 2023) and is cooperating. If Elevance complies, the sanctions never take effect. Even if they do take effect on March 31, they only prevent new enrollment -- existing 1.9 million members are unaffected, and the freeze occurs after the annual open enrollment window has closed. This is a process issue, not a franchise-threatening event. Compare it to the UnitedHealth DOJ fraud probe -- which is a genuine existential risk -- and Elevance's compliance issue is minor.

**"Medical cost trend at 7-10% will keep compressing margins through 2027."** Elevance is not passive in the face of medical cost inflation. The company has multiple offsetting levers: (1) commercial premium rate increases (premium yields drove 13% revenue growth in 2025); (2) deliberate exit from unprofitable Medicaid and commercial risk-based contracts (low-double-digit risk membership decline in 2026 improves mix); (3) Carelon vertical integration capturing margins previously paid to third parties ($71.7B revenue, $3.4B operating income in 2025, growing at 33%); (4) benefit design adjustments (higher copays, narrower networks, prior authorization refinements); and (5) the CMS final rate for 2027, which will partially offset medical cost inflation. Management guided the 2026 benefit expense ratio to approximately 90.2%, essentially flat with 2025's 90.0%, indicating the margin tools are working.

**"EPS is declining -- 2026 guidance of $25.50 vs. 2025 actual of $30.29 is a 16% drop."** This is the most misleading data point in the bear case. The 2025 adjusted EPS of $30.29 included $3.75/share in nonrecurring favorable items. Normalized 2025 EPS was approximately $26.54. The 2026 guidance of at least $25.50 is a 4% decline from normalized, not 16%. More importantly, management reaffirmed at least 12% adjusted EPS growth beginning in 2027. The 2026 trough is deliberate -- Elevance is repositioning its membership mix and absorbing Medicaid rate lag in a single year to build a higher-quality earnings base for 2027+.

## Catalyst Path

1. **CMS 2027 Final Rate Announcement (by April 6, 2026):** The single most important catalyst. The advance notice proposed 0.09%, but the 2026 final rate came in 2.83 percentage points above its advance notice. Any final rate above 2% would be materially positive and would trigger a sector-wide re-rating. The comment period closed February 25; the final rate is imminent.

## Risk Factors

1. **CMS 2027 final rate comes in at or below 1%.** If the final rate does not improve materially from the 0.09% advance notice, the Medicare Advantage business faces genuine margin pressure in 2027. This would invalidate the catalytic thesis and could push the stock toward the $255-260 range (10x trough EPS). While historically unlikely (the final rate has exceeded the advance notice in every recent year), the current CMS posture on "payment accuracy and sustainability" suggests a more aggressive regulatory stance than prior administrations.

2. **Medical cost trend accelerates beyond 10%.** If medical cost inflation pushes from 7-10% toward 12%+, even favorable CMS rates and pricing actions may not be sufficient to restore margins. The Q4 2025 benefit expense ratio of 93.5% could become the norm rather than the aberration. Q1 2026 MLR data in the April earnings report is the critical data point.

3. **CMS sanctions not resolved -- enrollment freeze extends through October AEP.** If Elevance fails to satisfy CMS requirements by March 30 and the enrollment suspension persists through the October-December Annual Enrollment Period, the company could lose significant new Medicare Advantage enrollment for 2027. This would create a multi-year membership headwind. The risk is low given Elevance's stated cooperation, but not zero.

4. **Medicaid redetermination continues to erode membership.** Elevance has already lost approximately 500,000 members year-over-year to Medicaid eligibility reverification. If attrition continues at the current pace, the fixed-cost leverage of the Health Benefits segment deteriorates, pressuring the operating margin.

5. **DOJ investigation contagion.** The UnitedHealth Group DOJ fraud investigation has created sector-wide regulatory uncertainty. If the DOJ investigation widens to include other managed care companies, including Elevance, the legal overhang would intensify and the multiple compression could deepen.

6. **Ceasefire or geopolitical de-escalation reduces the defensive bid.** In a stagflationary environment, managed care's non-cyclical revenue profile is a strategic advantage. If a ceasefire in the Middle East collapses oil prices, improves risk appetite, and triggers a cyclical recovery rotation, funds would rotate out of defensive stocks like ELV into growth and cyclicals.
