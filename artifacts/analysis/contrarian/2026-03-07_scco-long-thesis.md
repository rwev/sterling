# Contrarian Long Thesis: Southern Copper Corporation (SCCO)

## Investment Summary

Southern Copper is the world's lowest-cost copper producer, operating at $0.42/lb net cash cost after by-product credits, with a 58.5% EBITDA margin and 33% ROIC -- financial quality that is virtually unmatched in the global mining industry. The stock trades at $185, down 17% from its all-time high of $224 set just three weeks ago on February 27, 2026. Seventeen Wall Street analysts have a consensus Sell rating with a median price target of $98 (47% below current) and an average target of ~$136. Short interest has risen to 12.5% of float with 4.9 days to cover. Insiders have sold $3.1M in shares over the past six months with zero purchases.

The market is treating SCCO as an overvalued cyclical at peak earnings. The contrarian question is whether the market is anchoring to backward-looking valuation metrics while underestimating the multi-decade structural copper deficit that makes SCCO's cost leadership and reserve base worth more than any historical multiple framework suggests.

At current prices, the margin of safety is insufficient. The thesis becomes compelling at $140-155, where the forward P/E compresses to 22-24x on 2026E EPS of $6.36, the EV/EBITDA approaches 15x (still a premium to peers, but justified by cost structure), and the dividend yield rises to 2.6-2.8%. This thesis is a conditional buy -- a quality asset where the market is right about near-term valuation but potentially wrong about the structural earnings trajectory.

## Why the Market Is Wrong

The consensus bear case rests on three pillars, each of which contains a forensic vulnerability:

**1. "SCCO is overvalued at 36x trailing P/E."** The trailing P/E is misleading because it captures a year (FY2025) where copper averaged approximately $4.70/lb. Consensus estimates project 2026 EPS of $6.36 (21.4% growth), which compresses the forward P/E to 29x at $185 and 22-24x at the $140-155 entry range. More critically, if copper reaches $6.50-7.00/lb in 2027-2028 (consistent with J.P. Morgan's deficit-driven forecast), SCCO's cost structure produces normalized EPS of $8-10, implying a P/E of 18-23x at current prices and 14-19x at the entry range. The market is valuing SCCO on today's earnings, not on the earnings power of the world's cheapest copper producer in a structural deficit.

**2. "Production is declining 4.7% in 2026."** This is a transient headwind from lower ore grades at Peruvian mines. The production trajectory inflects upward in 2027 when Tia Maria begins commissioning (30,000 tonnes in H2 2027, ramping to 120,000 tonnes/year), driving a path to 970,000 tonnes in 2028, 1,060,000 tonnes in 2029-2030, and ultimately 1,600,000 tonnes by 2035 -- a 67% increase from current levels. The market is penalizing a one-year production dip while ignoring a $19.9B capital investment pipeline that expands output by two-thirds over the next decade. No other major copper miner has this combination of organic growth visibility and existing cost leadership.

**3. "Analysts are Sell-rated at $169 median target, stock is above target."** Analyst price targets on commodity producers have a documented history of lagging structural shifts. During the 2003-2011 copper supercycle, analysts consistently maintained targets well below the actual price of SCCO for years. The current median target of $98 implies analysts are pricing copper at $4.00-4.50/lb -- approximately 25-33% below the current spot of $5.90/lb -- in their models. If the structural deficit projections from S&P Global (10M mt gap by 2040), J.P. Morgan (-330kt in 2026), and ING (-600kt in 2026) are even directionally correct, these targets will be revised upward.

## Business Overview

Southern Copper Corporation is the world's largest publicly traded copper producer by reserves, with operations in Peru (Cuajone, Toquepala, and Tia Maria under construction) and Mexico (Buenavista, La Caridad). The company is 88.9% owned by Grupo Mexico S.A.B. de C.V. through Americas Mining Corporation, with 11.1% public float.

Key operational facts:
- **Reserves**: Largest publicly traded copper reserves globally, providing 50+ years of mine life at current production rates
- **Production (FY2025)**: 956,270 tonnes of copper
- **Cash cost**: $0.42/lb net of by-product credits (molybdenum, zinc, silver, sulfuric acid) -- industry lowest
- **Gross cash cost**: $2.17/lb before by-product credits
- **By-products**: Significant molybdenum, zinc, silver, and sulfuric acid production provides natural cost offset and revenue diversification
- **Growth pipeline**: $19.9B investment over the next decade, including Tia Maria ($1.8B, 24% complete), Los Chancas, and Michiquillay in Peru; $10.2B in Mexican projects under negotiation

The Grupo Mexico ownership structure is a governance consideration. The Larrea family controls SCCO through Grupo Mexico, and the dividend policy (64.7% payout ratio) is designed to upstream cash to the parent. The $3.0B share repurchase program has been inactive since 2016. While this limits shareholder-friendly capital allocation optionality, it provides dividend predictability and ensures the parent is incentivized to maximize SCCO's cash generation.

## Earnings Quality & Hidden Value

**GAAP vs. Non-GAAP Divergence**: Minimal. SCCO is a clean reporter. The company discloses adjusted EBITDA as its primary non-GAAP metric, but the adjustments are minor (typically less than 2% of the GAAP figure). There is no aggressive adjusted EPS framework that inflates reported earnings. This is a forensic positive -- what you see is what you get.

**Cash Flow Conversion**: Operating cash flow of $4.8B in FY2025 against net income of $4.3B represents strong cash conversion. The difference ($500M) is primarily non-cash depreciation and working capital movements. FCF of ~$3.5B after $1.3B in capex represents a 67% conversion of operating cash flow to free cash flow. As Tia Maria capex peaks in 2026-2027, FCF conversion will temporarily decline before recovering as new production comes online.

**Hidden Balance Sheet Strength**: The copper reserve base is carried at historical mining cost on the balance sheet. At current copper prices of $5.90/lb, the economic value of SCCO's reserves exceeds their book value by an order of magnitude. The company's 50+ year reserve life at current production rates is an asset that no balance sheet metric captures. Additionally, the by-product revenue stream (molybdenum, zinc, silver, sulfuric acid) effectively provides SCCO with a negative cash cost floor on copper production, meaning the company generates positive cash flow at virtually any copper price above $1.50/lb.

**Cost Curve Positioning**: At $0.42/lb net cash cost, SCCO sits in the first decile of the global copper cost curve. This provides structural protection during cyclical downturns -- in the 2020 COVID crash, when copper briefly touched $2.10/lb, SCCO remained profitable while higher-cost producers burned cash. This survivability premium is not reflected in a simple P/E comparison with higher-cost peers.

## Margin of Safety

The margin of safety at current prices ($185) is insufficient. At this level, the stock prices in much of the supercycle upside and leaves minimal protection against a cyclical downturn in copper (which could easily take the stock back to $120-140 based on 2020 and 2022 drawdown patterns).

The margin of safety becomes compelling at the $140-155 entry range:
- **Downside protection**: Even at trough copper prices ($4.00/lb), SCCO remains profitable with an estimated EPS of $3.00-3.50, implying a worst-case P/E of 40-50x at the entry range -- painful but not catastrophic for a business that will survive any price environment. More realistically, copper is unlikely to sustain below $5.00/lb given the structural deficit, which implies a floor EPS of $5.00+ and a floor P/E of 28-31x at entry.
- **Upside optionality**: At $148 entry, the target of $210 represents 42% upside. If copper reaches $7+/lb and production recovers, the upside extends to $225-250 (52-69%).
- **Margin of Safety calculation**: At $148 entry vs. a conservative fair value of $190 (25x normalized EPS of $7.60 using conservative copper assumptions), the margin of safety is 22%.

## Bear Case Rebuttal

**Bear: "SCCO trades at 20x EV/EBITDA vs. 8.4x for the large-cap copper miner peer group. The premium is irrational."**

Rebuttal: The premium is structural, not irrational. SCCO's EBITDA margin is 58.5% vs. the peer average of 30-35%. ROIC is 33% vs. the peer average of 10-15%. Net Debt/EBITDA is 0.3x vs. peer average of 1.5-2.0x. Reserve life is 50+ years vs. peer average of 15-25 years. The market is not paying 20x for average EBITDA -- it is paying 20x for the highest-quality EBITDA in the copper industry. The relevant comparison is to consumer staples companies with similar margin profiles and competitive moats. Procter & Gamble trades at 21x EV/EBITDA with lower margins. The question is not whether 20x is too high for a miner, but whether it is too high for a franchise with SCCO's quality characteristics. At the $140-155 entry range, this multiple compresses to 15-16x, which is defensible.

**Bear: "Grupo Mexico's 88.9% ownership means minority shareholders have no governance protection. Dividend policy upstreams cash to the parent."**

Rebuttal: This is a real risk but also a misunderstood feature. Grupo Mexico's incentives are fully aligned with maximizing SCCO's cash generation -- the parent derives the majority of its own cash flow from SCCO's dividends. The 64.7% payout ratio is high but stable, and the $3.97/share annual dividend is well-covered by FCF. The inactive share repurchase program is a negative, but in a rising-copper environment, retained earnings are being deployed into the $19.9B growth pipeline, which is value-accretive. The governance discount is already reflected in the stock's historical multiple range.

**Bear: "Insider selling of $3.1M with zero purchases signals a top."**

Rebuttal: The insider sales are small relative to the $153B market cap and involve directors, not operational management. Director Castillo Sanchez Mejorada sold $997K and was left with 600 shares -- these are not large holders making conviction statements. The CEO and CFO have not sold. Moreover, insider selling in mining companies after a 117% rally is routine profit-taking, not a bearish signal on the business itself. What would be more concerning is if management were issuing equity or if the company were accelerating debt issuance to fund operations -- neither is occurring.

**Bear: "Recession risk from the -92K NFP print could crush copper demand and take SCCO to $100."**

Rebuttal: A severe recession would hurt SCCO in the near term -- there is no denying cyclical risk. However, SCCO's cost structure provides survivability that higher-cost peers lack. In the 2020 COVID crash, SCCO's stock fell from $42 to $22 (a 48% drawdown) before recovering to $42 within 6 months and $80 within 18 months. The recovery was faster and more complete than peers precisely because of cost leadership. Additionally, the structural demand vectors driving the copper deficit (AI data centers, electrification, defense) are not GDP-sensitive. They are capex programs with 3-5 year budgets that will proceed regardless of a mild recession.

## Risk Factors

1. **Cyclical demand collapse**: A severe recession could take copper to $4.00-4.50/lb, compressing SCCO's earnings to $3.00-3.50 and driving the stock to $100-120. SCCO's cost structure provides survivability but not immunity from cyclical drawdowns. Position sizing must reflect this tail risk.

2. **Tia Maria execution risk**: The $1.8B project has been delayed for over a decade by community opposition and permitting challenges in Peru. While the mining license was approved in October 2025 and construction is 24% complete, renewed social unrest could delay the mid-2027 startup and undermine the production growth narrative.

3. **Grupo Mexico governance**: The 88.9% parent ownership limits minority shareholder influence on capital allocation, dividend policy, and strategic decisions. A parent-level financial stress event could lead to dividend increases that starve SCCO of growth capital, or to related-party transactions that do not benefit minority holders.

4. **Peru and Mexico political risk**: Both countries have histories of changing mining regulations, imposing windfall taxes, and restricting water access. Mexico's 2023 Mining Law changes reduced concession terms and imposed new community contributions. A future regulatory tightening in either jurisdiction could impair SCCO's operating economics.

5. **Copper price already elevated**: At $5.90/lb, copper is near all-time highs. While structural deficit supports further appreciation, a scenario where copper consolidates in a $5.00-5.50/lb range for 12-18 months would make the current valuation difficult to sustain.

6. **No superinvestor validation**: Zero positions from tracked value investors (Buffett, Klarman, Einhorn, etc.) according to Dataroma. This does not invalidate the thesis, but the absence of deep-value validation in a stock with a Sell consensus is notable.
