# Contrarian Long Thesis: Dominion Energy (D)

## Investment Summary

Dominion Energy is the worst-performing major utility stock of the past twelve months, lagging the S&P 500 Utilities sector by over 20 percentage points. The stock trades at approximately $60, a 17x forward P/E on 2026E operating earnings of $3.57/share, with a consensus "Hold" rating (3 Buy, 14 Hold, 2 Sell). The market has decided Dominion is a capex-burdened utility saddled with an $11.5 billion offshore wind project, $44 billion in debt, and negative free cash flow. Every element of this narrative is factually correct. And every element of it misses the point.

Dominion Energy Virginia sits atop the single most explosive electricity demand corridor in the world -- Northern Virginia's "Data Center Alley" -- with 47 GW of contracted data center capacity and 25 GW under active connection request through 2031. The company has responded with a $65 billion five-year capital plan targeting 11.9% Virginia rate base CAGR through 2030, with 74% of Virginia investments qualifying for rider recovery mechanisms that convert capex to earnings with minimal regulatory lag. The PJM capacity auction just exploded 6.7x to $14.7 billion, confirming that power scarcity pricing has arrived in Dominion's service territory. The market is pricing the burden of investment. It is not pricing the earnings power that investment creates in a regulated framework where rate base growth equals earnings growth.

## Why the Market Is Wrong

The consensus bear case rests on four pillars, each of which is either temporary, misunderstood, or already inflecting:

1. **"Negative free cash flow means the company is destroying value."** Dominion's FCF was -$7.2B in 2024 because it is spending $65 billion over five years to build rate base in the highest-demand electricity market in the country. For a regulated utility, negative FCF during a capex supercycle is not a sign of distress -- it is the mechanism by which future earnings are created. Every dollar of rate base earns the authorized return. The market treats Dominion's capex as a cost; it is actually an asset purchase with a government-guaranteed return.

2. **"The offshore wind project is a money pit."** CVOW has ballooned from $9.8B to $11.5B and slipped to early 2027 completion. This is real. But CVOW is 71% complete with all 176 monopiles and 9 export cables installed. The first turbine is installed and initial power delivery is imminent. The Virginia SCC approved cost recovery through rider mechanisms, meaning cost overruns are largely recoverable from ratepayers. CVOW is a headache, not a catastrophe, and the market has fully priced it as the latter.

3. **"Debt is too high at $44 billion."** Dominion's debt level is elevated but appropriate for a utility executing the largest capital plan in its history. Net debt to EBITDA of approximately 6.5x is high but within the range regulators tolerate for utilities with visible rate base growth. The $65B capital plan is 74% rider-eligible in Virginia, meaning cash recovery begins almost immediately after assets enter service. The capital structure will improve as rate base grows into the debt.

4. **"ROIC is below cost of capital."** Current ROIC of ~3.2% is below the ~6.1% WACC because Dominion is mid-cycle in a massive buildout. Assets under construction earn zero return until they enter rate base. As the $65B capex program delivers assets into service over 2026-2030, ROIC will converge toward the authorized ROE of 9.35-9.7% in Virginia. Judging a utility's ROIC during the investment phase is like judging a farmer's harvest before the seeds sprout.

## Business Overview

Dominion Energy is a regulated electric and gas utility serving approximately 3.7 million customers across Virginia, South Carolina, and several other states. The Virginia segment is the dominant earnings driver, generating $536 million in operating earnings in Q4 2025 alone, powered by customer usage growth in Loudoun County (the epicenter of US data center construction). The company operates ~30 GW of generation capacity across nuclear, gas, solar, wind, and other sources. Dominion's Coastal Virginia Offshore Wind (2.6 GW) is the largest offshore wind project in the US, nearing completion.

The strategic position is unique: Dominion Energy Virginia is the sole electric provider to the largest concentration of data centers on Earth. Northern Virginia hosts approximately 70% of the world's internet traffic. The 47 GW of contracted data center capacity represents decades of demand growth that no competitor can serve -- regulated utility franchises are territorial monopolies by design.

## Earnings Quality & Hidden Value

The GAAP vs. non-GAAP divergence at Dominion is remarkably narrow -- a rarity among utilities executing complex capital programs. In FY2025, GAAP EPS of $3.45 actually exceeded operating EPS of $3.42, meaning Dominion's non-GAAP adjustments (nuclear decommissioning trust gains, hedging marks, discontinued operations) slightly understated true earnings. This is the opposite of the typical corporate pattern where non-GAAP flatters the picture. Dominion's reported earnings quality is high.

**Hidden value the market ignores:**

- **47 GW of contracted data center capacity** is an asset that exists nowhere else in the US utility sector at this scale. At even modest connection fees and ongoing load growth, this contracted pipeline represents tens of billions in future rate base.
- **Rider recovery mechanisms cover 74% of Virginia capex.** This is not ordinary utility capex that sits in regulatory limbo -- the vast majority flows into earnings with minimal lag through legislatively approved riders. Cash conversion on this capex is structurally superior to peer utilities.
- **The GS-5 rate class** (approved November 2025) requires data center developers to directly fund infrastructure they require. This shifts construction risk and cost to the customer while Dominion earns the regulated return on the asset. It is a structurally advantageous cost-sharing mechanism.
- **PJM capacity pricing validates scarcity.** The 6.7x capacity auction increase to $14.7B means Dominion's generation assets in the PJM footprint are earning dramatically higher capacity payments. This flows directly to revenue with minimal incremental cost.
- **Dividend yield of ~4.2%** provides a floor. Even if the capital appreciation thesis takes time to play out, investors are paid to wait at a rate well above the 10-year Treasury.

## Margin of Safety

At approximately $60 per share, the downside is cushioned by:

1. **Regulated utility floor.** Dominion's authorized ROE of 9.35-9.7% in Virginia provides a floor on earnings power. Regulators do not permit utilities to earn below their cost of capital indefinitely -- rate relief is the backstop.
2. **Dividend support.** The ~4.2% yield at current prices, backed by growing operating earnings ($3.42 in FY2025, guided to $3.45-$3.69 in FY2026), provides downside support. Utility investors demand income; the dividend anchors the stock.
3. **Rate base growth is contractual.** Unlike speculative growth companies, Dominion's 11.9% Virginia rate base CAGR is driven by signed interconnection agreements, regulatory approvals, and legislatively mandated riders. The growth is as close to "locked in" as public equity gets.
4. **Asset replacement value.** Dominion's regulated utility infrastructure -- generation, transmission, distribution across Virginia and South Carolina -- would cost far more than the current enterprise value to replicate from scratch. No new entrant can serve this territory.

Margin of safety is estimated at 25-35% based on a conservative rate base growth model: $3.57 midpoint 2026E operating EPS growing at 8-10% annually through 2030 implies $4.80-$5.25 EPS by 2030, supporting a $77-$89 stock price on an 16-17x P/E -- a 28-48% return before dividends.

## Bear Case Rebuttal

**Bear: "Negative free cash flow for years means the company is destroying shareholder value."**
Rebuttal: This fundamentally misunderstands regulated utility economics. Capex invested in rate base earns the authorized return (9.35-9.7% ROE in Virginia). Negative FCF during a build cycle is the mechanism of value creation, not destruction. When the capex cycle matures, FCF will normalize. Every major utility capex cycle in history has followed this pattern.

**Bear: "CVOW is a $11.5 billion albatross that will never earn its cost of capital."**
Rebuttal: CVOW is 71% complete, structurally installed, and nearing first power delivery. The Virginia SCC approved rider recovery, making the vast majority of costs recoverable. The project adds 2.6 GW of generation in a service territory that is power-starved. Even at $11.5B, the levelized cost is competitive on a 30-year asset life in a market where PJM capacity prices just increased 6.7x.

**Bear: "Data center demand could decelerate if AI capex slows."**
Rebuttal: Dominion has 47 GW under contract and 25 GW with active connection dates through 2031. These are signed agreements with financial commitments, not speculative projections. Even if new contract signings slow materially, the existing pipeline provides a decade of demand growth. Moreover, once data centers are built and operational, they consume power continuously -- this is not cyclical demand.

**Bear: "Rising rates hurt utility valuations."**
Rebuttal: Rate sensitivity is real but diminishing for Dominion specifically. The data center demand growth story provides a secular earnings growth engine that reduces the stock's correlation to bond yields. Utilities with visible 8-12% EPS growth are valued differently than utilities growing at 3-5%. As the market recognizes Dominion's growth profile, the rate sensitivity premium should decline.

**Bear: "The 92% dividend payout ratio is unsustainable."**
Rebuttal: The payout ratio is calculated on current GAAP earnings. As operating EPS grows from $3.42 (FY2025) toward $4.30+ (FY2028E), the payout ratio naturally declines even with modest dividend increases. Management has guided to 4-6% annual dividend growth, well within the earnings growth trajectory.
