# Growth Long Thesis: Scorpio Tankers (STNG)

## Investment Summary

Scorpio Tankers is a pure-play product tanker owner experiencing a textbook revenue acceleration inflection driven by the Hormuz crisis rerouting global petroleum product flows. Revenue has accelerated for five consecutive quarters ($204M to $253M), operating margins have expanded from 28% to 53%, and the company sits on $309M net cash after eliminating $3.1B of debt since 2021. With product tanker rates surging to multi-year highs on Cape of Good Hope rerouting, STNG's 90-vessel fleet is generating free cash flow at 20x+ breakeven rates. The stock trades at 10.9x trailing P/E and approximately 3x EV/EBITDA -- a fraction of the earnings power embedded in current spot rates. This is not a peak-cycle valuation; it is a structural supply displacement event that is being priced as temporary.

## Growth Inflection Case

The growth inflection for Scorpio Tankers is driven by three converging forces:

1. **Hormuz closure creates structural product tanker demand.** The Strait of Hormuz carried approximately 20% of global oil and significant refined product volumes. Since March 3, 2026, no tanker traffic has transited the Strait. Rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope adds 10-14 days per voyage, absorbing vessel supply globally. LR2 rates on Mideast Gulf to Japan routes surged 117% in one week to WS435 ($98.53/ton). This is not a speculative rate spike -- it is a physical rerouting of global trade that mechanically tightens vessel supply.

2. **Sequential revenue acceleration for five quarters.** STNG's quarterly revenue progression tells the story: Q4 2024 $204M, Q1 2025 $214M, Q2 2025 $230M, Q3 2025 $241M, Q4 2025 $253M. This is a +24% sequential acceleration from trough to peak, and the Hormuz crisis has not yet flowed through to reported numbers. Q1 2026 will capture the full rate spike.

3. **Operating leverage is inflecting sharply.** Operating margins expanded from 28.2% (Q1 2025) to 52.7% (Q4 2025) as revenue scaled against a largely fixed cost base. The company's fleet-wide cash breakeven is approximately $12,500/day. With LR2s earning $35,000+/day and MRs at $28,000+/day pre-Hormuz, every incremental dollar flows to the bottom line. Post-Hormuz rates of $40,000-$50,000+/day imply operating margins above 60%.

The key insight is that STNG's product tanker fleet benefits from the Hormuz rerouting differently than crude tanker fleets. Product tankers carry refined petroleum products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel) that must reach end consumers regardless of geopolitical conditions. Demand for refined products is inelastic in the short term. When the shipping route lengthens, vessel-days consumed per unit of product delivered increases, creating a structural supply squeeze.

## Business Overview & TAM

Scorpio Tankers operates a fleet of 90 product tankers: 37 LR2, 42 MR, and 14 Handymax vessels with an average age of 10.1 years. The company is the largest publicly listed pure-play product tanker owner globally.

**Total Addressable Market:** The global product tanker market moves approximately 30 million barrels per day of refined petroleum products. Ton-mile demand is the critical variable -- longer routes increase ton-miles per barrel, expanding the effective TAM without any change in underlying product demand. The Cape of Good Hope rerouting adds approximately 3,000-4,000 nautical miles per voyage from the Middle East to Asia, increasing ton-mile demand by 25-35% on affected routes. With approximately 30% of global product tanker trade touching Middle East loading zones, the aggregate ton-mile demand increase is estimated at 8-12% globally.

**Fleet age dynamics:** The global product tanker fleet is aging, with limited newbuild deliveries expected through 2027 due to shipyard capacity constraints (yards are full with container ship and LNG carrier orders). STNG's fleet at 10.1 years average age is younger than the industry average, providing operational flexibility and lower maintenance costs.

**Market share:** STNG's 90-vessel fleet represents approximately 3-4% of the global product tanker fleet by capacity, making it the dominant public market proxy for product tanker exposure.

## Business Drivers

1. **Hormuz rerouting (Urgent, ongoing).** The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since March 3, 2026. No tanker traffic transited on that date. Marine war-risk insurance has been cancelled by 7 of 12 P&I clubs. Product tankers loaded in the Gulf must now exit via alternative routes or wait. LR2 rates to Japan surged to WS435 ($98.53/ton), a 117% increase in one week. STNG has locked in two LR2 time charters: STI Lombard at $33,000/day for 5 years and STI Rambla at $30,500/day for 8 years -- demonstrating management's expectation of sustained elevated rates.

2. **Vessel supply constraint.** Cape of Good Hope rerouting absorbs vessel-days, reducing effective fleet supply. Global product tanker orderbook is thin due to shipyard capacity constraints. The supply response to elevated rates will take 2-3 years to materialize (newbuild delivery timelines). This creates a multi-year window of elevated earnings.

3. **Balance sheet transformation.** STNG reduced net debt from $3.1B (2021) to $309M net cash. Cash of $937M with $767M undrawn revolver capacity provides firepower for opportunistic fleet expansion, dividends, or buybacks. The company raised its quarterly dividend to $0.45/share (12.5% YoY increase), signaling confidence in sustained cash generation.

4. **Fleet monetization strategy.** The March 5, 2026, announcement of selling three older vessels ($35M each for MRs, $60M for LR2) while chartering out two LR2s at $30,500-$33,000/day for 5-8 years is a sophisticated capital allocation move -- locking in long-duration revenue while recycling capital from older tonnage at elevated asset values.

## Operating Leverage & Margin Path

The operating leverage in STNG's business model is extraordinary at current rate levels. The fleet-wide cash breakeven is approximately $12,500/day. At Q4 2025 average TCE of $28,066/day, the margin above breakeven is $15,566/day per vessel. At current post-Hormuz spot rates of $40,000-$50,000+/day for LR2s, the margin above breakeven is $27,500-$37,500+/day -- approximately double the Q4 level.

With 90 vessels, each incremental $1,000/day in TCE above breakeven generates approximately $32.9M in annualized incremental revenue. The fixed cost structure means virtually all incremental revenue flows through to EBITDA and FCF.

Management's decision to lock in 5-year and 8-year charters at $30,500-$33,000/day confirms they believe rates will sustain above $30,000/day for an extended period. These locked-in charters provide a margin floor of $17,500-$20,500/day above breakeven per vessel on those specific ships.

Projected margin path: If fleet-wide average TCE reaches $35,000-$40,000/day in Q1 2026 (conservative given spot rates), operating margins should reach 55-65%, and annualized EBITDA would approach $800M-$1B.

## Competitive Position & Moat

STNG's competitive moat rests on three pillars:

1. **Scale and fleet diversity.** As the largest publicly traded pure-play product tanker owner, STNG offers customers fleet-wide coverage across LR2, MR, and Handymax segments. This matters for chartering relationships with major oil trading houses that need reliable tonnage across multiple vessel classes.

2. **Balance sheet strength.** The net cash position of $309M with $1.7B total liquidity (cash + revolver) positions STNG to acquire vessels at distressed prices if opportunities arise, or to weather a rate downturn without financial stress. Many smaller tanker operators carry significant leverage.

3. **Modern fleet.** Average age of 10.1 years positions the fleet in the sweet spot -- young enough for fuel efficiency and regulatory compliance (IMO 2023 carbon intensity rules), old enough that depreciation has lowered the capital base.

**Competitive threats:** TORM (TRMD) is the closest public market peer, with 2025 TCE of $910M and 2026 guidance of $850M-$1.25B. TORM trades at a comparable valuation but with a different geographic mix. DHT Holdings and Frontline (FRO, in portfolio at 4%) are crude tanker peers, not direct product tanker competitors.

## Risk Factors

1. **Ceasefire/diplomatic resolution.** A diplomatic breakthrough reopening the Strait of Hormuz would collapse product tanker rates from $40,000-$50,000+/day to $20,000-$25,000/day within days. This is the primary binary risk. STNG's locked-in charters at $30,500-$33,000/day provide partial protection, but the majority of the fleet remains on spot or short-term contracts.

2. **Recession reduces refined product demand.** The NFP -92K print signals potential economic weakness. A recession would reduce demand for gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel, partially offsetting the ton-mile benefit from rerouting. However, the short-term supply displacement effect dominates the demand impact.

3. **Commodity-like business with no pricing power.** Tanker rates are set by the spot market. STNG has no ability to set prices -- it is a pure price-taker in the freight market. Rates can move 50%+ in either direction within weeks.

4. **Escalation risk -- vessel attacks.** If Iran's IRGC begins targeting product tankers, war-risk insurance costs could spike further, partially offsetting rate gains. A direct attack on an STNG vessel would create both financial and reputational damage.

5. **Fleet aging.** At 10.1 years average age, capital expenditure requirements for fleet renewal will increase over the next 3-5 years. Management's current sale of older vessels partially addresses this.
