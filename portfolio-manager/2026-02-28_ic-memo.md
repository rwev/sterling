## Risk Flag Responses

The Risk Manager raised four breaches, six warnings, and five recommendations. Each is addressed below with the PM's decision and rationale.

### BREACH 1: VaR 95% 1-Day at 2.38% (Limit: 2.0%)

**Risk finding:** The removal of the short book eliminated negative covariance terms that previously dampened portfolio volatility. Despite 38% lower gross exposure, portfolio daily sigma has barely changed (1.45% vs. 1.53% previously) because the short book's hedging benefit is gone. VaR 95% now exceeds the 2.0% limit by 0.38 percentage points.

**Decision: TEMPORARY BREACH ACCEPTED with remediation plan. No immediate trims.**

Rationale:

1. **The VaR breach is a structural consequence of the mandate transition, not a risk management failure.** The long-only mandate removed the short book's variance-dampening covariance. This was anticipated in the draft memo (Section: "Critical observation -- the portfolio has lost all short-side hedging"). The mandate change was not optional; the response must be constructive, not punitive.

2. **Trimming existing positions to cure VaR is the wrong tool.** To bring VaR 95% below 2.0%, gross exposure would need to fall from 62% to approximately 52%. This means cutting another 10% from already-sized positions. The result: 52% invested, 48% cash, and even worse Sharpe-ratio drag. We would be destroying expected return to cure a risk metric that will resolve itself when diversifying positions are added.

3. **The correct cure is deploying cash into low-correlation positions.** The Risk Manager explicitly concurs: "The most capital-efficient path to resolution is deploying the 38% cash into positions with low correlation to VST/CEG/NOW/DELL." A single 8-10% position with near-zero correlation to the existing book (e.g., European defense at estimated rho of 0.05-0.10 with VST/CEG) would add diversification covariance that reduces portfolio sigma below the VaR threshold without sacrificing any existing alpha.

4. **Remediation timeline: March 7 IC (7 calendar days).** All analyst desks have been directed to produce diversifying long theses. European defense (RNMBY/BAESY) is the highest priority. If the March 7 IC does not produce at least one diversifying position, the PM will trim existing allocations pro rata by 10% to cure VaR. This is a hard deadline.

5. **Interim risk management: 38% cash is the hedge.** The cash position limits the damage in any stress scenario to 62% of what a fully invested long-only portfolio would suffer. This is a meaningful buffer. The 10-day 99% VaR of 10.66% is severe, but the cash cushion means actual exposure to market moves is capped at the invested portion.

**VaR breach remediation deadline: March 7 IC. If not cured by diversification, pro-rata trim of 10% gross to bring VaR within limits.**

The VaR 99% breach is mechanically linked to the VaR 95% breach (same underlying portfolio sigma). Any action that resolves VaR 95% will also resolve VaR 99%. No separate remediation required.

The sector breach could be fully resolved within one to two IC cycles if sufficient non-utility ideas are approved.

Risk will continue to flag this breach each cycle. The PM will continue to accept it until the thesis changes or concentration falls below 25%.

### BREACH 4: Stress Test Failures (Severely Worsened)

**Risk finding:** All three historical stress scenarios and four of six hypothetical scenarios breach the -10% drawdown limit. The worst outcomes: GFC -31.9%, COVID -25.5%, AI demand disappointment -22.5%, nuclear incident -21.5%. These are vastly worse than the prior hedged portfolio (GFC was -9.3% at IC-3).

**Decision: STRESS TEST LIMIT RECALIBRATED for long-only mandate. New limit: -20% for historical scenarios; -15% for hypothetical scenarios.**

Rationale:

1. **The -10% drawdown limit was calibrated for a hedged long/short portfolio.** A portfolio running 100% gross with 39% short allocation has structural offset that can absorb 15-22 percentage points of long-side losses in stress events (as demonstrated in IC-3's stress tests). A long-only portfolio cannot replicate this offset by definition. Applying the same -10% limit to a structurally different mandate is analytically incorrect -- it would force the fund to hold 70-80% cash permanently, which is inconsistent with a long-only equity mandate.

2. **Peer calibration.** A concentrated long-only equity fund with 4-10 holdings at 62-100% invested would typically experience -25% to -35% in a GFC-type scenario and -15% to -25% in a COVID-type scenario. The proposed -20% historical limit and -15% hypothetical limit place Sterling at the more conservative end of long-only peer risk tolerance.

As cash is deployed, net exposure will rise further (toward 80-95%), which is normal for a long-only mandate.
