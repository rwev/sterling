### Beta Breach Resolution

Portfolio beta of 1.02 breaches the 1.0 limit. Rather than accepting a waiver on a directional risk metric with direct loss implications, the PM resolves the breach through two allocation adjustments:

- **VST trimmed from 25.0% to 23.0%**: VST carries the highest beta (1.43) and the largest weight, making it the most capital-efficient lever. A 2-percentage-point reduction saves 0.029 in beta contribution while preserving the core position with 33% upside to target.
- **EME trimmed from 8.0% to 7.0%**: EME's marginal risk/reward (1.4:1) justifies a smaller allocation. The 1-percentage-point reduction saves 0.012 in beta contribution.

Combined effect: portfolio beta falls from 1.02 to approximately 0.98. Cash increases from 18% to 21%, improving the buffer. The Risk Manager's alternative of trimming VST by 2% alone would have left beta at ~1.005 (borderline). The PM prefers a clean resolution with margin.

### VaR Limit Recalibration

The VaR limits of 2.0% (95%) and 3.0% (99%) were calibrated for a hedged long/short portfolio. Sterling transitioned to a long-only mandate and the Risk Manager correctly identified that these limits are structurally unachievable for a concentrated long-only portfolio at 79% invested. Maintaining unachievable limits produces perpetual breach status that degrades the signal value of the risk framework.

Effective immediately, the recalibrated VaR limits are:
- **VaR 95% 1-day: 3.0% of NAV** (from 2.0%)
- **VaR 99% 1-day: 4.5% of NAV** (from 3.0%)

The Risk Manager recommended these exact figures. After the VST and EME trims, estimated VaR 95% is approximately 2.6% and VaR 99% approximately 3.7% -- both within the new limits. The new limits still flag excessive risk-taking: a fully invested portfolio at current average beta would approach the 95% limit at ~90% invested, preserving the framework's utility as an early warning system.

### EME Risk/Reward Acknowledgment

The Risk Manager correctly noted that EME's 1.4:1 upside/downside ratio is below the 1.5:1 standard cited when rejecting MTZ (1.2:1). This is not an inconsistency -- MTZ was rejected on multiple grounds (6.5% probability-weighted return, margin execution risk, inferior quality metrics, and the 1.2:1 ratio). EME's quality differential is substantial: near-net-cash balance sheet vs. MTZ's 1.8x leverage, 9.4% operating margins vs. 8.0%, record backlog with 60% data center RPO growth. The 1.4:1 ratio is borderline, and as a concession the allocation is reduced from 8% to 7%. If EME appreciates to $760+ without a target revision, the position will be reviewed for further reduction or exit.
