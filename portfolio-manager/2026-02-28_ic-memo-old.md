## Pitch Reviews

### VST (Vistra Corp) -- Long

**Presented by:** Long Analyst
**Decision: Approved**
**Allocation: 35% of NAV**

**Analyst Summary:** Vistra is the largest competitive power generator in the U.S. with 41 GW of capacity across nuclear, gas, coal, and solar. FY2025 delivered record adjusted EBITDA of $5.9B and adjusted FCF of $3.6B. Management guides FY2026 EBITDA to $6.8-7.6B (+22% YoY). Long-term PPAs with AWS (1,200 MW, 20-year) and Meta (2,176 MW, 20-year) provide contracted revenue visibility to the mid-2040s. At $171/share, the stock trades at ~14x forward P/E and ~8.5x FY2026E EV/EBITDA. Analyst targets $230 (12-month), with a $140 stop.

**Rationale:**

This is the highest-conviction position in the book, and it earns the largest allocation. Three factors justify the outsized weight:

1. **The transformation from merchant power to contracted infrastructure is genuine and mispriced.** 3.8 GW of nuclear capacity under 20-year PPAs is signed, binding revenue extending to the mid-2040s. The market still prices VST as a volatile merchant power company. At 14x forward P/E and 8.5x FY2026E EV/EBITDA, Vistra trades at a material discount to its growth rate and revenue visibility. Consensus PT of $234 implies 36% upside. This is the kind of valuation disconnect that justifies concentration.

2. **FCF generation is exceptional.** $3.6B in FY2025 adjusted FCF on a ~$35B market cap yields a 10%+ FCF yield. Management targets $12.60/share in adjusted FCF before growth in 2026 -- 30% growth. The company is simultaneously buying back stock and deleveraging toward 2.3x net debt/EBITDA. This is a capital return story layered on top of a growth story.

3. **The demand catalyst is structural, not speculative.** U.S. electricity consumption hit an all-time peak in 2025. Data center interconnection queues are signed and growing. AI training and inference workloads require baseload power that only nuclear and gas can reliably provide. This is infrastructure demand with multi-year, multi-decade visibility confirmed by binding contracts.

**Risk management at 35%:** The primary risk is leverage ($17B net debt, 2.9x EBITDA). An unplanned nuclear outage at Comanche Peak would pressure EBITDA and push leverage to 3.5x. The $140 stop (18% downside) limits maximum loss to approximately 6.3% of NAV. The contracted PPA revenue provides a floor that merchant generators lack, which supports the larger sizing.

**Entry range:** $160-$175 (current $171 is within range; do not chase above $180)
**12-month target:** $230
**Stop loss:** $140

### CEG (Constellation Energy) -- Long

**Presented by:** Long Analyst
**Decision: Approved**
**Allocation: 30% of NAV**

**Analyst Summary:** Constellation is the largest electricity producer in the U.S. following the $16.4B Calpine acquisition (closed January 2026). The combined entity operates ~21 GW nuclear, ~26 GW gas, and 725 MW geothermal. The deal is 20%+ accretive to FY2026 EPS. The stock has pulled back 23% YTD to $288. Analyst consensus is Strong Buy with a median PT of $406. Analyst targets $350 base case with $400+ upside on successful integration.

**Rationale:**

CEG is approved as the second-largest position. It earns a 30% allocation -- significant, but below VST for three specific reasons I will address after stating the bull case.

The bull case is compelling:

1. **Nuclear scarcity is real and irreplaceable.** Constellation owns the only 21 GW nuclear fleet in America. New nuclear capacity takes 10+ years to permit and build. Hyperscalers need 24/7, zero-carbon baseload power now. This is a monopoly-like position in a market with structurally growing demand.

2. **The Calpine deal is transformational and well-priced.** Acquired at 7.9x EV/EBITDA, the deal is immediately 20%+ accretive to EPS and diversifies the generation mix with efficient gas and geothermal assets. The combined entity produces 175 TWh annually -- the largest power producer in the country.

3. **The 23% YTD pullback is macro-driven, not fundamental.** Broad rotation out of U.S. equities, sector-wide multiple compression, and tariff uncertainty have brought the stock to $288. This is a dislocation from intrinsic value, not a reflection of deteriorating fundamentals.

Why 30% and not 35% (the three concerns):

1. **Integration risk.** The Calpine deal closed less than two months ago. Pro forma leverage is 3.0-3.5x EBITDA, materially above pre-deal levels. Execution risk on a $16.4B acquisition is real and unresolved until we see the March 31 Business Outlook call.

2. **Higher valuation, thinner margin of safety.** At 25-27x forward P/E, CEG trades at nearly double VST's multiple. The premium is justified by growth and asset scarcity, but it means any disappointment compresses the multiple faster and harder.

3. **Correlation with VST.** Both names are long power infrastructure / AI electricity demand. Running 65% of the book in two correlated longs is a deliberate concentration bet on the power infrastructure thesis. I am comfortable with this because the demand signal is structural, the contracts are signed, and both names are at the low end of analyst ranges. But if the AI capex narrative cracks, both positions will move against us simultaneously.

**Entry range:** $270-$295 (current $288 is acceptable)

### NKE (Nike) -- Short

**Presented by:** Short Analyst
**Decision: Approved**
**Allocation: 20% of NAV**

**Analyst Summary:** Nike faces a triple headwind: ~$1.5B in annualized tariff costs (320bps gross margin hit), collapsing China business (-17% YoY, sixth consecutive quarterly decline), and accelerating market share losses to On Running and Hoka. Net income fell 32% in Q2 FY2026 despite revenue +1%. The stock trades at 33x forward earnings for a company guiding revenue down low single digits with gross margin declining 175-225bps before tariff impact. Analyst targets a $50-$55 cover range from the current $63.91.

**Rationale:**

This is a clean short thesis with multiple independent catalysts, and it earns a 20% allocation as the primary short position. Four reasons:

1. **The tariff math is devastating and structural.** Nike manufactures virtually all product overseas. The 10% global tariff adds $1.5B in costs with no near-term mitigation path. Reshoring is not feasible on any relevant timeline. The company must either absorb (margin destruction) or pass through (volume destruction). The administration is threatening 15%, which would compound the damage. This is not a one-quarter headwind -- it is a structural cost increase with no exit.

2. **China is structurally impaired.** Six consecutive quarters of decline, with footwear down 21% and online down 36%. Anta and Li-Ning are gaining share from a position of cultural relevance and local distribution advantage. Nike's brand relevance with Chinese consumers is eroding. Management guided Q3 to be "similar to Q2" -- there is no inflection in sight.

3. **Market share losses in running are accelerating.** On Running and Hoka have captured the performance running category. This is a product innovation gap, not a fashion cycle. Nike's pivot back to performance will take 2-3 years and will be margin-dilutive during the transition.

4. **The valuation is disconnected from fundamentals.** 33x forward earnings for a company with declining net income (-32%), negative revenue guidance, and a 320bps structural margin headwind. Fair value at 25-28x implies $50-$62. The stock has room to fall even on these already-lowered expectations.

The near-term catalyst is the March 31 Q3 FY2026 earnings report. Management has set a low bar (revenue down low single digits, gross margin down 175-225bps ex-tariff), but any miss on these reduced expectations would accelerate the decline. Tariff escalation to 15% would be an additional catalyst.

**Key risk:** Tariff rollback or exemption would remove the $1.5B cost headwind overnight and could trigger a sharp short squeeze. Short interest at 3.0% of float is not excessively crowded, which limits squeeze risk. I size at 20% rather than 25% to manage the tariff policy tail risk.

**Entry range:** $63-$68 (current $63.91 is in range)
**Cover target:** $50-$55
**Stop loss:** $75

### MSFT (Microsoft) -- Short

**Presented by:** Short Analyst
**Decision: Approved**
**Allocation: 15% of NAV**

**Analyst Summary:** Microsoft is spending aggressively into AI infrastructure ($100B+ annualized capex) while Azure growth decelerates (39% to guided 37-38%). Non-AI Azure is growing only 13-17%. Copilot monetization (~$5.4B annualized) is a fraction of the capex spend. More Personal Computing segment is shrinking (-3% YoY). The stock trades at 24x forward P/E and 39x EV/FCF. Analyst targets a $320-$340 cover range from $390-$410 entry.

**Rationale:**

I am reversing my initial inclination to reject this pitch. On reflection, with a mandate to be fully deployed, MSFT earns a slot in the book as a 15% short position. Here is my updated reasoning:

1. **The capex-to-revenue conversion story is genuinely deteriorating.** Microsoft is spending 45 cents for every dollar of revenue on capex, up from 25 cents two years ago. FCF is being structurally compressed -- capex now consumes 52% of operating cash flow, up from 35%. This is not temporary investment; it is a structural shift in the business model from asset-light software to capital-heavy infrastructure. The market has not fully repriced for this transition.

2. **Non-AI Azure at 13-17% growth reframes the business.** Strip out the AI contribution and the core cloud franchise is growing at mid-teens -- respectable, but not a growth rate that supports 24x forward P/E. The market is paying a growth multiple for a business where the high-growth component (AI) requires disproportionate capital investment with uncertain returns.

3. **The rotation out of mega-cap tech is structural, not tactical.** MSCI World ex-US +8% YTD versus flat S&P 500. The Magnificent Seven as a group are underperforming the equal-weight S&P 500. Capital is moving from intangible to tangible economy. MSFT is on the wrong side of this rotation, and a weakening dollar (DXY down 9% over 12 months) accelerates it.

4. **The macro setup supports the short.** Our Macro Research has U.S. Mega-Cap Tech as Underweight. The sector rotation, capex fatigue narrative, and late-cycle deceleration all provide structural headwinds for MSFT.

**Why the smaller allocation (15%):**

This is a valuation short on the highest-quality balance sheet in the world. Microsoft has net cash, investment-grade credit, and $5.3B/quarter in buybacks providing a floor. The company could positively surprise on Copilot adoption or Azure AI monetization at any point. The thesis is about multiple compression, not business impairment. I size it as the smallest position in the book to reflect the asymmetric risk of shorting a fortress balance sheet.

**Catalyst:** Q3 FY2026 earnings in late April. Azure growth guided to 37-38%; any further deceleration below 37% triggers another leg down. Capex guidance for H2 is the second key data point. Between now and earnings, the position benefits from the structural tech rotation and any tariff escalation that pressures risk assets.

**Entry range:** $390-$410 (current ~$392 is at the low end; acceptable for initial entry)
**Cover target:** $320-$340
**Stop loss:** $440

### Hedging Considerations

1. **VST/CEG correlation risk.** 65% of the book in two power infrastructure longs is a concentrated bet. If the AI capex narrative cracks (hyperscalers revise down infrastructure spending), both positions will move against us. The MSFT short provides a partial natural hedge: if AI capex disappoints, MSFT should also decline, partially offsetting losses on VST/CEG. This is an imperfect hedge but directionally correct.

2. **Tariff tail risk.** Tariff escalation to 15%+ hurts NKE (good for the short) but could also pressure the broader market and the long book. Tariff rollback helps NKE (bad for the short) but would likely be market-positive for the longs. The directionality is partially offsetting.

3. **Interest rate risk.** Both longs are sensitive to higher rates (leverage, present value of long-duration PPAs). If inflation re-accelerates and the Fed delays cuts, the long book faces a headwind. The short book would likely benefit (higher rates pressure growth multiples on MSFT and consumer spending on NKE). This provides some natural hedging.
